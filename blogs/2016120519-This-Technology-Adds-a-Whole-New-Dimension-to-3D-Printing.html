This Technology Adds a Whole New Dimension to 3D Printing

Renae Lesser

December 5, 2016


Harvard researchers have developed a new 3D printing method for creating dynamic objects that move and change shape when immersed in water. The structures are designed using mathematical models and printed with a hydrogel ink that swells when wet. Using sophisticated algorithms, scientists can now carefully engineer printed structures that will move on their own in precise ways. This technique is being called 4D printing because it introduces the element of time.

The inspiration for this new technique? Researchers studied how flowers and leaves respond to environmental stimuli and worked to mimic those organic reactions synthetically. As you can see from this vialogue, the results are beautiful and the potential applications for this technology are wide-ranging. Because these objects are soft, pliant, and move in organic ways, 4D printing opens the door for biomedical breakthroughs, soft electronics, smart textiles and even a new field of 4D printed art.
