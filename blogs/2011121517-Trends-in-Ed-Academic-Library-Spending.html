Trends in Ed: Academic Library Spending

LauraCostello

December 15, 2011

The US Department of Education just released the figures for academic library spending in 2010. Most of the statistics were rather unsurprising: libraries are spending much more on electronic content at the cost of print monographs and serials. In-person reference interactions have been declining though most libraries in 2010 reported subscription or in-house chat reference. Though libraries reduced staff on net, they still spent nearly half of total annual expenditures on staff costs ($3.4 billion). Staff engagement in digitization projects increased during 2010 but numbers remained static for teaching and information literacy programs in the library. It's clear from the survey that 2010 academic libraries prioritize providing content and access services to their patrons. The survey did not address the avenues of content creation that many libraries are now providing beyond digitization services, but the lack of emphasis on teaching and information literacy services seems like a disturbing trend. It could be that there's dwindling demand for these services in an increasingly friendly and information-rich internet space, but information literacy skills remain important especially early in higher education and there's currently no institution better primed to provide these services to students.
