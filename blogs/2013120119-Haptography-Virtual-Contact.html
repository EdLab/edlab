Haptography: Virtual Contact

Fred Rossoff

December 1, 2013


Title: Haptography: Capturing and Recreating the Rich Feel of Real Surfaces

Authors: Katherine Kuchenbucker, Joseph Romano, & William McMahan

Source: The International Symposium on Robotics Research

Study Design: Although the potential learning applications of audio and video lessons are far from tapped out, the question inevitably arises as to what else in the human experience can be digitized, reproduced, and transmitted en masse, to students or consumers. Haptic technology, which reproduces the sensation of touch (through a hand-held tool) in a virtual environment, is one promising avenue.

Drawing upon a wide body of research, the authors of this paper determined that "a sensorized hand-held tool and a sophisticated signal processing algorithm can be used to create very accurate models of the tool-mediated feel of any real surface." They collected data from the vibrations produced by dragging a test stylus across various surfaces, developed a model that could predict (and therefore reproduce) those vibrations with reasonable frequency, and then, using a prototype of coils, springs, and magnets embedded in a stylus, tested their ability to recreate the feel of actually using the stylus on a surface, but in a virtual setting.

Findings: Although there is a great deal of fine-tuning still to be done, the model seems to function as a reasonable facsimile of actual physical contact. The vibration signals produced by the stylus were very close to the original vibrations they were modeled on, with only minor and random variation from that baseline.

Moving Forward: The applications of such a tool are far more numerous than this restrained paper feels comfortable enumerating. In particular, it may, if sufficiently accurate, provide a worthwhile simulation of many hands-on tasks in medicine, science, and art, thereby allowing students to learn cheaply and swiftly what has traditionally required a vast apparatus and expensive personal supervision.
