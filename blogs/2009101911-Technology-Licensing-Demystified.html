Technology Licensing Demystified

Adrienne Garber

October 19, 2009

Recently, I attended "It's Worth How Much? - Technology Licensing Demystified," an event hosted by Columbia Technology Ventures, the technology transfer office of Columbia University. The event's speaker, Dr. Daniel Abraham, discussed overvaluation and undercapitalization of early stage inventions as the "Valley of Death" that innovators like us face on the path to commercialization. A former VP and Chief Technologist for The General Electric Company's Technology Licensing Group, Abraham outlined the major components of a University technology license such as: term of the license grant, scope of the license grant (i.e. exclusive versus non-exclusive), field(s) of use, royalties and other payments, sublicensing, US or worldwide IP protection, disclaimers of warranties, and indemnification. He also discussed other factors the University considers in formalizing a licensee-licensor relationship such as technology assessment, market potential and the commercial plan (aka technology roadmap). He reduced the stages of development for an early stage inventions down to "in the head," "in the lab," or "in the fab[rication]." Most useful in understanding the University's goals in licensing faculty and staff inventions was a radar plot that Abraham provided, which included several axes for categorical data such as:

  • equity in the venture
  • upfront costs
  • milestones such as alpha and beta testing, patent issuance, and FDA approval
  • fees such as license issue fee
  • expenses such as patent prosecution
  • annual minimum payments
  • earned royalties such as % of net sales or $ per product sold
  • sublicensing
    • A few relevant statistics on commercialization, according to the Columbia Technology Ventures website are as follows:
