MOOG vs MOOC

Kate Meersschaert

March 6, 2012

No, World of Warcraft (an MMORPG or what I refer to as a MOOG) won't take-over online course delivery anytime soon... although that could be around the foreseeable corner, instead as this New York Times article points-out the new term "MOOC" defines "Massive Open Online Courses" like Stanford's 75K+ Artificial Intelligence course, Udacity and now MITx. What defines these courses and sets them apart from say, Khan Academy or MIT's original open courses? MOOC vs Traditional Online course: -Time-frame... learners are engaged for a set amount of time with temporal obligations to finish homework and quizzes. Like World of Warcraft, there needs to be a feeling of moving-through a mission TOGETHER and not separately going-through lessons on your own as in Khan or the original MIT video lectures. -Badges/Credentials...Some credential and/or badge is given for completion--or in the case of correspondent message boards--for participation. -Shared Goals...Learners are working towards a common goal or mission, whether to finish a the course with a certain minimum grade and credential or to help each other for badges in the discussion boards. Free... Unlike many MOOGs, MOOCs (so far) are free. However, as the NYT's article mentioned above alludes, this may not last. Many MOOC's including MITx, who is now in the inaugural session, will most-likely consider charging a fee for credentialing and testing which would be completed off-site. EdLab Relevance... As we move-forward with our plans for the mSchool and try to imagine both the value of and process for credentialing in the future what elements of the MOOG & MOOC model can we borrow for our unique online learning ecosystem? What would our "quest" for learners and instructors be focused on? Is it possible to motivate based on knowledge acquisition alone, or do we have to offer a credential for maximum engagement?
