Trends in Ed 02.03.10 Paying for Convenience

Jeannie Crowley

February 3, 2010

The New York Times has revealed an interesting new paywall strategy. The Times will allow anyone who arrives at the site through a link (RSS feeder, Twitter, Facebook, email, etc.) to read content for free . Loyal readers who go directly to the Times site, however, will have to pay for direct access to the content. At first, it may seem as if all users will simply begin searching for Times articles in other networks and linking in to avoid paying. This thinking behind this strategy is that most users aren't willing to waste time browsing the Internet to get free articles. Loyal users will be willing to pay for convenience and direct access to content. The Times is banking on their content being enticing and unique enough to keep users from switching to free news sources. Would this new paywall strategy be an option for higher education institutions? Does it even make sense to try to apply a paywall model developed by a for-profit organization to scholarly publishing?
