False Affordances and Control

Anthony Cocciolo

October 1, 2008

There is a great post on the Architectures of Control blog about how many elevator close buttons don't do anything at all. The "Door Close button is there mostly to give passengers the illusion of control. In elevators built since the early '90s, the button is only enabled in emergency situations with a key held by an authority." I think here of the door close button in the elevators in Russell Hall. I know for certain that the door close button does absolutely nothing, yet occasionally when I am in a rush I still hit it a couple of times, even though rationally I know it will do nothing. When you hit the door close button, you can hear some clicking up on the 6th floor, which provides some satisfaction that there is some mechanism receiving your request, although it still does nothing to make the door close faster. The blog author wonders if there are other instances where a fake affordance makes you feel like you have control, and gives you the psychological perception of control, even if it is only that (perception versus reality). My ancient Mercedes (ah, I mean vintage) has lots of perceived affordances that pretend to offer lots of control, yet don't actually offer any (however, this is more a function of the breakdown of affordances rather than designer-intent, e.g., cruise control, window cranks, etc.)
