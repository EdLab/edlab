EdPop! – The Future of the Internet: Challenges Ahead in Global Cyber Policy




The following is a synopsis of “The Future of the Internet: Challenges Ahead in Global Cyber Policy” panel held Wednesday, November 30, 2016 6:00 PM - 8:00 PM at the Faculty House Presidential Ballroom


Panelists:

It includes a discussion with:

Laura DeNardis, Professor and Associate Dean, American University (LDN),

Ambassador David Gross, Partner, Wiley Rein (ADG),

Gordon Goldstein, Managing Director, Silver Lake, Adjunct Senior Fellow, Council on Foreign Relations (GG), 

Moderated by Merit E. Janow, Dean, School of International and Public Affairs (MEJ)


Panel Focus:

The panel is aimed at dealing with the following issues. Who should govern cyberspace and the Internet? Over the past decade, two approaches have emerged: a multi-stakeholder model (MSM) involving a diverse body of State actors that seek consensus through a complex decision-making process, and a cybersovereignty model (CSM) that is built around State sovereignty with less input from the private sector and civil society.


We are at the beginning, not the end, of geopolitical discussions about Internet governance. Our expert panel will explore how the incoming Trump administration should weigh these considerations and address the complex challenge of governing this essential resource, for States and citizens alike.

Part I: Talk

Panelists:

[A] Laura DeNardis (LDN):

-the governance of the internet has shifted from: 

(a) being interesting to a handful of academics and scholars, to 

(b) being at the top of agendas for the G20 countries and is on par with humanitarian concerns, the global economy, terrorism, etc.

-as such, it has been a big shift over the past 10 years with regard to the importance of the internet as a global concern

-technical issues (e.g., protocol, names, numbers, etc.) are high on the agenda on all ends of the political spectrum


Why has there been this shift regarding the internet?

-the internet has become a strategic global resource

(e.g., the presence of the internet contributes $4T to the global economy)

-the internet has impacted every sector

-the internet is now considered the 5th domain of warfare (after land, sea, air, and space)

http://www.economist.com/node/16478792

-technology companies are also now at the top of global rankings of international businesses

-a number of high profile issues involve technology

[e.g., Snowden, Wikileaks, Stuxnet, Russian hacking of the U.S. election, distributed denial of service (DDoS), massive data breaches, etc.]

-as a cumulative result, technology is now in the public consciousness

-from this focus on the internet, there is corresponding greater attention to governance

(e.g., to impact/cope with joblessness, technological change, etc.)

-building on this, the Internet of Things (IoT) will add to these already high stakes


Internet Governance:

-there is an essential conflict between two sides (as noted at the beginning of the panel):

(1) Multi-Stakeholder Model (MSM): 

-this model is universal

-it is an open network

-it is admininstered by a combination of the government and private sector

-as a result, it has led to multi-stakeholder governance


(2) Cybersovereignty Model (CSM): 

-this model is top-down in nature

-it works in the name of authority


-both (1) MSM and (2) CSM are not just visions

-instead, MSM and CSM involve policy choices with real implications for civil liberties, plus the actual security of the internet itself


Conflicts over Internet Governance: (e.g., behind the scenes)

-there are a number of issues dealing with the governance of the internet:

(1) Interconnection of Regulations and Strategy:

-this occurs at places like the International Telecommunications Union (ITU) and the UN

(2) Data Localization: 

-there is a drive/desire to provide privacy in the wake of government surveillance

(3) Cyber Order: 

-these are policies in countries like Iran, Cuba, North Korea, etc.

-this type of order is policed through Domain Name Registries, etc.

-there is a stronger level of multilateral control

(4) Intelligence and National Security: 

-the internet has implication for different types of security

-(e.g., financial security through compromising companies and individuals)

-(e.g., national security)

-(e.g., there are efforts to weaken encryption) 

-ultimately there is a balance between privacy v. law and order


Shifting Narratives:

-as a result of all of the issues discussed above, there is a changing narrative from (1) MSM to (2) CSM

-all of these changes are not just theoretical discussions; they deal with: 

-the stability of the internet

-the protection of human rights


Differences Over Time:

- if the issues/interventions that are occurring today happened two decades ago, would we have the same internet as we have today? (e.g., during the infancy of the internet)

-the internet transcend borders, the internet also transcend national laws

-technologists can often compromise cybersecurity 

(e.g., through encryption)

-even more problematic are the implications for civil liberties 

(e.g., censorship, repress LGBTQ speech in Russia)

-how will the internet proceed/develop in the future?


Fragmentation:

-should the internet be:

(1) universal or

(2) bordered?

-the internet is not universal today, it still have a lot of divides 

(e.g., through filtering, access, technology)

-however, in jurisdictions like the U.S., it has enabled permissionless access

(e.g., people can post content to WordPress whenever they want)



[B] Gordon Goldstein (GG)

-CU/SIPA has built one of the leading thought centers in the world regarding technology, internet governance, cybersecurity, etc.


The Role of Perspective:

-there is an old maxim in political science, “where you stand depends on where you sit”

-I come from 3 different perspectives:

(1) earned a Ph.D. at Columbia; this involved deep reading in neoliberalism, etc.,

(2) my work at Silver Lake, and 

(3) being an Adjunct Senior Fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations (GG), 

-these internet/internet governance issues capture/intersect with these three competing perspectives


The Unique Characteristics/Development of the Internet:

(1) there has been an advance in the internet unlike any other strategic resource:

(a) the internet propels political movements (e.g., Arab Spring),

(b) incidences of hacking/cybersecurity threats have possibly intervened in the recent U.S. election, and

(c) the internet has become an engine of global competition

(e.g., the 5 largest companies in the world are all American: Google, Apple, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft)


The Changing Face of Cybersecurity:

-there have been newer, and more spectacular, cybersecurity breaches

-internet-based breaches have become a driver of the underground economy

(e.g., by 2019, the projected gross aggregate value of illegal and malicious activity on the internet will surpass $2.1 T)


The Changing Face of Geopolitics:

-as a result of all of these developments, the internet has become a game board for geopolitical competition and it is accelerating

-to exemplify these changes, think of the following four examples:


-Example #1: 2003: 

-there was a UN-based multilateral setting

-at the time, the U.S. was managing the Internet Corporation for Assigned Names and Numbers (ICANN)

-ICANN was renewed on a 2-year basis.

-Russia and China introduced resolution regarding ICANN

-there was a push to make ICANN, previously under the control of the US, under control of the UN 

-this effort continued over the years


-as above, there were two competing visions of internet governance:

(1) multi-stakeholder model (MSM), and

(2) cybersovereignty model (CSM).

-CSM is state-driven and asserts state-sovereignty

-CSM has become ascendant in recent years

-there is a grand strategy for:

(1) MSM to use International Telecommunications Union (ITU) to challenge 

(2) CSM

-GG and ADG worked together regarding original rules to govern the internet that were composed back in 1988

-the core issue regarding the internet:

-the US and its allies versus the others; from this, the internet was subject to some UN oversight/authority

-for the US, this issue is a red line; there was no deferment to the US system and the line held until penultimate night of the conference

-identified ITU in international internet governance and future of the development (e.g., occurred at1:30 am on a Thursday night)

-red line crossed, US decided not to support the treaty:

-the US tried to rally its allies

-over a short period, the was US joined by 54 countries that wouldn’t accede

-but 89 countries did accede:

-as a result, the world was in two competing blocks (1) MSM v. (2) CSM


-Example #2: 2010:

-this previously described development has continued since 2010

-China’s position was supported by the G77

-their manifesto? internet governance is subject to the sovereign right of states


-Example #3: Early 2016:

-in 2016, the presidents of Russia, China, India have supported state sovereignty

-they wanted to take technology to create a different internet governance structure around it


-Example #4: Late 2016:

-during late 2016, the World Telecom’s conference in Tunisia

-first to address futuristic tech (e.g., Internet 2.0: “Digital Object Architecture”)

-this was an attempt to organize the system of object identifiers

-as many as 200B devices and people will be connected

-this effort was created by engineers for a non-profit Swiss foundation and ITU

-when will the new navigational technology of Internet 2.0 be employed?

-the entire industrial technology should be connected, including the capacity for surveillance, cybersecurity conflict, etc.

The Future:

-harder than normal to predict


Core Principles:

(1) values reflect those involved in it…

-this is the part of the problem of internet…

-its reflect very important US values:

-free speech

-connectivity

-entrepreneurial spirit

(2) American internet-based success causes envy in the world

(e.g., China players play differently, few from Europe, SA, Africa, or Asia)

-has repercussions around the world

(3) one thing easy to predict what will happen: controversy

-already have had some controversy…

-have not seen the level of controversy we are going to have, especially as the internet will become increasingly important

-there have been some quaint issues (e.g., U.S. control of ICANN)

-it doesn’t go to the core of the internet’s issues (e.g., social, political, economic)

(4) will see governments expressing views of leadership to find their comparative advantage

(e.g., can be offensive or defensive: these divisions will always exist)

(e.g., N. Korea feels danger and is defensive)

-therefore, due to the internet, the chance of conflict increases

(5) there is a fundamental error for futurists: they often believe that the future will look like the past

-all we know is that future will be different than the past

(e.g., rules, beneficiaries, survivors will inevitably change)

(6) need to be flexible (e.g., with respect to the rules governing us)

-new technologies? new drivers of technology?

(7) most interesting discussions will be in the technical community

-new internet architecture

-new technology that will supplant DNS? what will replace the internet of today?

-these possible changes are what makes this so exciting and so difficult

(8) those institutions trying to lock down the internet and technology will, in the long term, be the losers

(9) hope U.S. and other societies will be flexible and innovative


The Dawn of 5G:

(e.g., development of new technology: met President of Bell Labs

-talking about 5G: 5th generation of wireless technology

-5G should be fundamentally different

-4G: good for cat videos

-5G: rolled out in S. Korea and Japan:

-5G has two exciting characteristics:

(1) low latency

(2) high bandwidth

-therefore can do things differently with 5G

(e.g., how we produce)

-not as much impact today in productivity with 4G than with 5G in the future

-winners? Those countries/institutions that build and operate 5G systems

-losers? Those countries/institutions that don’t have flexibility or economic power

-therefore digital divide will become very substantial


Conclusion:

-the future is… uncertain

-the future will go to those who are flexible, responsive, and willing to change…
