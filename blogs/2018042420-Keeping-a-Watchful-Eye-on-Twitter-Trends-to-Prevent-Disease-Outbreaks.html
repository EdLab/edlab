Keeping a Watchful Eye on Twitter Trends to Prevent Disease Outbreaks

Melanie.Hering

April 24, 2018


At what point does the fear of a vaccine surpass the fear of the disease it’s meant to prevent? What are the effects of fighting over whether or not to vaccinate on the larger community? Vaccine refusal has become a growing trend over the past few decades, and one group of researchers from the University of Waterloo has released a study on how this trend affects disease outbreak—and how social media can function as a predictor and preventor of such outbreaks.

Researchers focused on the measles outbreak in Disneyland in 2014–15 to better understand how Google and Twitter trends demonstrate patterns of vaccine refusal and subsequent disease outbreak. With the help of a mathematical formula and a machine-learning algorithm, nearly 28,000 tweets from 2011 to 2016 posted on the topic of vaccines were categorized based on author sentiment and GPS location. Google searches related to vaccines were also compiled and categorized. This data was utilized alongside a mathematical model that assisted in the analysis of vaccine coverage and measles cases in California.

Results revealed that even two years before the Disneyland outbreak, warning signs existed that California was reaching a tipping point regarding measles vaccination. While vaccine compliance was low before the outbreak, with significant anti-vaccine sentiment prevalent in tweets and Google searches, the mathematical model correctly predicted the shift towards greater fear of the disease once the outbreak occurred. Google searches and Tweets peaked at the same point that measles cases occurred, then shot down once measles cases decreased and vaccination increased.

This study demonstrates how digital social data can be used to detect early warning signs of disease and health hazards, and prevent outbreaks before they occur, potentially years in advance. The application of mathematical theories to public health could help researchers, social workers, and medical professionals discover the gaps in health education and help them identify and assist at-risk communities.

Pananos, A. D., Bury, T. M., Wang, C., Schonfeld, J., Mohanty, S. P., Nyhan, B...., & Bauch, C. T. (2017). Critical dynamics in population vaccinating behavior. Proceedings of the National Academy of Science of the United States of America, 114(52), 13762–13767.
