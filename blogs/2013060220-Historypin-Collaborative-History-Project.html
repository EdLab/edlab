Hurricane Sandy: Record, Remember, Rebuild
Hurricane Sandy had significant physical impact on the landscape and community of the Tri-State area and as a cultural phenomenon it left a robust digital record. The organization and preservation of born digital artifacts is an emerging field and information professionals are still experimenting with ways to make these records meaningful. The archival map site Historypin along with Google and several local library organizations including Metropolitan New York Library Council (METRO) and the American Association of State for Local History are working together to preserve and curate digital artifacts from Hurricane Sandy and its aftermath. Historypin is a website that encourages users to "pin" archival photos to particular locations on a map. This action organizes the photographs physically and creates a time-layer for additional meaning. For some popular locations on the site, it’s possible to watch a building, neighborhood, or landmark develop over time. Hurricane Sandy: Record, Remember, Rebuild aims to create a similar layered map with photos from the storm, the destruction left in its wake, and the reconstruction of the affected communities.

Preserving Digital Ephemera
Hurricane Sandy was a highly digital event and the way Sandy-produced web artifacts are curated will set the tone for similar wired news events in the future. The communication between social and traditional media during the storm was volatile and deeply interconnected. While traditional media content has long been collected and made available for posterity via paid databases, open social news coverage and unpaid content sometimes seems destined for the obscurity of rotten links and unsearchable archives. Projects like this and similar ventures like the Library of Congress’ work with Twitter archives seem determined to set a standard for the curation and accessibility of these digital artifacts. The Hurricane Sandy project launched for submissions in early January, but has not yet gone to press on the Historypin site. Contributions are currently being accepted through the Archivists Round Table of Metropolitan New York.
