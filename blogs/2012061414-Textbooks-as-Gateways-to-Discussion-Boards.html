Textbooks as Gateways to Discussion Boards

Kate Meersschaert

June 14, 2012

Details: Yes, you read that correctly, an economics professor (very appropriately) is proposing a new patent for what has been described as a, “web-based system and method to capture and distribute royalties for access to copyrighted academic texts by preventing unauthorized access to discussion boards associated with copyrighted academic works.” In a nutshell...if developed, and widely adopted, this new system would ensure that publishers make more money and students have less free access to collaborative, supplementary learning materials... namely, discussion boards. Fairness? Is this proposed "web-based system" fair to learners? What are alternatives to ensure that students are paying for copyrighted materials and yet not "fencing-in" on otherwise open and free environments for learning. Aren't we moving more towards wiki's, OER and farther from more licensing schemes? EdLab Relevance: Discussion boards are a hotly debated and much researched topic here at the EdLab. Despite the debate, a consensus has been reached that they do add value for learners in online spaces, however, just how much value and how to best offer and structure this experience is still up for grabs. Any thoughts? What is your best online discussion board experience? Worst? What were the circumstances? Read more in this ArsTechnica post!
