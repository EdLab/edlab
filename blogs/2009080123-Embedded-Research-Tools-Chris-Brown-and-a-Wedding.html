Embedded Research Tools, Chris Brown, and a Wedding

HSC

August 1, 2009

Two weeks ago Jill Peterson, Kevin Heinz, and their wedding party became an internet sensation when their video (see below) was viewed more than ten million times on YouTube in a one-week span (currently, it has received more than fifteen million views). What's remarkable about the video is the convergence of copyright law, viral marketing, Chris Brown (who was recently in the news for domestic violence), the monetization of video, and embedded research tools (I've blogged about web analytics tools on YouTube like "Insight" a couple of time before). Specifically, Sony Music, the copyright holders for the wedding party entrance/Chris Brown song "Forever," is able to use embedded research tools to assess the impact of their decision to overlay "click to buy" links in the video to drive traffic to iTunes and Amazon. Here is a pretty interesting (and profitable) finding: "... the click through rate on the "JK Wedding Entrance" video is two times the average of other Click-to-Buy overlays on You Tube. And this newfound interest in downloading "Forever" goes beyond the viral video itself: "JK Wedding Entrance" also appears to have influenced the official "Forever" music video, which saw its Click-to-Buy CTR increase by 2.5x in the last week." To me, this is another case of the power of embedded research (and the almighty dollar trumping copyright regulations). You can read the complete YouTube video case study here. This case also raises some pretty interesting questions about whether the creators of the video should see a share of the profits (Chris Brown is back in the top five downloads in iTunes and Amazon)? How far are we from seeing the music industry paying individuals to help their products go viral? Join the discussion at Techdirt.
