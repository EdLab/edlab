Mandatory Online Education in Idaho

Joanna Fu

January 4, 2012

This article in yesterday's New York Times touches upon a whole host of issues including: - the requirement for Idaho students to take two online courses to graduate high school - the emerging (and unwelcome) presence of technology in classrooms - lack of teacher development and training to effectively use these new tools - the unproven effectiveness of online teaching Of particular interest was the way the article juxtaposes high-tech classroom tools with one teacher's more traditional use of the Socratic method in her high school English classroom. This contrast also seems to exist here at the Gottesman Libraries: we have Socratic Conversations coexisting with the EdLab under the same roof! However, the consensus amongst educators isn't that we ought to forbid technology in classrooms for the sake of human-only instruction; the same teacher that uses the Socratic method in her class also used a YouTube video to supplement the lesson on World War II. I feel like there is less of a divide between the two disciplines — the humanities and technology — than is often implied. STEM education and technology seem like a natural pairing, but I believe that there is also plenty of room for the emergence of tools in which the humanities play a starring role.
