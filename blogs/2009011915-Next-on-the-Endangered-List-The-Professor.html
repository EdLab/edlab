Next on the Endangered List: The Professor

HSC

January 19, 2009

I thought this opinion piece by Stanley Fish in the New York Times provided an “interesting” perspective on the future of higher education. The basic argument is that universities as they have traditionally been conceptualized (learning for the sake of understanding and exploration) cannot survive in today's world. Referencing Frank Donoghue, author of "The Last Professors: The Corporate University and the Fate of the Humanities," Fish explains that liberal education and humanism have been in decline for over a century. As evidence he cites "the shrinking number of tenured and tenure-track faculty and the corresponding rise of adjuncts, part-timers more akin to itinerant workers than to embedded professionals." He adds: "Except in a few private wealthy universities (functioning almost as museums), the splendid and supported irrelevance of humanist inquiry for its own sake is already a thing of the past. In “two or three generations,” Donoghue predicts, “humanists . . . will become an insignificant percentage of the country's university instructional workforce.” What does this mean for a place like Teachers College? Are TC faculty safe since the institution functions more like a trade/professional school? Or will places like Western Governors University Teachers College force us to change our name to The Museum of Teachers College?
