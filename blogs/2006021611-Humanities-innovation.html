Humanities innovation

Jeff Frank

February 16, 2006

I recently read the report that the Wilson Foundation released in September, 2005 The Responsive Ph.D.: Innovations in U.S. Doctoral Education. This report is not just theoretical; it “presents 41 innovations in doctoral practices at the 14 founding partner institutions.ï¿½? This report is interesting for the Lab, because many of these practices deal specifically with teaching support. In addition, I was fascinated by Humanities Out There a program at UC Irvine. This program creates a collaborative environment where advanced students in humanities and experienced teachers come together to create innovative and challenging curriculum for the Santa Ana School District. This is exciting, but it also raises the question: what is the point of having humanities departments at schools of education if this type of program really works? Imagine the leading Ph.D. students in English at Columbia working collaboratively with the most experienced teachers in the city. Will they create better products than students at schools of education who are trying to create curriculum but lack both the intellectual training that students at Columbia have and the experience that long-time teachers have? This may be an unfair question. Instead, we should ask: what can we do here at TC to make us attractive as collaborative partners? What can we bring to the table in this type of conversation?
