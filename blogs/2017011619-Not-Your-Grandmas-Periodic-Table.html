Not Your Grandma’s Periodic Table

Renae Lesser

January 16, 2017


Ptable is an interactive periodic table of elements that goes far beyond the simple graphic at the back of your textbook. This free web app allows users to customize and visually organize the periodic table in dozens of dynamic ways. Ptable also links users to a database of photos, videos, podcasts, and encyclopedia entries about each element or elemental group.

Pros:

I’m amazed by the way this small but mighty app turns an ordinary table into a rich, versatile resource. Users can visually code the table for various properties and characteristics like electronegativity, orbitals, isotopes, valence, and density. Within each of these visualizations, Ptable offers a dynamic search and sorting function that allows users to quickly find and organize elements by their material properties. For example, you can set the app to visually indicate how far an element is from its melting or boiling point at a selected temperature and then use the search bar to instantly highlight elements that boil within a given temperature range. Without a doubt, this densely packed app could be a staple for scientists and students of chemistry. But even those of us who haven’t cracked open a textbook since high school can enjoy exploring the wonders of the chemical world. For example, when I view Ptable through the abundance tab, I can see the relative abundance of each of the periodic elements and can filter by location. This allows me to discover how much nitrogen is in the ocean or how much carbon is in the human body. Features like this make Ptable engaging even for a layperson like myself.

Cons:

As a chemistry novice, I found the technical language and sheer quantity of data contained in Ptable to be a bit overwhelming and before watching the tutorial video I wasn’t sure where to begin. I think that grade school science students might be similarly disoriented without a good deal of hand holding from an instructor. That said, this app is a serious, no-frills digital reference and I don’t think it’s meant to be pretty or kid-friendly.

Our Takeaway:

Ptable seems like an essential and invaluable tool for instructors, students of chemistry, scientists, and anyone interested in learning more about the elements that make up our world. While the app might be over the heads of children or those uninitiated in scientific jargon, I think most users with some chemistry background will find this to be a worthy and enduring resource.
