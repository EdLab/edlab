Testing an Autism Therapy with Virtual Reality

saraehardman

July 4, 2016


As we learn more about autism, therapy options for children on the spectrum continue to grow. A study recently tested the effects of a new therapy practice to improve autistic children’s social cognitive skills: it involves virtual reality (VR) and Second Life.

The researchers aimed to investigate the effects of Virtual Reality Social Cognition Training on autistic children’s social skills. Over the course of ten sessions, kids with high functioning autism entered the virtual environment of Second Life using a VR headset. There, they encountered numerous everyday scenarios so they could practice skills such as emotion recognition and social attribution. In the virtual setting, they practiced making small talk on the schoolyard with other kids, inviting someone to a birthday party, and confronting a bully, among others. All of this was done in pairs of two with a trained clinician supervising the activity in real life.

The effects were clear: after five weeks, the researchers found improvements in emotion recognition, social attribution, and analogical reasoning in the participants. The children were altogether better able to use social clues to understand emotions and differing perspectives, compare social scenes, and consider why other people felt the way they did.

The researchers believe that a virtual environment creates a safe and supportive space for autistic children to practice social skills without the fear of rejection they may experience in face-to-face interactions. With a VR headset, they can practice everyday scenarios with virtual characters and repeat the same scene an unlimited number of times. Although this study does not claim its therapy method is perfect, it does show its potential to help autistic children have less frustrating and more connected social interactions with others.

Didehbani, N., Allen, T., Kandalaft, M., Krawczyk, D., Chapman, S. (2016). Virtual reality social cognition training for children with high functioning autism. Computers in Human Behavior, 62(1), 703–711.
