Small Children with Power Tools

AJ

October 4, 2005

Apparently, people outside of edLab have been trying to revolutionize inefficient teaching practices and anachronistic materials for some time. (Yeah–I missed the memo too.) Some radical members of the rank-and-file have opted to use primary source collections, interdisciplinary texts, and discovery-based technology guides in lieu of traditional, board-approved material. I have heard first-hand (or read about on a seedy website) about the following tools being used in classrooms. Bear in mind that each one was used as the exclusive “knowledge compendiumâ€?, and all assignments and supplementary course material were derived directly from it.

    On Mathematics:
  • Eric Weisstein's MathWorld Classroom
    On History:
  • Howard Zinn's A People's History of the United States
    On the Sciences:
  • Bill Bryson's A Short History of Nearly Everything
  • NASA's Learning Technologies
    On Composition:
  • Thinkmap's Visual Thesaurus
  • Lynne Truss' Eats, Shoots & Leaves
    On Civics:
  • Jon Stewart's America: The Book
    On Economics and Urban Planning:
  • Diane Coyle's Sex, Drugs & Economics
  • who-else-but-Google Google Earth
Why teach children to assemble their knowledge with a screwdriver, when you can give them a drill with a variable bit set? Which tool fosters curiosity and creativity? (Ok, so the analogy needs work, the point remains unsubstantiated, and my credibility is still lacking from my popped collar… but you understand where I'm going with this...)
