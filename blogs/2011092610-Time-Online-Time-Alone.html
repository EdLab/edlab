Time Online, Time Alone

Kate Meersschaert

September 26, 2011

I am beginning an informal investigation into how TC students spend their time online. This unofficial study has 2 goals 1) to generate research for EdLab tools 2) I am curious. To kick-off this effort, I want to share a graph that was recently created by Citi Investment & Research Group: This graph shares some of the most up-to-date findings of which social networks and online suite of tools command the most attention and time. It is interesting to note that despite a splashy "invite-only" beginning of Google+, Google "sites" seems to only control a small percentage of people's attention as compared to Facebook.com. I do call-into question this research as really comparing "apples" (or apps) to "apples" here? Facebook.com has really only been The Facebook, whereas Google through their labs, Docs & beyond, has had many incarnations and identities online. In addition, to further explore this realm, I have started to read Sherry Turkle's , Alone Together: Why We Expect More from Technology & Less from Each Other, which explores 15 years of the MIT Society & Technology professor's experience studying human/machine interactions. She describes in the introduction, "Digital connections and the sociable robot may offer the illusion of companionship without the demands of friendship. Our networked life allows us to hide from each other even as we are tethered to each other." As we evolve our mSchool tools to help move education forward into a web 3.0 reality, what role does this push-pull of a desire to be connected & an inability to actually connect play in product development?
