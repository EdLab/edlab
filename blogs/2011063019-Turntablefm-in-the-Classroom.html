Turntable.fm in the Classroom?!

Kate Meersschaert

June 30, 2011

In the spirit of our recent partnership with the Rock & Roll Forever Foundation, and inspired by this blog post on Publicyte.com, I will attempt to build a case both for and against using Turntable.fm in the classroom. At first glance, with its cute avatars and glitzy font, Turntable.fm comes-across as simply another glorified, social music-sharing gimmick du jour. However, after a cursory exploration and quick spin on the virtual turntables, I felt surprisingly engaged and despite a plethora of musical "rooms" to choose from, not overwhelmed. What would ordinarily seem like too much "choice," with the right UI and levels of customization, allows you to become both a participant and curator of your musical experience. How does Turntable.fm accomplish these mighty tasks and how might this relate to education? 1) Membership. Turntable.fm is invite-only (unless you have a friend on Facebook who is a "Turntabler" which auto-generates an invite), thereby increasing virality & the "peer-review" effect of wanting to impress your friends. This is also a great feature for a teacher who is then able to invite a class to share a session. 2) Game dynamics. DJ's are rewarded with points for playing songs others like, or in a classroom scenario, are highly relevant to a class or lesson. DJ's are also demoted for playing songs that no-one likes or for misconduct. 3) Patience. The fact that DJ's must take turns and share the spotlight is another unique feature and potential "way-in" for the classroom. This creates a tech-enabled "share-a-song" take on the classic "show & tell" model. 4) Privacy. "Rooms" for listing to DJ's musical sets can be made private or public providing yet another selling-point for the classroom teacher who many want to keep a 60's era classic rock jam session private. What is the dark side of Turntable.fm for the classroom? NPR offers the following caveat on their blog, All Songs Considered, "Of all the music-based social media on the web, Turntable.fm is built around arguably the most useful new idea. It's also the biggest time-suck I've come across in a long time." There is the rub, while incredibly collaborative, Turntable.fm also has the potential to be a huge waste of time. Monitoring & assessing the amount of meaningful time that students spend together in a DJ session may be one of the major road-blocks to adoption in the classroom. In Conclusion... Music has always been a central feature in young people's lives OUTSIDE the classroom & Turntable.fm just may help to create a more meaningful, connected, virtual learning environment IN the classroom. Imagine a scenario in which each student gets a chance to play DJ, selecting a song for the class that is most appropriate to a lesson's historic time period, piece of literature or even a connection to a geographical region of study, thereby creating powerfully relevant musical context with which to frame learning. Like the social media backchannel, will social music find a place in the classroom?
