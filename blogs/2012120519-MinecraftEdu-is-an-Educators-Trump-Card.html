MinecraftEdu is an Educator’s Trump Card

jvazquez

December 5, 2012


Latching onto the mammoth popularity of the game that has been likened to virtual Legos, Joel Levin, a computer teacher in NYC, instantly saw the relevance of Minecraft to education. Working with the game’s publisher, Mojang, Joel created the spin-off known as MinecraftEdu. This version, made specifically for use in schools, grants teachers access to customizable maps and powerful building tools that let students experience ancient worlds, chemistry, English, and other subjects.

MinecraftEdu is not without its critics. As with any other classroom activity done on the computer, there is the danger of distraction. Also, some students find the 1991-style graphics off-putting. However, the magic of Minecraft, as players will tell you, is not about the graphics, but the compelling gameplay.

Part of the charm of Minecraft is that its audience is hard to define: high schoolers, college kids, professionals, and even children seem to enjoy losing themselves in the 16-bit world. For many, Minecraft has proven to be a creative escape, where they can spend hours building their dream world out of 3D cubes. In fact, within a short time, uninitiated players soon realize that the actual focus of the game is not on accomplishment, like so many games before it, but on exploration. Minecraft is the ultimate strategy game where players learn resourcefulness is the key to survival-- a lesson that teachers all over the world seek to instill in their students.
