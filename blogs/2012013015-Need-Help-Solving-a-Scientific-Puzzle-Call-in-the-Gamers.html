Need Help Solving a Scientific Puzzle? Call in the Gamers!

Demetri Lales

January 30, 2012

Visit msnbc.com for breaking news, world news, and news about the economy

Back in September, a group of scientists were at the end of their rope trying to figure out a molecular structure that would help them find a cure for AIDS. This structure had these scientists stumped for more than a decade, so in a last ditch effort to help them figure out the structure they recruited the help of gamers all around the globe. They did this by designing a game called Foldit where the object of the game is to fold the structure of proteins in the best way possible. In about 10 days, gamers with no prior knowledge of biochemistry solved a problem that left scientists stumped for more than a decade. This story is a great example of how gamification can solve problems in real world science. Potential for sySTEMic Change: The general public should dismiss the notion of gaming as a time consuming, or a waste of time, and embrace the fact that some games, when they are well designed, enable users to solve some really complex problems. The Foldit game succeeded in solving the structure problem because it was designed to attract as many people as possible, allow them to collaborate on solving a structure, and getting instant feedback via the scoring system in the game. Science has many puzzles to be solved, and so presenting the puzzle through an engaging game environment will attract more of the general public to try and solve them thus increasing the possibility of solving a puzzle that might lead to new scientific discoveries. Let's keep solving puzzles, everyone.
