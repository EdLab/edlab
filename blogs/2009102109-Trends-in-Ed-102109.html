Trends in Ed 10.21.09

Jeannie Crowley

October 21, 2009

Quality vs. Control
The (re)design seminar and Arianna's post about Create Debate bring to light some issues about content control and content quality in learning sites. Traditional formal learning sites generally control who can add or edit content. Is this strict control necessary to guarantee quality in an online learning environment? Newer learning environments, such as Create Debate, provide a mixed response to this question. The publicly created debates are not of high quality, as demonstrated by the following most popular debates:

  • The Scariest: Vampires vs. Werewolves
  • Are girls on debate websites physically unattractive?
  • Are cake brownies better than fudge brownies?
    • Teachers and others can create private debates to control member access, but now we are back where we started: using control to regulate quality.
    Another example of an open content control model is the Apple Discussion Forum. Users post questions and advice for other users about Apple software and hardware. There are no off-topic posts (trolls are discouraged by the TOS) or offensive language. What makes this environment so different from CreateDebate? Perhaps the specific focus of the Apple Discussion Forum attracts a certain type of user. Maybe users feel they are part of a community of practice and act accordingly. Or, maybe the discussion forum is more heavily regulated by Apple staff than we realize and only approved content is allowed to remain on the site. This scenario leads to two questions for the creation of content in learning environments.
  • How do site builders balance the users' desire to create content with the developers' desire to control quality?
  • Is it the illusion of total freedom (with strict editing behind the scenes) an ethical or viable solution for online learning environments?
