Trends in Ed, 4.7.09

Arianna Choi

April 7, 2009

Collision of Memes! Contacts, Friends, Friends of Friends (FOFs), Followers (e.g. in Twitter), and etc.… ahhh, the relationships we form and maintain through online social network are infinite. This network could be cosmic — so vast and so complex — that maybe some of us want to just shout what's going on! Brian Solis, whom I thanked for sharing the web 2.0 conversation prism, recently blogged about Micro Disruption Theory and the social effect. When he explained about tools that chart, plot, or map relationships between people and the conversations they have, I went yes, uh huh. But when he introduced the Social Collider, there was an information traffic jam in my head. Here's the gist: On Social Networks we're bound by context and not necessarily by the relationships that link us in the real world. We listen to relevant keywords to learn from others who share our interests and passions. We're followed or friended by people prior to understanding who they are and how we're potentially affected through an alignment. The continuously-developing connections could be imagined like creating an orchestral music, except there is no conductor. When we are bound by a common context (e.g. G major), you and I are like notes with different pitches and our conversation frequency the rhythm. (I might have incorrectly interpreted this metaphor.) Now let's try visualizing connections/alignments not just by human relationships, but by twitter conversations looked through time. Social Collider shows cross-connections between conversation (x-axis) and tracked time (y-axis). It's visualized “much like the way a particle collider draws pictures of subatomic matter.” This JavaScript visualization demonstrates that posts resonate, spin off, and horizontally link to individuals or topics who relate to them, either directly or in terms of their content.
