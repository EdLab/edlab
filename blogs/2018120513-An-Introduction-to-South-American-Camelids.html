An Introduction to South American Camelids

Melanie.Hering

December 5, 2018

Animals, like celebrity couples and basketball shoes, come in and out of fashion. The trendy woodland creature of 2018 will likely give way to a mythical beast stealing the hearts and imaginations of the populace once January comes round. While I invite you to wonder at what creature will be on our mugs and sweaters next spring, I will 

not be offering my predictions in this post; more importantly, I write to address a desire by one Ryan Wu to possess the knowledge to properly identify one of this season's trendy animals: the llama. Of course I would be remorse if I didn't address the llama's brethren, which are often unfairly excluded from these discussions. So, here you are, Ryan, and world: an overview of South American camelids. 


What are camelids?

Camelids are members of the biological family Camelidae and include all species of camels (yup, there are like three different species of camel) as well as four camelids indigenous to South America. Camelids are large herbivores with split upper lips, long necks, and three-chambered stomachs (instead of four, like cows, deer, and sheep, which are part of another family of mammals called ruminants). They also have elliptically shaped red blood cells which allow for added oxygen consumption at high altitudes. 


One of the most distinctive characteristics of camelids are their toes. They do not have hooves, but rather two-toed feet with toenails and soft foot pads. These are excellent for gripping rocky rocks (aka treacherous terrain), which is very important for our South American camelid friends living in the Andes.


What are the South American camelids?

Why I'm so glad you asked! There are four camelid species residing in South America: the alpaca, the guanaco, the vicuña, and of course, the llama. Alpacas and llamas are domestic, while vicuñas and guanacos represent their wild relatives. 


What is the difference between these four species?

Here's the SparkNotes version:

Llama: The Big One.

Alpaca: The Very Woolly One.

Vicuña: The Small and Elegant One.

Guanaco: If it doesn't fit in the above categories, it's a guanaco. 


Here's a bit more detail:


Llamas are the most well-known of the South American camelids. As the largest of the group, they grow to be nearly four feet at the shoulders (up to six feet at the top of their head), and can weigh between 280 and 450 pounds. Llamas have been used as pack animals for centuries and as such were instrumental in the construction of Machu Picchu. They're sassy, stubborn, smart, and social, but are also pretty peeved at you for spreading that rumor about them spitting all the time. Llamas do spit, but not often, and not without reason; spitting is a social cue to other llamas when asserting superiority, expressing contempt, or for the purpose of self defense. Examples of proper occasions for spitting include but are not limited to the following: you're a lady llama and a dude llama is interested in something you are NOT interested in reciprocating; you're a dude llama who knows another dude llama definitely can't lift as much as he says he can and needs a good neck wrestle to learn some humility; you're a llama whose little sister llama is being a selfish glutton and is trying to eat all your food. Moral of the story? Bad behavior gets you a big hunk of green goo in the face. Take note, humans.

 

Alpacas might not have as much personality as llamas, but they are lovably kind. They are the ones responsible for all those soft sweaters, scarves, and hats that often sport images (or mistaken for images) of those traitorous llamas everyone seems to love so much these days. Their soft wool is their defining feature; their dense, full-body fluff make them look sort of like they stuck one of their toes in an electrical socket. Alpacas are considerably smaller than llamas, coming up to 3 feet at the shoulders and weighing between 105 and 185 pounds. Because they are bred for their wool and not their labor, their behavior is similar to that of sheep, so though they build social hierarchies, the herd mentality is strong. Proof of point: when one alpaca has to poop, typically a whole bunch of other alpacas realize they also have to poop so they all go together and create communal dung piles. Because alpacas like to relieve themselves in the same place time after time, they are actually fairly easy to house train. Want a pet alpaca?Here is your excuse to move out of the city. 

 

Vicuñas are delicate, elegant, and graceful, looking strikingly like gazelles when running. They are the ballerinas of the camelid world. Alpacas and llamas are united in their hate of them and their sleek bodies and their who-me-I'm-just-a-sweet-innocent-little-vicuña act. However, like all Marilyn Monroes of the world, vicuñas have suffered pretty significantly for being gorgeous. Honestly, if you're going to look up more about any one of these four camelids, choose vicuñas because their history with Incan royalty and the illegal wool trade is fascinating. All I'll say is this: Herman Adams, President Eisenhower's Chief of Staff, was forced to resign in 1958 for accepting a coat made of vicuña wool. 


At only 3 feet tall and weighing a maximum of 90 pounds, vicuñas are the smallest of the South American camelids, and used to populate the Andes in numbers close to 3 million. Because their cinnamon coats are finer than cashmere (the finest of all fibers, 12 micrometers in diameter compared to the 50 micrometers of sheep's wool), however, they were hunted until only 5,000 remained in the 1960s. They're heavily protected now, and their population has been restored to about 350,000, but they're still a rare sight, and are almost exclusively found high up in the Peruvian Andes. They're very timid and wary of intruders, but as wild animals, they're entitled to that. They also have better hearing and eyesight than other camelids, so they have both talent AND beauty and should be respected. Did you hear that, llamas in the back? 

 

Guanacos are (I hate to say it, but) the least memorable of the four South American camelids. They really don't deserve it, though; they can spit up to six feet (llamas want you to know that they can spit between 10 and 15 feet), their wool is second only to the vicuña in terms of fineness, and they are the ancestors of the domestic species. Always playing second fiddle, guanacos are one of the largest wild animals in the Andes but still smaller and more slender than llamas, weighing in around 175 to 265 pounds and coming up to about 3.6 or 3.8 feet tall at the shoulders. They can, however run the fastest - up to 40 miles per hour - and can run almost from birth. Respect. 


Like vicuñas, guanacos have faced their fair share of poaching because of their fine wool. However, while 90% of vicuñas live in Peru, guanacos are mostly found in Chile and Argentina. In fact, guanacos really put the "camel" in camelid, as they live not only in the mountains, but in the Atacama Desert, the driest desert in the world, where they survive on water-bearing cacti flowers and lichen. No camel hump, though. I was going to make a jab at camels not being able to run as fast as guanacos because of the hump, but it turns out that camels can also run at 40 miles per hour. I am too impressed to come up with a better way to end this section, so I'll just leave you to YouTube some footage of running camels.

 

How can I tell all these awesome camelids apart?

Beyond size, physical features like ears, wool, and face shape can be helpful when differentiating between South American camelids. Llama ears are banana shaped, with an inward curve, while alpaca ears are smaller, straighter, and blunter. Llamas and guanacos have longer faces than alpacas and vicuñas, but vicuñas and guanacos have similar wool - fine and smooth - while alpacas have wool that looks like a well-groomed sheep's and llamas have wool that can look longer when groomed, or like one of those sad, sad mountain sheep that's been left out in the rain. 

 

In terms of behavior, llamas are the easiest to pick out. They tend to be pretty independently minded, while the others fit better under the categorization of herd animals and tend to be more skittish. Alpacas, however, can also be differentiated by their ability to be amiable companions for humans.


In Conclusion...

As a child, I was that kid that memorized a whole bunch of animal facts and took every opportunity possible to share them with anyone who so much as glanced my way. Clearly I have not changed that much, as I ended up devoting quite a bit of my free time to giving a far more thorough answer to a question Ryan probably expected me to respond to in a couple paragraphs. But it made me happy, and hopefully it will give some of you a new conversation starter or two when at those awkward dinner parties and networking events when you don't know anyone. The again, maybe only I think they're awkward because the sorts of conversation starters I use are things like, "Did you know alpacas use communal dung piles?"
