Good People Chapter Nine: Idiosyncrasy vs Connectedness

kk2012

June 29, 2017



If a man does not keep pace with his companions, perhaps it is because he hears a different drummer. Let him step to the music which he hears, however measured or far away. – Henry David Thoreau, Walden

 

Tjan connects idiosyncrasy to many things from fearless creativity, risk taking and genius to Steve Jobs’ black turtle necks and Lady Gaga’s meat dress, e.e. cummings use of the lower case and Emily Dickinson’s use of the dash.  He evokes Zappos and Southwest as businesses that recruit and hire idiosyncratic people and are successful as a result. 

 

When he says idiosyncrasy, I think he means authenticity – being comfortable in your own skin, unafraid to be who you are, warts and all.  To be yourself and live your truth.  I think he is trying to reconcile that a “good” life necessitates this integrity to one’s self, yet, sometimes that means rubbing people the wrong way or being in conflict with others.  Sometimes it means that you don’t fit in.

 

Connectedness, he says, comes from “being present and being in partnership with another person.” To do this, all parties need to bring openness to others’ differences.  This helps build multidisciplinary organizations that are more innovative.  People who can communicate their story to those who see the world differently are better positioned to build those connections.

 

Tjan tempers his enthusiasm for idiosyncrasy – be weird, be yourself, but not too weird or too much yourself – with a call for connectedness.  The chapter  makes a case that somehow you can go too far with one’s idiosyncrasy and interfere with your connection to others.  That one should be careful that one’s eccentricity not  “isolate or offend” others.  I think he misses the boat here.   

 

I would argue that it is not for the eccentric person to repress his/her eccentricity for the sake of connectedness.  Rather, the individual’s job is to accept and know him/herself and to weather the pain when connection with others is difficult or impossible; to remain open to others even in the face of rejection; and to disconnect when connection inhibits one’s fullest self.

 

If you believe, as I think Tjan does, that connecting diverse individuals in pursuit of a common goal, and helping those individuals “become the fullest versions of themselves” is the secret to leading “good” organizations, then the onus is on the organization to foster a culture that celebrates people’s unique qualities.

 

 “Good People – Chapter 9”: Idiosyncrasy within Connectedness (summary by me with liberties taken)

·      Be yourself.

·      Love yourself in all your weirdness and imperfection.

·      Recognize others are also weird and imperfect. 

·      Love them anyway. 

·      Work on interesting things together.  The rest will follow.

 

 

If the day and the night are such that you greet them with joy, and life emits a fragrance like flowers and sweet-scented herbs, is more elastic, more starry, more immortal—that is your success.   – Henry David Thoreau
