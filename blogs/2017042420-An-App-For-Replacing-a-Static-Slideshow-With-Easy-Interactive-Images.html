An App For Replacing a Static Slideshow With Easy Interactive Images

csd2126

April 24, 2017


ThingLink is a multimedia editing platform that allows students and teachers to easily annotate images or videos. The ThingLink editor gives teachers and students the power to create their own interactive visuals, 360-degree virtual reality images, and videos.

Pros:

With this platform, educators can move beyond static slides and create rich interactive media. ThingLink is an online editing tool where teachers or their students can upload a piece of media and add tooltip points of interactivity that show additional information as viewers hover over them. The ability to integrate images and information in one piece of media could make it easier to use for instruction. This might also be a great way to let learners discover information with more independence. Students can use media to develop their presentation skills and communicate through visuals. The ability to add a layer of information on top of an existing image could enable teachers or students to draw out key facts and figures, connect viewers to new videos, or share personal stories related to these images.

Cons:

ThingLink could be a fun one-off to add to a project. As ThingLink requires closely viewing media and discovering information added in the overlays, it might work best for individual viewing, which could limit its use in a traditional classroom. The $35/month subscription to ThingLink may not have enough value for classroom educators unless they extensively use media in their activities and instruction. While this tool seems to be geared toward classroom teachers, it might be more useful for online educators who need to convey more information in visual and text-based media.

Our Takeaway:

ThingLink is an easy-to-use tool for creating professional-looking media. However, it may have too many bells and whistles for the average instructor to really take advantage of. While online educators may rely more on closely linking images and text, the average classroom teacher, student, or course instructor likely will not need multiple images with information overlays.
