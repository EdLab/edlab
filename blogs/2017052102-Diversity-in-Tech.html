Diversity in Tech

ABARRY

May 21, 2017

Addressing gender and racial inequalities in STEM remains a social and economic pursuit. The majority of software engineers are white males. For example, at Google only one percent of its tech employees are black and only three percent are Hispanic. Oakland, one of the most diverse cities in the US, is implementing computer science as part of its curriculum in almost all of their public schools. Two years ago, there were only three public schools in Oakland offering computer sciences courses. Now, 14 out of 17 is offering the course. One of Oakland's high schools recently participated in one of the best robotic competitions in the world. The gender and racial makeup of the team offer hope that tomorrow's technologists will be more encompassing and diverse. 


Oakland public schools, like other major school districts across the country, intends to offer computer science courses in every high school by 2018. Every high school freshman will be required to take a computer science course as it will become part of Oakland's core academic requirements. 


The robotics team came together due to a shared interest and passion for learning and leveraging the power of technology. They worked together towards a common goal. Collaboration is becoming an ever increasing part of industry. The Oakland example highlights my belief that there are students across the country who are interested in computer science but do not have the resources. By making it a part of the curriculum, Oakland, and other major cities are filling an important niche.
