Virtual Chemistry

Fred Rossoff

October 21, 2013


Title: Effect of a Virtual Chemistry Laboratory on Students' Achievement

Authors: Zeynep Tatli & Alipasa Ayas

Source: The Journal of Educational Technology and Society

Study Design: At a first glance, a laboratory science course would not seem like a good candidate for online education. The purpose of the course seems to be to provide the hands-on experiences that canâ€™t be found in an online course.

To test the feasibility of such a course online, the authors, working with high-school chemistry teachers, created a virtual chemistry laboratory course, and then had it tested in a high school chemistry course in Turkey. They divided the students of the chemistry class into three groups of 30 students each, and then gave the experimental group the virtual course, leaving the traditional hands-on course for the other two groups. They tested each student on their chemistry knowledge, and familiarity with laboratory equipment, before and after the course, and used these findings, along with qualitative interviews, to analyze the effectiveness of the online program.

Findings: Each group of students gained in chemistry knowledge, with the experimental group seeing at least as much knowledge growth as the control groups. In addition, by the end of the course the experimental group seemed, if anything, more familiar with the use of laboratory materials than the control groups, perhaps because the in-person courses tended to involve the teacher demonstrating experiments with the equipment, rather than students using the tools on their own.

Moving Forward: The hands-on, intensive learning that takes place in a laboratory science course can require prohibitive amounts of money and effort, to say nothing of scheduling and safety concerns. The findings of substantial learning among online students should encourage efforts to transition laboratory courses online, where students can more easily and cheaply gain a familiarity with the scientific world.
