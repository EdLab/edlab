What Motivates Risky Online Behavior?

DebraLee

October 31, 2017


For young adults, social media sites provide a unique platform to enhance social development, increase social capital, and identify academic and employment opportunities. A good online reputation is essential to achieving these goals and yet for many social media users, self-presentation management, or the successful portrayal of a positive image while avoiding an unfavorable one, runs counter to posting potentially damaging information online.

To find out what drives the self-disclosure of personal and unfavorable information, researchers investigated whether risky online postings are associated with spontaneous (impulsive) or deliberate (self-monitoring) processes. They designed a risk exposure scale containing 19 items relating to potentially risky images or texts, such as drug and alcohol use, sexual content, personal details, and offensive material, and used it to measure risky online self-presentation in 178 British and Italian undergraduate students, who each completed an online questionnaire.

The results revealed young people behave both spontaneously and deliberately depending on the situation. Interestingly, postings that may be viewed as impulsive, such as those relating to drug consumption, may turn out to represent deliberate choices driven by an individualâ€™s active control of their behavior and the way they presented themselves to others. The study also found that while young adults tend to focus less on being employable and are, therefore, less concerned about the potential future use of information that can be harvested online, many come to regret previous online disclosures.

The authors posit further research will not only enable better understanding of counterintuitive online behaviors, but also help to develop educational and technological strategies to enable young people to more appropriately manage their online self-presentation and avoid later regret and unfavorable consequences.

White, C. M., Cutello, C. A., Gummerum, M., Hanoch Y. (2017) Cyberpsychology, Behavior, and Social Networking. (00)00. Ahead of print.
