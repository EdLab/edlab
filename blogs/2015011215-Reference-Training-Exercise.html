Reference Training Exercise:

The following is a reference guide illustrating how one might go about collecting books, articles, and citations for a psychology or education-related research project, using resources from the Gottesman Libraries.

Step 1: Picking search terms.

A. Brainstorm, pick a topic, and break the topic down into a few fairly detailed questions.  For the purpose of this guide, I'll pretend that I'm a psychology student just starting to brainstorm a potential dissertation subject about recommended therapy techniques for lesbian, gay, bisexual, transgendered, and queer (LGBTQ) Native American and First Nation teenagers living on and off reservations, who are statistically at high risk for depression and self-harming behaviors. Brainstorm: From previous readings, I've gathered that this population is at high risk for depression and self-harming behaviors. I'd like to find culturally-sensitive articles and books that will inform me about factors impacting this population's resilience, including familial and community relationships, attachment styles and development, and spirituality, within the broader context of the tribes and countries that I am looking at. I'd also like to read about therapy practices that are recommended when working with Native American and First Nation peoples, and with LGBTQ teenagers of all cultural backgrounds. I'd additionally like to find information about the problematic aspects of applying psychoanalytic principles to a population that has historically been involuntarily subjected to a great deal of unwanted scientific inquiry. Topic: Depression, self-harm, resilience, attachment, development, and recommended therapies among and for LGBTQ Native American and First Nations teenagers, reservations. Questions: 
  • Is there a correlation between depression and self-harming behaviors and sexual minority status in teenagers? If so, what factors contribute to these intersections?
  • Is there a correlation between depression and self-harming behaviors and 1) socio-economic status, 2) cultural minority status, 3) inherited or multi-generational trauma? If so, what factors contribute to these intersections?
  • What are some recommended therapy practices for 1) LGBTQ teenagers, 2) Native American teenagers, 3) First Nations teenagers, 4) native communities?
  • Are there LGBTQ Native American and First Nations communities within some tribes? Have there ever been?
  • What are possible problems and risks when applying general therapy practices within tribal communities?
  • Are there efforts being made within and outside of tribes in America and Canada to connect and support this population?
Clearly, some of these questions are too broad, and I also haven't broached many more questions on this topic that might be relevant. This is a large and complicated topic, and as of now, I know very little. That said, all of these questions are helpful for me to keep in mind while conducting research; some of them could emerge in the resources that I find on this topic. However, for the purpose of beginning this research, I'm going to try to zero in on what feels like the most relevant and manageable question. B. From these questions, pick one question to start your research with:  Research Question: Are there recommended therapy practices to prevent and treat depression among culturally-marginalized and physically-isolated teenagers, including LGBTQ teenagers, Native American and/or First Nations teenagers, and specifically LGBTQ Native American teenagers, and if so, what are they? C. Pull some generalized search terms out of the question. Search terms: 
  • LGBTQ
  • teenagers
  • therapy
  • depression
  • Native American

Step 2: Fine-tune your search terms, using a Thesaurus and Boolean Search Operators.

Here's how you can double-check the efficacy of your search terms within the database that you are searching. My go-to database for topics related to education and psychology is EBSCO, through ERIC. First I'll find EBSCO's favored search terms, and then I'll search for my topic within the database.
  1. Go to the Gottesman Libraries main page at http://library.tc.columbia.edu.
  2. Click on Databases.
  3. Scroll down to ERIC (EBSCO).
  4. Click on the tab for the Thesaurus.
  5. Enter your search terms into the Thesaurus search engine, to see alternative terms. Here's an example from my search:
  6. According to the EBSCO Thesaurus, the term adolescents can be an alternate (and likely more effective) term for teenagers, and American Indian will be used instead of Native American.
After plugging the rest of my terms into the Thesaurus, I now know that my search terms in ERIC (EBSCO) for my current questions may include:
  • teenagers
  • adolescen* (The star is a boolean operator to cover all variations of the word. More on that in the next section)
  • sexual orientation
  • American Indian*
  • sexual minority staus
  • depression
  • therapy
In the next section, I'll figure out how to mix and match these terms with boolean operators generate a productive search.

Step 3: Testing your search terms.

Note: Depending on how much or little is written about this topic, your question may lead you to an even more specific question, or to a different question altogether. It's time to put our search terms to the test. Here's how I'm going to search for my topic on ERIC (EBSCO).
  1. Click on the tab for New Search.
  2. Click on the link to Choose Databases.
  3. Click on a few of the other databases. If I were researching an education-related question, I'd definitely look in Education Research Complete, and Education Full Text also.
  4. Below the search box are boxes where you can choose whether your results will be full text and peer-reviewed. You can also specify how recent you'd like your results to be, or if there is a particular time frame you'd like to search within. 
  5. Use the boolean operators AND and OR (avoid NOT if you can) to create a specialized search. AND is used for two terms searched in conjunction (adolescents AND therapy AND american indians). OR is used for two related terms searched simultaneously (therapy OR treatment). One other operator that is quite helpful are quotation marks, which ensure that the words within the quotations will appear as an 'unbreakable' or ordered phrase (ex: "american indians"). I added more search terms by pressing the addition symbol highlighted below. Here's my first search:

  6. The first couple of articles look relevant, and may include some citations that I'll want to pursue. I'm going to put collect the articles that I will later send to myself by clicking on the blue folder icon that is next to each article I want to save.

  7. Once you've put an article into the folder, the icon changes.
  8. Once I've put all of the articles that I want from this search into a folder, I'll go to the folder icon in the upper right corner of the page, select the items I want to send to myself, and click the email icon on the center right of the page.
  9. After that, I'll go back to the search page and do another search, using a different combination of my search terms. The original search terms begin to change as I see what search terms are present in the articles that seem most relevant to my search. From this research, I have noted that the terms two-spirited or berdache may be used to refer to LGBTQ Native American and First Nations peoples. The term berdache is now considered problematic, but might be worth searching for as it was used until relatively recently in academic literature. Here are a few more of my searches.
 

Step 4: Research, research, research.

Now that we've collected some articles and feel familiar with our search terms, we can do a more localized search using Super Search, which links to articles, news, and ebooks in many of TC's databases; and EDUCAT, which is the TC Library's online catalog. A. Cbeck out more databases. ProQuest is a great one, another go-to database. Here is my search and a few of the results. B. Pocket Knowledge: Home of TC's online archive. D. Digital Dissertations.
