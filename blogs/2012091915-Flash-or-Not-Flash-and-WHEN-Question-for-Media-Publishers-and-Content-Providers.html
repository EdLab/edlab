Flash or Not Flash (and WHEN): Question for Media Publishers and Content Providers

zhou

September 19, 2012

Some of you may already know about the announcement that Adobe made late last year, that Adobe's support for Flash runtimes would cease on Android devices. And since Apple does not use Flash from the beginning, it means Flash is dying on mobile devices except RIM. Since August 15, 2012, Android devices will not be able to install new Flash players. On the desktop version, Adobe is switching the focus of the Flash runtimes as well. In Adobe's roadmap for Flash runtimes 2012, future emphasis will be place on gaming and premium video contents. Flash developers will have to get licensed in order to use the premium Flash runtimes features such as hardware acceleration. What do these changes mean for EdLab projects? Personally I don't think HTML5 is there yet to replace Flash for streaming videos on a PC or Mac. But mobile devices are already starting to embrace HTML5 videos. It is not just iOS devices. Even for Android, since the website framework is HTML5 and CSS3 (in order to increase interactivity and work with iOS devices), videos probably have to go HTML5 as well because Flash integrates poorly in the HTML5+CSS3 framework. For example, in todays NLT testing, Vialogues videos work fine on iOS devices but poorly on Android. There will be at least two to three years for HTML5 to catch up. But the plan must be discussed as for how everyone of us switch to HTML5 from Flash, and when to do it. It's a hard question.
