Alison.com

Kate Meersschaert

February 23, 2014


We believe that all certifiable or standards-based learning for every subject can be made available for free online. We also believe that Article 26 of the United Nations Universal Declaration of Human Rights which states "Everyone has the right to education. Education shall be free..." will, through ALISON, become a reality. (From the Alison.com site)

Described as "A World of Free Certified Learning" on their site, Alison.com (ALISON stands for: "Advance Learning Interactive Systems Online") straddles the line between professional certification and informal, self-paced online learning. With courses ranging from the practical, a "Diploma in Legal Studies," to the future-focused new course offering, "Virtual Currencies: A Case Study of Bitcoin," Alison.com offers something for almost everyone. From the lifelong learner to the professional who would like to gain new knowledge to build a career, the platform fills many professional and educational niches. Beyond the aforementioned use cases, the platform provides a compelling way for learners to explore potential fields of study or career changes without investing a large amount of time or (any) financial resources. Put directly, "The mission of ALISON is to enable anyone, anywhere, to educate themselves for free via interactive, self-paced multimedia. It is our belief that through ALISON, the cost of access to high-quality education can be removed." While removing cost as a barrier, the platform does impose display ads from partner organizations and companies. Beyond potentially distracting advertising, the site does offer a wide range of helpful features, from a user dashboard and progress reports to content organized by publisher as well as clearly stated "learning outcomes" that accompany each course. Publishers of content hosted on Alison.com range from "Android Developers" to the University of North Carolina offering "Fundamentals of Human Computer Information Retrieval."

An Alternative to MOOCs?
Unlike course platforms from MOOC providers Udacity or Coursera in which the learning experience is tied to courses that might be offered at a traditional post-secondary institution, Alison.com provides a platform in which basic introductory level content necessary for on-the-job or remedial training is combined with more casual interest-based learning opportunities. This mix of foci and content is a nice point of differentiation in an already saturated online learning market. Of course only time, in the form of a few thousand online hours, will tell whether employers will accept this version of free, and free-standing, education as a valuable credential.
