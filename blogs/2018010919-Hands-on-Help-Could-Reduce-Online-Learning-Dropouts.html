Hands-on Help Could Reduce Online Learning Dropouts

DebraLee

January 9, 2018


Online learning is popular with adults who live out of town and/or juggle hectic schedules. But despite the industry’s consistent growth curve, high dropout rates have plagued degree programs since online distance education emerged in 2001.

Researchers in a study of 2,129 students (856/40.2% male and 1,273/59.8% female) enrolled in a cyber university in South Korea, set out to provide insights about dropouts by empirically testing a path-analytic model that described the detailed relationships among the main factors affecting distance student dropout decisions. These included scholastic aptitude, physical constraints, interaction with course content, satisfaction, and GPA.

They found that adult distance students who have more physical constraints, like social and family responsibilities, are more likely to dropout of online degree programs and have lower GPAs. They are also less likely to interact with course content and be satisfied with online courses.

Researchers argue that dropout rates could be reduced if instructors and administrators provided hands on help; for example, if they made it easier for students to interact with course content, used learning materials with short segments accessible through mobile learning tools, provided brief summaries of course content, offered periodic one-on-one assistance with tutors, and gave encouragement through engaged instructor support. Such efforts could increase learner interaction with course content, which could, in turn, positively impact satisfaction, GPAs, and the crucial decision to persist in online degree programs.

Choi, H.J., Park, J. (2017). Testing a path-analytic model of adult dropout in online degree programs. Computers & Education, 116, 130–138.
