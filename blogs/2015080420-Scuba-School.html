Scuba School

EdLab Studios

August 4, 2015


While ELA, math, and social studies education does find its way into the school day, it's hard to ignore some major differences in being a student at the Urban Assembly New York Harbor School and being a student most anywhere else. Namely, having class under water and the Governors Island ferry as a school bus. As part of the school's career and technical education program that begins sophomore year, students can enroll in any of six CTE programs of study: aquaculture, marine biology research, marine systems technology, ocean engineering, vessels operation, and professional diving, each of which leads to industry certification and real-world experience. The professional diving course, taught by Liv Dillon and Joe Gessert, is no exception. These students, just old enough to drive a car, have teamed up with the Billion Oyster Project in an effort to foster environmental stewardship and put their aquatic skills to the test. Diving below the murky waters that surround the five boroughs, students plant oysters grown in nurseries and survey the restoration efforts directed to the once-plentiful oyster population, the thriving marine life that successful oysters bring, and new sites for future BOP work. The goal is to eventually restore one billion oysters to New York Harbor; an objective that New York Harbor School students will have a profound effect in achieving.
