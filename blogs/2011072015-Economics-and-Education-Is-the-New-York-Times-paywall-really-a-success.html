Economics & Education: Is the New York Times paywall really a success?

Skanda Amarnath

July 20, 2011

This post is more economics than education, but the basic nature of selling knowledge as a good or service makes this story relevant. Many have been singing the praises of the NYT paywall, given the jump they've seen in the number of subscribers. As a result, analysts are improving their ratings of NYT stock and other publications are entertaining the idea of a paywall, including Time magazine. Yet before they do so, they should keep in perspective the gains that they seek to achieve from putting up the paywall. If it is simply a one-time increase in subscribers that a publication is seeking, then the paywall might well do the trick. I doubt NYT will continue to see strong increases in the number of subscribers. Most who consume NYT content but have chosen not to subscribe have probably figured out how to bypass it by now. As more people learn about ways to bypass it, some set of those that were motivated by the paywall to pay up will likely end their subscriptions at some point. The only way the paywall can actually continue to yield a steady increase in subscribers is if the technology behind the paywall gets better and shuts down many of the easy ways to bypass the system. Unless the paywall gets better, I see it as merely a tool for producing a one-time increase in the number of subscribers.
