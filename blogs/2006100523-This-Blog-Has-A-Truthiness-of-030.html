This Blog Has A Truthiness of 0.30

Dan Mallinger

October 5, 2006

After Waterloo, Napolean said "History is but a fable agreed upon." In 2002, Richard Suchenwirth created a TCL script to estimate the truthfulness of certain statements using the Google API. (Really just representative of the time and every coder's desire to utilize that knowledgebase to glean social concensus.) This week, Eric Schmidt, of Google, predicts the company will be able to classify political statements in fuzzy sets by an "Is True" predicate. The notion of "fuzzy truth" and the "social construction of reality" are uncomfortable realizations. I'd much prefer if the answer was 42. In their days as educational researchers, both Dewey and Thorndike noticed that young children gravitated toward algebra. And both reasoned that the children drew satisfaction from a notion of singular truth: 2 + 2 = 4.    (read more) With increased global connectedness, will people really learn to "see all the sides" and recognize that right and wrong can't apply in most situations? Will "fuzzy truth" really be a comfortable concept? From my experience, the Daily Show never agrees with Bush; and Fox News never promotes liberal politics. Maybe I don't give people enough credit, but I don't see this changing. And I wonder if there isn't a lesson here for new social education tools. Are we focusing too much on diverse and varied content (i.e. too much to maintain an audience)? What can we do to create online communities with integrated goals and a sense of social belonging for users? We might be able to learn a lot from Fox News - without the utter absurdity of course (see the last 3 minutes of this clip).
