About mobile applications for library services

Ting Yuan

March 10, 2010

The Use of Handheld Mobile Devices: Their Impact and Implications for Library Services Library Hi Tech / Vol. 28 No. 1 / 2010 / pp. 22-40 Methodology â€” A survey is delivered to participants (N=206) via university portal and Facebook together with hard copies given to people entering and leaving university library to measure whether people want to access an OPAC with a small screen. Additionally, through open-ended questions, the survey attempts to gain a broader understanding of handheld mobile computing's impact on, and implications for, the services provided by academic libraries. Review and Findings

The Harvard University Libraries mobile site provides an immensely useful service that provides real-time info on campus library hours. Some institutions choose a catalog add-on such as III's AirPac to enable mobile usage of their services. Some institutions have used translator services to provide text-only access to their website, which make it much easier for a screen reader to navigate, and makes most sites fit much easier on a handheld device. 35.4% of the respondents had web enabled cell phones only, 12.6% had both web enabled cell phones and a PDA, and PDA only ownership was far less common at 6.8%. 45.2% of respondents indicated that they would use one of these small screen devices to search the library' catalog whether they currently owned a web-enabled handheld device or not. A user that connecting to an academic library website through a mobile device will most likely have very specific tasks in mind.These tasks may vary slightly by institution but include tasks related to the catalog interface.
