It is interesting to note that, with a few exceptions, terms searched on the app tend to be much shorter than those on the desktop site.



Of the total site searches, only 17% were filtered by category. For searches that were sorted, here’s how they stacked up:

  • Articles (39% of sorted searches)

  • Books (21%)

  • Everything (19%)

  • Journals (16%)

  • Website (3%)

  • Archives (3%)

  • Knowledge base (2%)


Only 15% of total site searches went on to look for “More Results.” As usual, the categories most sought after were Articles and Books.

  • 79% of “More Results” were for Articles

  • 17% of “More Results” were for Books

  • 2% of “More Results” were for Journals

  • 1% of “More Results” were for Archives

Takeaways

  • As we move forward into the future of app development, it will be worthwhile to take stock of the behavior differences between searches on site vs the app. For example, while the content searched on both platforms is predominantly for library holdings, app searches for things like “hours” and “endnote” may point to a rising trend in patrons using the app over the site to acquire just-in-time information. Perhaps the shorter app search terms demonstrate this urgency.

  • Something to watch: the tendency for singles to book rooms from the app might signify its viability as the “personalized” library experience, the original vision of the app.
