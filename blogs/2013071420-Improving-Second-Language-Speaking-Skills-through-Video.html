Improving Second Language Speaking Skills through Video

Fred Rossoff

July 14, 2013


Title: Innovative Second Language Speaking Practice with Interactive Videos in a Rich Internet Application Environment

Authors: Juan A. Pereira, Silvia Sanz-Santamaria, Raul Montero, & Julian Gutierrez

Source: International Education Studies

Study Design: If the rumors are true, there are whole nations composed entirely of bi- or multi-lingual students. For our more average domestic population, learning a second language can be a difficult or even an impossible task. A growing population of native Spanish speakers has brought a new focus on the pedagogical challenges of second language teaching and learning.

The authors of this paper, Spanish natives focused on crossing the bridge between Spanish and English speaking, analyze an online learning program called the Babelium Project (BP), developed to help language learners practice their oral skills. BP provides tools for "producing and sharing interactive video exercises in different languages." This study analyzed the usage of the program by Spanish university students enrolled in English courses.

Findings: Although they provide no data on actual fluency, pre- or post-study, the authors report that students using the program ended up devoting significantly more time to their English studies than they felt would otherwise have been necessary. Interviews with students and teachers suggest that BP encourages oral language practice; sharing and critiquing videos of second-language speech seems to be a more inviting learning tool, with a lower threshold for engagement, than traditional drills.

Moving Forward: They say that the best way to learn a language is to practice using it. The emerging suite of tools provided by new, widely available video recording and sharing technology should make it easier to do so even when a studentâ€™s offline environment is monotonously monolingual. Whether the Babelium Project is the proper vehicle is, of course, another story, but certainly we should look for something like it to enter into widespread pedagogical practice.
