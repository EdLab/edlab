Wearable Technology Effectively Coaches Autistic Children in Social Skills

Melanie.Hering

February 8, 2018


Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) affects approximately 1 in 68 individuals, and yet very few evidence-based interventions exist for one of the key characteristics of ASD: social skills deficits. Interventions that do exist tend to be costly, resource-intensive, and vary in terms of outcomes. However, researchers have recently developed a social skills coach in the form of wearable technology that can listen to conversations the user is having and prompt appropriate social responses.

This coach takes the form of Holli, a software application that runs through Google Glass, a pair of modified eyeglasses with a small display, microphone, and touchpad. The device captures spoken word from conversations and translates it into text. Then, Holli provides potential responses users can give.

In order to determine the effectiveness, efficiency, and user satisfaction with this new technology, researchers conducted a study of 15 children between the ages of 8 and 16 with ASD. Though the researchers acknowledge that this is a small sample size, they assure it is sufficient for revealing at least 90% of usability problems. Participants were asked to utilize Holli while engaging in a restaurant-themed interaction with a researcher. The researchers noted how accurately the application responded, how quickly the user and application responded, and then had participants complete a post-session questionnaire to evaluate their experience with the application.

Results of the study demonstrate that Holli is a viable tool for children with ASD. The application accurately detected and recognized 9 our of 10 user utterances in real time, and all users were able to complete the full 10-turn exchange. User response time varied due to differences in reading time, pointing out a potential flaw with the application. Additionally, more complex artificial intelligence is needed for more sophisticated conversation topics. However, participants expressed a strong affinity for the technology, describing it as easy to operate and enjoyable to use.

The positive reaction to Holli demonstrates that this technology might be a real breakthrough for those with ASD. Google Glass and Holli can provide the ability to practice social skills in a safe, controlled environment while reducing cost and other accessibility barriers. While previous ASD-assisting technology based on human-to-computer interactions has been criticized for being socially isolating, this study demonstrates that utilizing technology for human-to-human social coaching is possible.

Kinsella, B., Chow, S., Kushki, A. (2017). Evaluating the Usability of a Wearable Social Skills Training Technology for Children with Autism Spectrum Disorder. Frontiers in Robotics and AI. 4 (31)
