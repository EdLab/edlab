The State of STEM Education

rendy

February 24, 2014

The recent Organization for Economic Development (OECD) results show that America's students are far behind their peers in other industrialized nations. According to the exam, American students rank 25th in math and 17th in science. The results come at a time when the demand for STEM related jobs are an all time high. The STEM industry is one of the few has seen sustainable growth even in the midst of the recession. The results from the OECD also highlight a troubling trend: that students are shying away from STEM. The effect is a lack of skilled workers to fill STEM jobs. How can we recapture student interest in STEM? Does this amount to a crisis in our schools and ultimately our economy? When the landmark report, “Nation at Risk” was released 30 years ago, educators, business leaders and the American public all used it as a watershed moment to really address the problems ailing our schools. Now, some are calling for a similar response to the STEM crisis. The National Research Council has released a set of science standards and key stakeholders also recently met a national conference to discuss ways to encourage and maintain student interest in STEM. Are we really in a crisis as a nation when it comes to STEM education? What are some possible solutions?
