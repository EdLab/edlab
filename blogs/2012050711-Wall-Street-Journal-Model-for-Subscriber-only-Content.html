Wall Street Journal Model for Subscriber-only Content

Kate Meersschaert

May 7, 2012

Image from the collections of the National Archives. Why do we care? As we move-forward with development of the New Learning Times, I thought I would share the Wall Street Journal's model for promoting subscriber-only content. First, I confess that the Wall Street Journal Online has not been one of my go-to destinations on the web. Instead, I generally subscribe to an assortment of news feeds through Instapaper's mobile app and receive daily updates via email from The New York Times. Second, I do not pay for ANY content online. Despite these two factors, I was *almost* compelled to break-out the credit card when I encountered the WSJ's pay-wall pictured below. I came-across this barrier-to-reading after attempting to view an article on Facebook's IPO that was deemed "subscribers only." It is worth noting that most articles on WSJ, unless they are featured on the homepage as a headline, are "subscriber only" (sounds like TCR!). Why is the WSJ pay-wall different? The online version of the classic publication teases you with side-bars of intriguing, subscriber-only content and then forces you to subscribe to get any measure of reader satisfaction. No 20 articles free to start-you-off... instead, you are immediately faced with an option to subscribe to the "WSJ Digital and Print" or "Digital Plus" WEEKLY subscriptions for either $4.99 or $3.99 respectively. Would you pay for this "premium" content? I can now honestly say that I would. Not only do I trust (thought previously underused) the content provider, but they use simple headlines, and effective sample text to dangle an answer to a question I am curious about. Also, they provide no other option... either you pay or go to a different site. Additional Questions... How did they decide on a weekly fee-structure? Why is digital-only content only $1 more than digital + print? Is a monthly cost of more than $15 & less-than $20 too much for premium news content? Who do you envision being the ones to pay for this content? What other content providers have handled a pay-wall the best?
