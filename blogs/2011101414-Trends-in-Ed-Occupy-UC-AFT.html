Trends in Ed: Occupy UC-AFT

Fred Rossoff

October 14, 2011

Inside Higher Ed reported this week on efforts by the “University of California chapter of the American Federation of Teachers” (UC-AFT) to block the UC system from transitioning to online education to cut costs. A new provision accepted by the University of California last week will prevent them from implementing online courses that “result in ‘a change to a term or condition of employment' of any lecturer without first dealing with the union.” It seems that this gives the union power to effectively prevent any online program it wishes, although, rather than a veto, they simply have the right to pause innovations until a process of intermediation has been undergone. But union lecturers, understandably worried that their jobs might be rendered obsolete by this new trend, are expected to slow down the process as much as possible. At this point it looks like a tempest in a teapot, considering how little use the University of California has been making of online learning technology, but there may be larger repercussions down the line, and the claim, by union president Bob Samuels, that he doesn't think conclusive empirical studies about the benefits of online learning are possible on the issue, doesn't give me a lot of confidence in the union's good faith. This is a simple conflict of interest, but seeing as online courses look like a great way to lower university costs (and decrease student debt), it may be one of a growing number where unions and the progressive youth of the future (#OWS) find themselves on opposite sides.
