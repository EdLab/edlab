Sky Map

LauraCostello

September 4, 2012


Not so long ago, several Google employees used their 20% time to build the first version of Sky Map. The astronomy application was an immediate hit in the early Android boom and gained much praise thanks to its usability and expansive information. Using the GPS, compass, and clock on a smartphone, Sky Map users peer through their smartphone at the sky and the app reveals the names of celestial bodies seen and unseen around them. This January, more than 250,000 downloads later, Google decided to make the app open source and announced a partnership with Carnegie Mellon University. Google engineers will remain on the Sky Map team as advisors, but the real development of the app will fall to students and researchers from the University.

Pros:

The interface orients quickly via compass and there is seamlessly little lag time during movement and panning. This truly delivers the "window on the sky" feel promised by Google’s tagline. The well-designed app allows amateur astronomers to peer into the night sky even when the stars are obscured by ambient light or clouds. For users who happen to be viewing actual stars in tandem with Sky Map, the tool features a night vision mode with low light and contrast. Other bonuses include the time warp feature that reveals skies past and a gallery of Hubble Space Telescope images that can be located in the sky, much as Google Maps locates near landmarks.

Cons:

Also like Google Maps, there are times when the GPS fails. In this scenario night sky users can kick it old school by orienting to the North Star, a quaint touch feature that some nostalgic users may opt for, even with perfect GPS functionality. Sky Map’s popularity has led to the development of a set of similar apps aimed at different audiences. Some reviewers prefer the story-heavy Star Chart app, which can be downloaded for a nominal fee. Sky Map feels more scientific than most of its brethren, especially since it will soon be informed by the scintillating research of Carnegie Mellon’s Bruce and Astrid McWilliams Center for Cosmology.

Future Possibilities:

Sky Map is one of many apps that demonstrate the power of mobile computing in the education of the future. The power to superimpose a layer of learning on physical life and wring out information from everyday realities, and the ability to do so at any time and in any place, is a brand new and extremely exciting trend in educational technology. The rise of similar apps from different disciplines like Leafsnap, Historypin, Streetmuseum, and Layar seem to suggest a future laden, with earthly and celestial learning opportunities.

Our Takeaway:

Lost in Space? Sky Map is illuminating the dark reaches of the universe.
