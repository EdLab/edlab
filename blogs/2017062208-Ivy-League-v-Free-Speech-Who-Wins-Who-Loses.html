Ivy League v. Free Speech? Who Wins? Who Loses?

dinosossi2

June 22, 2017



The recent rescinding of offers of admissions to 10 incoming Harvard students regarding errant Facebook posts is a fertile ground for the debate over the First Amendment.

 

In institutions that, in their fullest expression of their higher education ideals, promote freedom of speech, what are the limits for students in terms of expressing themselves?

 

This case raises a number of interesting issues:

 

  • Does the fact that the students were admitted, but not matriculated, mean anything?
  • Does the rescinding of an offer of admission constitute a breach of contract according to common law?
  • Are there distinctions between private universities like Harvard and their public counterparts?
  • Do institutes of higher education that espouse freedom of speech have a right to limit these constitutionally embedded rights once students are matriculated?

 

Social media allows some of the freest forms of expression possible. Even those suffering from the most dire of financial circumstances can log on to a free kiosk at a local library, sign up for a social media account, and post as they wish. How should we as a reputedly free society that cherishes free speech deal with expression that deviates from the norm? Should we?
