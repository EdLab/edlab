All Feedback Is Not Created Equal

Cinthia.Fabian

March 27, 2018

Last Tuesday, the NYC EdTech meetup hosted an EdTech Expo in collaboration with TCEdTech (right in our own backyard!). Up-and-coming companies showcased their work to the TC community in exchange for customer guidance on how to improve their products. 


Several of the companies in attendance were ones we’re already familiar with — Buncee, Selected, and MoxieReader have led EdLab Seminars, whereas Best Fit and ReBuild were pitched by students at the Innovation Showcase held in the Learning Theater last fall. 


Other companies included Ruvna (helps schools stay connected in emergency situations), Quartolio (curates academic research into a single, personalizable platform), and BioDigital (a 3D human visualization tool). 


Upon arrival, attendees were given paper slips with the prompt, “How could this product be more useful, user-friendly, and valuable?” 


Feedback was ostensibly informed by conversations between attendees and product developers, but nonetheless I'm curious: How helpful are answers to a question so broad? Did companies have specific areas of concern that weren't addressed? How familiar must you be with a product for your criticism to be helpful/valid/non-superficial? 


The utility of feedback solicited in this way may depend on the stage of development in which we find the product. 


Overall, I have two major takeaways in terms of our work at EdLab: 

  • As we think about how we might assess events held in the Learning Theater, in what ways might we creatively scaffold the feedback process? Rather than a single nonspecific question posed on a small slip of paper, could we somehow structure the process so that feedback is more organic/nuanced/relevant? 
  • Should we consider hosting an event with a similar purpose — gathering outside feedback on our own works in progress? What products might we focus on? Who do we want to hear from? 


Let me know your thoughts below!
