Using Mobile Devices to Promote Children’s Learning

Adrienne Garber

February 4, 2010

Did you ever watch Sesame Street when you were growing up? Are you curious about what The Children's Television Workshop (since renamed Sesame Workshop), founded in 1968, is up to now? How have technological developments in the digital age affected this organization? Teachers College alumnus Nabeel Ahmad, Ed.D. and doctoral candidate Dominic Mentor, instructors of the nation's first mobile phone learning class, invite you to a special webcast of their course to hear Carly Shuler discuss the impact of mobile devices in children's learning. Details:

  • Link - http://webconf.tc.columbia.edu/mobileed
  • Date - Thursday, February 4, 2010
  • Time - 5:45 p.m. to 6:45 p.m.
  • Space is limited; please join the session early.
  • For those who cannot login, the session will be recorded and available for playback.
    • Session description: Perhaps the most ubiquitous technology in childrens' lives today are mobile devices – tools such as cell phones, iPod devices, and portable gaming platforms that traverse home, school, and play via the hands and pockets of children worldwide. However, most educators and parents have been skeptical, until now, about mobile devices' value in learning. The Joan Ganz Cooney Center at Sesame Workshop recently issued a study, Pockets of Potential, which drew on expert interviews, market trends and model programs to document the untapped potential of mobile learning. Carly Shuler will discuss key findings of her recent report "Pockets of Potential". Currently, Shuler is a Cooney Fellow focused on industry initiatives and research at The Joan Ganz Cooney Center at Sesame Workshop, where she recently researched and authored the reports "D is for Digital" and "Pockets of Potential". Attend the virtual session today (Thursday, Feb. 4. 2010) from 5:45 p.m. to 6:45 p.m. to learn what the future holds for this new medium in education. For more information, contact: Nabeel Ahmad (na2189@columbia.edu) Dominic Mentor (djm2123@columbia.edu)
