Apple's ARKit - Emerging Technologies Consortium

Christine.Odenath

February 20, 2019

On Friday Paul and I attended an Emerging Technology Consortium event where Steve Hayman from Apple demonstrated how anyone(!!) can build an app using Apple's developer tool Xcode (free in the app store). For about an hour, Paul and I sat mesmerized as Hayman used Xcode's ARKit to build an Augmented Reality app in real time. I left the event fully confident in my ability to make an app using Xcode. Paul, who has actually attempted coding in his life, seemed more skeptical of the "ease" of using these tools... and he was right.


When you build your app on Xcode, you can choose to build in Single View (simple), Game View (advanced), Augmented Reality (seemingly simple, definitely advanced), or a few other pre-programmed app types.




One of the coolest things about Xcode was that you could build out your UI without even TOUCHING the code, then you can test how the app would look either on your phone or on your computer screen.





If you're interested in building an AR app, you'd probably like to know how Apple's AR works! Apple's AR anchors AR objects in space by using scene/image recognition, facial recognition, and/or world tracking configuration. What is world tracking configuration? According to my notes, world tracking “uses a gyroscope, etc. to track your place relative to your AR-placed objects; stable coordinates - visual inertial odometry → visual + inertial = stability of view.”


Gyroscope, etc.


So Apple is currently using gravity to understand your device’s location relative to other real life objects and AR objects. Apple is also working on some technology where you can scan a real life object 360 degrees and then incorporate that object into mixed reality scenarios. It seems that development on AR, VR, mixed reality applications are moving quickly, so we'll need to stay tuned.  


At this point in the blog post I’ve deleted many lines of copy that detail rudimentary features of ARKit with great enthusiasm. I’m too much of a novice to know whether the qualities I found amazing in the app building demonstration are actually worth sharing. Plus, I started to sound like an ad for Apple and I’m not here to hand out free PR. However, I feel obligated to reiterate that Hayman made the app building process seem so intuitive and attainable. Xcode makes coding so easy, 9 year old Anvitha Vijay used it to create Smartkins: an app that helps you learn animals and 82 year old Masako Wakamiya used it to create Hinadan: an app aimed at elderly game play.


If 9 year olds and Nanas are building apps, I can too! Right? Wrong. I had big plans to end this blog post with a link to my new app. I was going to hop into Xcode and commit maybe 2 or 3 hours to building an AR app, then by lunch time I’d be strutting around EdLab showing off to my adoring fans. “Days? No, it did not take me days. It was really so easy!” I’d say, pointing my iPhone at an empty table and wowing the crowd as what appeared to be a real kitten appeared on the screen. Then I’d upload my app to the app store and make millions

of people happy (free download).


In reality, the process of downloading Xcode took 20 minutes, then I played around for another 30 minutes in Xcode and it dawned on me that I really don’t know how to code.  Per Hayman’s demonstration, you can build a lot of your app in Xcode by just using the UI view. But if you want an app that has more than a static cat image on the screen, you need to know how to code. And honestly I can't even figure out how to upload a cat image.
