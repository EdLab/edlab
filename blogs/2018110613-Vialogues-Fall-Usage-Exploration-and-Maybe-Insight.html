Vialogues Fall Usage - Exploration, and Maybe Insight

Nikita.Bogdanov

November 6, 2018

As someone new to EdLab, this initial project has afforded me an interesting lens through which to expand my understanding of the team. Perhaps more so than any information session or seminar, working directly to familiarize myself with some of the usage data from various EdLab platforms has forced my curiosity and has highlighted how much there is yet to learn and to understand about what we are and what we do. Adding to this novelty is the challenge of negotiating personal interests with institutional agendas: how do I honor at once my own questions and those most pressing for the staff closest to these platforms? Put differently, the crux of this assignment lies in the navigation of a more fundamental uncertainty around organizational life and ownership--an uncertainty that asks, “Who owns EdLab? Who is EdLab? What are we hoping that our platforms do for the world? And who decides this?” I grappled with these questions frequently in preparing this post, wondering how much I could truly contribute to an organization I had not yet made mine--nor that had yet made me its own. The operative word, of course, being the imminent “yet.”


And so, I present some overview statistics and nascent insights into the Vialogues usage data from October of 2018--my first such report and my first foray into this field. Please, chime in with any questions into which you would like more insight. Moreover, I invite you to contribute a sentence or two that for you captures the nature and purpose of Vialogues, which I hope will help guide future such reports.


The big trend: more users, less engagement. That is, this October and this fall semester, more unique individuals from unique devices (new users) visited Vialogues than during these same times last year--though the percentage increases are marginal. And yet on average those that visited did so across 7.24 percent fewer sessions and saw 34.08 percent fewer pages. Disaggregated at the level of new and returning visitors, two interesting statistics standout:

  • Returning visitors engaged in 14.56 percent fewer sessions this October than they did last October, while new visitors actually increased their number of sessions.
  • New visitors visited 44.16 percent fewer pages per session this October than they did last October, at the same time as for returning visitors this decrease was only 17.58 percent.

Generalized, these trends suggest that returning users are finding fewer reasons to engage with Vialogues and are visiting fewer pages, while new users are finding more reasons to engage with the Vialogues platform but fewer reasons to engage with multiple pages while visiting the website. Of course, the complexity of the digital engagement landscape forecloses the possibility of determining with any significant level of confidence, and from this data alone, either the veracity of this assessment or the nature of the underlying causes.





That said, longer-term trends and anecdotal experience suggest the primary dynamics at play are several fold. First, usage tends to follow the academic schedule, peaking regularly mid-semester and then declining slowly until the beginning of the next term. Second, many teachers use Vialogues in their assignments, meaning that students have very specific reasons for visiting the platform and that they may therefore be more likely to leave after completing their required tasks--unlike the general public, which may engage in more browsing. If widespread, such academic usage could account for the decrease in the number of pages viewed per session.


At the same time, however, other engagement metrics are on the rise. For example, September and October of this year saw more videos posted than their counterparts in 2017--991 vs. 748, up 32.49 percent; and 635 vs. 603, up 5.31 percent, respectively. This fall further saw record-high numbers of comments, with September having the most of any other month on record (9,518) and October coming in second (8,897).




Now, on what days and during what hours do users comment on and otherwise engage with Vialogues? The most popular commenting days in October of 2018, for example, were Tuesday (2208), Monday (1827), and Wednesday (1648). October of 2017 follows this pattern by and large. Conversely, across all of fall semester of 2017, the most popular days are Thursday (4536), Wednesday (4354), Tuesday (4059), and Monday (3853). Across other slices of time Thursday likewise alternately is among the most or least popular. Despite this variation, what we can say for certain is that the work week consistently sees the most comments, with activity dying down significantly during the period of Friday through Sunday. By hour, both in October of 2018 and across all time, 10am to 4pm is consistently the most popular comment posting time, with the majority of comments being posted between 10am and 2pm.


For the site as a whole, and closely tracking the trend I describe above, Monday through Thursday see the most traffic, though the exact order within this day-range varies by the period under examination.


Finally, a word on groups. Data for groups stretches back only to August of 2017 and in this short time span shows little patterning. What we can say, however, is that in October of this year users created four groups, while in October of last year this number was ten. Monthly, there are an average of about 76 users who take advantage of the groups function, with as few as 5 and as many as 182 using groups in any given month. And in total, through October of 2018, 1223 users have utilized this feature. Of these, however, 228 users belong to just five groups, the names of which suggest they are associated with courses. This assessment further suggests that while users are taking advantage of the groups feature, organizations, academic courses, or other interest groups are the primary users, with the general population both underutilizing groups in their usage and in their creation.


From here, I'll take in your comments and suggestions, in hopes of providing more, and more targeted, insights in the near future.
