2010 AERA, 4.30.10: Virtual Schooling Presentation

Ting Yuan

May 1, 2010

Hi everyone! I arrived at Denver last night and just had my first day at this year's AERA. The weather in Denver seems fast-changing. There was a bit snow, then a bit rain in the morning, before it turned very sunny in the afternoon. This is my second visit to this city and it is great to see its bright blue sky again! I'm staying at the Hyatt Tech Center which is about 12 miles from the Convention Center. Fortunately, the AERA bus arrives every 15 mins. I feel the transportation is very well organized by AERA! Our presentation (Stephen and I) session (Researching Virtual Learning Environments) was from 12:00 to 1:30. There were five presentations in total. Each got 10 minutes, followed by discussant's comments and Q&As from the audience. I was a bit surprised that the presentation room was full (over 25 people) and several late attendants even sat on the floor. Here are our presentation slides:

The U.S. K-12 Virtual Schooling on Prezi

The presentation went well. We didn't get any questions from the Q&A but the discussant (Prof. Craig A. Cunningham from National-Louis University) gave very helpful feedback. He liked our writing about historical introduction of distance learning programs in the U.S. and suggested all presenters to take a critical perspective to look at the definition of "virtual" by those K-12 online programs. He further suggested some potential next steps we could consider, for example, how and why those K-12 online programs make different emphasis in their mission statements/rationales, and we can explore more online demo/beta lessons offered by those online programs from 2009 to 2010. After the session, I had a short discussion with Prof. Cunningham. He told me about the book The Metaphysics of Virtual Reality by Michael R. Heim who identifies seven different concepts of Virtual Reality: simulation, interaction, artificiality, immersion, telepresence, full-body immersion, and network communication. Though the definition has certain futuristic romanticism attached. It offers some thoughts for what a virtual school should ideally look like. The presenters of the other four sessions were assistant professors from different schools. While the first presenter from North Carolina State University talked about his quantitative study on the subjects of math and foreign language, which showed significant learning results in a K-12 virtual school in North Carolina, all other studies explored virtual learning/design in higher education online learning contexts.
