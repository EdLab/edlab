Trends in Ed, 4.14.09

jvazquez

April 14, 2009

Why so serious? As a new Edutopia article reports, social justice game development (or, “serious” gaming) is on the rise. Here are some of the games highlighted: Peacemaker- The object of this game is to obtain Middle East peace and thus win. The catch is that the player must win twice- once as the Israeli prime minister and once as the Palestinian president. Operation: Climate Control (for teens) and Climate Challenge (for adults)- Presents the “difficulties” of climate change as “challenges.” In Climate Change, for example, the player acts as the president of Europe and must decrease carbon emissions while staying within budget and keeping voters happy. ICED (I Can End Deportation)- Players act as an immigrant teen trying to gain citizenship. Game play simulates the frustrations and terror that many young immigrant teens face in daily living. These games are being billed as “serious games” (as evidenced by the Serious Games Summit at the annual Game Developers Conference). Not the most attractive genre name, but I guess when your subject is social justice, you can't be too kitschy. Examples of game companies developing serious games are ImpactGames, Breakthrough, Games for Change, and Red Redemption. Although they are being used in the classroom, the article goes on to warn that games can be costly, in both time and money spent. Still it's a growing area of academic interest and with increasing mainstream attention, I'm looking forward to serious gaming companies obtaining additional funding and producing more quality products.
