EdLab Review: Project Noah

LauraCostello

August 26, 2011

Project Noah (stands for networked organisms and habitats) was dreamed up by NYU's Interactive Telecommunications Program to crowdsource species identification and act as a local guidebook to our plant and animal neighbors. Pros: The site seems to have real educational potential; it works well and the user base seems devoted to documenting and identification. Users can help out on “missions” like that of the Great Pollinator Project, which aims to identify and improve the habitat of pollinators in NYC or the Mitten Crab Watch's attempts to document the spread of this invasive species in the Hudson. It's easy to look at local flora and fauna or go exploring for exotic species and it would be easy to incorporate this in an ecological curriculum once it's more robustly filled with plants, bugs, fungi, and animals. Project Noah is actively seeking teachers and librarians to help them with this goal. Cons: There are only a few devoted users in our neighborhood as yet and still no rat identification! Sign up for the site must be bundled in to one of your existing networks with all the scary-sounding permissions that entails, but the offerings are pleasantly myriad (AOL?) A few of the comments allude to the bug-heaviness of the site, but the world is pretty bug-heavy. There are a couple mild design issues: the logo on the site is pretty bland and some of the identification buttons on the map could be more explicit. EdLab Relevance: I'd really like someone to identify the horrifying spider that lives right about where EdLab is identified on the map. I see it on my walk to work every morning and I'm pretty sure it's a baby tarantula or something. Project Noah that beast! Our Takeaway: Project Noah has potential to educate and build local communities around identification and preservation of plants and animals.
