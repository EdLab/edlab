Economics & Education: The So-Called Higher-Ed Bubble and Baumol's Disease

Skanda Amarnath

June 16, 2011

Inside Higher Ed has a great takedown of the promoters of the "Higher Ed is a bubble about to burst" hypothesis. As much as I have a huge problem with the existing higher ed system, it is no bubble; seems like guys like Thiel are confusing Baumol's cost disease for an asset bubble. More to the point, there will always be an insatiable demand for education, just like there is for healthcare. Education and healthcare are two areas of spending where a person would be willing to risk bankruptcy. One is a matter of life and death. The other is related to the prospect of permanently enjoying a better life. As other sectors of our economy become more productive, more surplus will inevitably be devoted to education, thus raising the price of education (at least that's my two-bit way of looking at it). Unfortunately, there have been few instances of productivity gains in the higher-ed sector in the past twenty years to help keep costs in check or raise the quality of education.
