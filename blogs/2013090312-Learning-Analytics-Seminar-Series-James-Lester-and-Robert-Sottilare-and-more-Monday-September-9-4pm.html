Monday, September 9, 2013 4pm-5:30pm Location: TBA (another email will go out) Teachers College, Columbia University James Lester, North Carolina State University Jonathan Rowe, North Carolina State University Bob Sottilare, Army Research Laboratory Keith Brawner, Army Research Laboratory --- Recognizing Students' Goals in Game-Based Learning Environments: A Markov Logic Approach James Lester, Jonathan Rowe

Abstract: There is growing evidence that game-based learning environments are effective learning tools across a broad range of domains. Rich virtual environments afforded by commercial game technologies offer opportunities for guided inquiry-based learning and problem-solving scenarios with multiple solution paths. However, dynamically supporting students' learning processes within a game-based learning environment requires computational models capable of recognizing and reasoning about students' goals and plans during learning. Goal recognition in game-based learning environments involves inferring students' goals from observed sequences of low-level actions performed by the student in the game environment, such as navigation, manipulating objects, and interacting with characters. Game-based learning environments pose significant challenges for goal recognition, such as inherent uncertainty, exploratory actions, and ill-defined goals. In this talk, we describe recent work devising a goal recognition framework for identifying students' problem-solving goals based on Markov logic networks (MLNs). The goal recognition model's parameters are directly learned from a corpus of student interactions with an open-ended game-based learning environment, Crystal Island. We describe the model's structure and present experimental results, in which the MLN-based goal recognition model was compared to several n-gram baseline models. The findings suggest the MLN goal recognition model yields significant accuracy gains beyond the n-gram models for predicting student goals in an open-ended game-based learning environment.

Abstract: A primary goal is for adaptive tutoring to provide guided learning experiences which are tailored for each individual without the need for a human tutor. This powerful technology is limited in its application by high development costs and limited adaptiveness to the needs of individual learners. This talk will address key issues to be resolved for adaptive tutoring technologies to be practical and affordable for use in education and training. Discussion will be narrated in the context of the adaptive tutoring learning effect chain which describes acquisition of learner data, modeling of learner states, selection of optimal instructional strategies and the influence of these processes on learning gains (e.g. performance) in computer-mediated tutoring experiences. Dr. Sottilare will also touch on processes for affordable authoring and assessment of Intelligent Tutoring System technologies.

Use of sensor-based data mining techniques to improve learner modeling: Keith Brawner
Abstract: Computer-based instructors, just like their human counterparts, should monitor the emotional and cognitive states of their students in order to adaptive instructional technique. Doing so requires a model of student state to be available at run time, but this has historically been difficult. Because people are different, generalized models have not been able to be validated. As a person's cognitive and affective state vary over time of day and seasonally, individualized models have had differing difficulties. The simultaneous creation and execution of an individualized model, in real time, represents the last option for modeling such cognitive and affective states. This talk presents and evaluates four differing techniques for the creation of cognitive and affective models that are created on-line and in real time for each individual user as alternatives to generalized models. Each of these techniques involves making predictions and modifications to the model in real time, addressing the real time datastream problems of infinite length, detection of new concepts, and responding to how concepts change over time. Additionally, with the knowledge that a user is physically present, this work investigates the contribution that the occasional direct user query can add to the overall quality of such models. The research described finds that the creation of a reasonable quality affective model is possible with an infinitesimal amount of time and without "ground truth" knowledge of the user, which is shown across three different emotional states. Creation of a cognitive model in the same fashion, however, was not possible via direct AI modeling, even with all of the "ground truth" information available, which is shown across four different cognitive states.
