The problems of federalism, dissemination of information, and electoral problems

dinosossi2

June 30, 2017



A recent report revealed the depths and extent of hacking in the most recent election.


It was worse than it was initially reported.


In fact, Bloomberg reported that there was interference in as many as 39 states (!!!), involving attacks on the registration of voters as well as campaign finance and other types of election databases.


So why wasnâ€™t this reported earlier?




In some part, it was a result of tensions between federal and state governments.


Federalism is incredibly useful in large and diverse countries like the United States, India, or Canada. It helps to devolve certain powers that are not enumerated (e.g., listed) in the constitution to give states and provinces more local control. Think education. As a result, through successful negotiations and goodwill, federal standards can apply over certain issues with broad application (e.g., criminal law, international trade) while state and provincial jurisdiction can exercise judgment on issues of regional concern like educational curriculum.


However, when information from federal investigations to states are delayed with respect to issues like elections, important information that protects our democracy can be compromised.


Ideally, there will be more transparent communication with respect to issues like electoral interference, which has recently become a global phenomenon.


Our respective democracies depend on it.
