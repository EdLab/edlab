Trends in Ed, 12.22.09: E-book Readers

Ting Yuan

December 22, 2009

A Video Review from The Wall Street Journal: Kindle vs. Nook Walt Mossberg is the author and creator of the weekly Personal Technology column in The Wall Street Journal, which has appeared every Thursday since 1991. Recently, he gave a video review of Nook, a newly released e-book reader by Barnes & Noble. In the review, he compared the main features of Nook and Kindle: Some highlights of Walt Mossberg's review include:

  • The features of Nook that stand out are a touch screen and a lending function that enables Nook owners to lend books to others for two weeks.
  • The quality of book collection in Nook is not as good as Kindle.
  • The browsing speed in Nook is not as satisfactory as Kindle.
  • Overall, Nook is promising and interesting, however, I recommend you to wait for a while, until it gets more developed.
I think the touch screen and the lending function of Nook sound very interesting. Any individual Nook owner can lend a book to others: you can select a book from your collection and lend it to someone listed in your Nook contacts, and then they receive an email message on their Nook "main page"; when they accept, they can read the book for two weeks; at the end of the two weeks, the system will deal with the automatically-returned books. This function brings great convenience to the future book borrowing and lending among individuals, which also means a big change to libraries. So the future informal learning tends to happen in a more collaborative and efficient way. Furthermore, in future libraries owning such devices, if the system can be intelligent enough, the borrowing and lending services can be taken care of mostly by the system.
