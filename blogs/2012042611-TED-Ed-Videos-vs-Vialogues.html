TED-Ed Videos vs Vialogues

Kate Meersschaert

April 26, 2012

TED-Ed Launches in BETA: As JoAnn reported here, TED announced the addition of a dedicated "Ed-focused" site about a month ago. Now, the site has officially launched in beta and is ready for educators and anyone interested in new online learning opportunities to explore (check-out the tutorial embedded below). Review of the Details: The final version of the site allows educators who are nominated by their peers to record lessons using "an audio recording booth" that the TED-Ed team provides, to capture their "best" ten-minute lesson. The audio is then paired with an animator (also peer nominated) and an animated version is uploaded to the TED-Ed site. Educators and learners can then search for these videos by either "subject" or thematically by "series." The "Flip:" Additionally and perhaps most compellingly, the TED-Ed site provides the option to "flip" videos. Users can select from existent TED-created animated video or those uploaded from YouTube to customize their "flip" with supplemental elements like multiple-choice quizzes, short questions and additional text-based reinforcement material. "Flips" are then searchable by any user in the TED-Ed video library. Additionally, anyone who flips a video is then given a unique URL to share with each learner, this URL allows the behavior of each interaction with the flipped content to be tracked and shared. How is Vialogues Different? One element that TED-Ed misses and Vialogues (Video + Discussion = Vialogues) really excels at is easy-to-use, beautifully integrated annotation. These time-stamped comments are displayed to the right of the vialogue; thereby creating a 1:1 ratio of video content to comments. TED-Ed videos, as on YouTube, display user comments with static time stamps at the bottom of the page (unless they are quiz questions). Additionally, Vialogues offers the ability to easily embed a vialogue on any site, share a secure, private link to vialogue and create, track and record quizzes and polls. Users are notified when comments have been made in their vialogue and a daily digest is sent-out that lists these comments. Finally, Vialogues provides an easy way to upload video in (almost!) every format and to easily create a vialogue using a YouTube link. Our Takeaway While the TED-Ed project is noble, animated lessons are beautiful and the method of capture is revolutionary (though expensive!), Vialogues still excels as a just-in-time solution for sharing, annotating and embedding asynchronous video content for the flipped classroom.
