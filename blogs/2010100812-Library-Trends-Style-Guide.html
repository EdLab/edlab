Library Trends Style Guide

Julia Martin

October 8, 2010

You're at the desk. It's not easy, we know. But in between the emotional peaks and valleys that come with providing Service, we're asking you to learn about the shifting work of libraries. And create a bridge. Yes, a bridge. A bridge between the library and TC! And, pardon the expanding metaphor, but here's the roadmap: You're on the Service Team, and you already know a lot about TC: your work, your program, and the people around you every day. But to build this bridge we're going to need you to learn more about libraries. Specifically, we're asking you to join the Pressible publishing effort as an explorer of library websites. Which means we're asking you to write (and share publicly) posts about interesting stuff. This could include posts on more traditional aspects of libraries (books, resources, research methods, etc.), current events, new services, or other things going on in the Library and Information Science field. And, for bonus points, relate it to the work of real people and projects at TC! The Blog Roll Below find a list of blogs (a "blog roll," if you will) to get your started. If you discover a blog, article, organization, or something interesting you think might be relevant, do not hesitate to write about it!

  • Lorcan Dempsey’s Weblog: http://orweblog.oclc.org/
  • American Libraries Magazine: http://americanlibrariesmagazine.org/
  • ACRL: http://acrlblog.org/
  • The Sheck: http://thesheckspot.blogspot.com/
  • LISNews: The Cream of the Crop: http://lisnews.org/
  • iLibrarian: http://oedb.org/blogs/ilibrarian/
A Word About Style When writing your post about an article or post or whole website, it should look something like this: Post Title: a brief, fun, catchy title <body> and include at the end, if applicable, the work referenced: Author, date, title of article, title of site/blog, full URL<link to the original post> Select the "From the Blog Roll" topic in the Categories list Add at least five tags for your post, including peoples' names (who may figure into your post's content). Go ahead and publish it when you think it's ready to go live. If you need to make changes later, feel free to update your posts, but note that it's likely to get it's most reads when its first published. Good luck!
