"All The Irrelevance That Fits The Pixels"

Gus Andrews

April 14, 2009

A colleague in my Changing Nature of Public Controversies class over at the J School posted this link to an analysis of user experience at the New York Times webpag. Very interesting stuff, pointing out that among other things: * Only 23% of a given NYT page "above the fold" (i.e. seen without your having to scroll down) is dedicated to content relevant to the story you went to the page for; * Only 25% of the words above the fold are dedicated to relevant content; * Pages in their historical archive do not link to subsequent content on the topic you searched for. A lot of this seems a little obvious. Of course web pages are filled with ads; how else can we pay for content? Haven't newspapers always had a very small "news hole" compared to the ads they have to print to stay afloat? Don't pages need interface elements to help you find other parts of the site? But then, what if we could do things differently? How will this look in ten years?
