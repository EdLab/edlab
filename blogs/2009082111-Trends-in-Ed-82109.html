In the spotlight (for once): Civics Education

"Don't call it 'civics' because 'civics' is easily the most boring word in America," Dreyfuss says. "Call it what it is: political power."
And so begins our introduction to actor Richard Dreyfuss's crusade to garner civics education the attention it deserves. With his newest project, he has set upon creating a national curriculum in civics education, which is being called The Dreyfuss Initiative. It will make use of scholarly presentations and Dreyfuss himself as a storyteller in an effort to empower and enlighten students while entertaining them. To quote from the article:
"I stopped defining myself as an actor and I went to Oxford because I believe that America is a miracle," Dreyfuss said. "And I think that there is nothing easier in the world than for us to lose this miracle and to be reduced to words on paper.”
While his goal for this project is to enhance the teaching and learning of civics, his ultimate goal is much loftier. Speaking candidly, he says, "Not to be glib about it, because nothing about this is glib, but I'm going for the Nobel.” Retired Supreme Court Justice Sandra Day O'Connor has a similar passion for the teaching of civics, which drove her to create http://www.ourcourts.org. She, like others, feels that No Child Left Behind is responsible for taking the significance of civics away from the curriculum. I say hats off to both of these fine citizens.
