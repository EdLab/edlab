Time for Change?

Khalil Abubakar

July 9, 2012

FIFA, soccer's governing body, recently agreed to implement goal line technology after years of refusing to do so. This new technology is meant to accurately decide whether a ball crosses the goal line. High profile incidents at the past several major tournaments led to an outpouring of support for goal line technology. FIFA's initial response to the outcry was to include two extra referees to the usual three but that did little to quell the discontent among fans and pundits. The new technology is a Hawk Eye System, based on the principle of triangulation, which uses visual images and timing data provided by high-speed video cameras at different locations around the area of play. The system uses six, spread out, high-speed cameras to triangulate and track the ball in flight. Additionally, another new technology, GoalRef, features a microchip in the ball and low magnetic waves around the goal. Any change in the field on or behind the goal line determines the scoring of a goal. I prefer the GoalRef because it's easy to install and cheap, therefore making it easier for smaller and poorer countries and clubs to install. Hopefully FIFA will approve the new system in time for the 2012-13 season. Soccer is one of the few remaining major sports where there is no use of technology to determine contested plays. Most American sports use instant replay while the aforementioned Hawk Eye System is used in cricket and tennis.
