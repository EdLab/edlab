How Learners Build Social Capital

DebraLee

March 25, 2018


The capacity of Massive Open Online Courses (MOOCs) to deliver a socially interactive learning environment is at the heart of ongoing debate around the pedagogical effectiveness of the platform. MOOCs rely on the connectivist model of learning, where learners form connections based on shared interests, and learning outcomes are not pre-defined by a facilitator. Making connections in MOOCs is critical to learner success, and yet little is known about how learners build social capital (investment in social relations for future return) in open large-scale courses.

In this study, researchers used two connectivist MOOCs to investigate how learners leverage access to social capital, and the factors associated with a successful learning experience. The goal of the study was to understand the influence of language, media, time, and learners' activeness in the development of social capital in a connectivist MOOC.

The results showed Twitter and Facebook provided better opportunities for building connections with peers in MOOCs, but Facebook and blogs were better at reaching the more influential learners within the network. Twitter enabled the best and speediest information outreach. The results also revealed that learner-contingent factors such as discussion, and pedagogy-contingent factors such as media or time in the course, impact individual development of social capital.

The authors argue that facilitators should extend network formation beyond the shaping and leveraging of information flows throughout the learning network, and facilitators also need to assist individual learners in choosing best-fit media to facilitate interactions and introduce instructional elements that enhance group and individual communication skills.

Joksimovića, S., Dowellb, N., Poqueta, O., Kovanovića, V., Gaševićc, D., Dawsona, S., & Graesserd, A. C. (2017). Exploring development of social capital in a CMOOC through language and discourse. The Internet and Higher Education, (36), 54-64.
