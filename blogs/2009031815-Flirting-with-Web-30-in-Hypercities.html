Flirting with Web 3.0 in Hypercities

Doug Beacom

March 18, 2009

I've been going on about google maps and collaborative map-building as long as I've been in the EdLab--you've probably heard me going on about my MA project: NYCLL Mapping Public Text.

So imagine the bittersweet surprise I felt when a Linguistic Landscape contact of mine at Berkeley told me about: Hypercities.

This is a MacArthur-funded project that grew out of an idea at Stanford, but has stakeholders across the world, including academic institutions in Los Angeles, Tel Aviv, Tokyo and here in NYC. What I'm most interested in is their declaration that Web 3.0 will be all about collaborative, user-submitted media, and encourage you to contribute to the map with their (albeit clunky) interface. Also, they have worked with the google maps code in an interesting way and managed to let the viewer change the temporal view as well. So you can look at NYC a la Gangs of New York or 2040. Confusing but fun. to quote one of their explanation slides (re: why a map, why this format?):

"Why? Because humans do not organize their lives by keywords." I've only looked at the NYC portion of their working page. I'm not sure if there is a place for my public text data like this: but I guess if I'm free to add media, that is up to me.
