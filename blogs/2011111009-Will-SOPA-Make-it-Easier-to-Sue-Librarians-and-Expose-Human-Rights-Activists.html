Will SOPA Make it Easier to Sue Librarians? (& Expose Human Rights Activists?)

Kate Meersschaert

November 10, 2011

Will the new bill, Stop Online Piracy Act (H.R. 3261) or SOPA, make it easier for librarians to be sued for copyright infringement? According to this recent issue of InsideHigherEd.com, this could be the case. In an open letter the Library Copyright Alliance (LCA) outlines the dangers of this bill for librarians and higher ed libraries. The LCA is a group consisting of three main associations including the American Library Association, the Association of Research Libraries, and the Association of College and Research Libraries. Some of the dangers outlined in the letter and explored more fully in the Insidehighered.com article listed above, include conflating “fair use” exemptions with “willful infringement,” which would carry criminal penalties. Brandon Butler, the author of the LCA letter explains to InsideHigherEd.com, “The willful infringement level would swallow the ordinary infringement level, thereby significantly broadening the range of activities subject to criminal sanctions.” What are your thoughts on these dangers? Are they overblown? Who would benefit from this legislation? What are other examples of copyright laws hurting non-profits?
