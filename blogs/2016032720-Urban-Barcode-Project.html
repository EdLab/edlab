Urban Barcode Project

EdLab Studios

March 27, 2016


In 2012, Mary Achaempong, Marisa VanBrakle, and Bobby Glover used DNA barcoding to reveal that ginko capsules sold at GNC, Walmart, Walgreens, and Target contained no ginko DNA, only rice. Their conclusions were later supported by the New York State Attorney General resulting in a cease and desist order against selling fraudulent supplements. But they werenâ€™t scientists. These were three high school students participating in the Urban Barcode Project competition. Spanning the five boroughs, the Urban Barcode Project is one of three separate DNA barcoding programs hosted by the DNA Learning Center. Led by a qualified teacher mentor, small student teams propose a project that explores the environment or detects food fraud or medicinal mislabeling before collecting samples from parks, rivers, grocery stores, pharmacies, and everything in between. At Open Lab days at the Harlem DNA Lab, participants receive training and access to equipment in order to follow through on their investigation. Through extracting and amplifying DNA as well as analyzing sequence data at the bench, students are not only able to explore biodiversity in NYC but also to question it with evidence that makes a real contribution to science.
