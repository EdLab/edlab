How To Make Invisible Science Visible

Jihii Jolly

November 2, 2014


Augmented Reality for Interpretive and Experiential Learning (A.R.I.E.L.) is a project of The Franklin Institute, one of the nation’s oldest centers of science education, named for America’s first scientist, Benjamin Franklin. Its mission is to inspire a passion for learning about science and technology through exciting and unique exhibits. To this end, the A.R.I.E.L. project conducts research through the design and implementation of visualization techniques to enhance visitor interactions with traditional hands-on exhibits—say for example, by using augmented reality to help viewers see how force fields look when they manipulate a set of magnets with their hands. The project’s ultimate goal is to reinvent the exhibit experience to keep up with changing 21st century learning expectations and inspire similar change in informal science education across institutions.

This video, filmed in April 2014, features interviews with Karen Elinich, Principal Investigator at A.R.I.E.L, and Jayatri Das, Chief Bio Scientist, who explain the goal of the project, define augmented reality and the various forms it can take, and walk us through how their two latest exhibits work. We also see young museum visitors interact with each of the devices; and the excitement they feel seeing traditionally "invisible" aspects of science become visible, is just as apparent as the invisible phenomena itself.
