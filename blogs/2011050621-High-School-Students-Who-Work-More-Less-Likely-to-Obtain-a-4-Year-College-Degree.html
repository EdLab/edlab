High School Students Who Work More Less Likely to Obtain a 4 Year College Degree

Duncan Asiedu

May 6, 2011

Researchers from the University of Michigan recently released findings from a decades long study which links the amount of working hours completed by a high school student to their likelihood of completing college. The study concluded that students who work less than 15 hours a week are far more likely to obtain a bachelor's degree than those who exceed that figure. Such results were obtained from data first collected in 1976 as part of the Monitoring the Future Project which annually surveys high school seniors across the country. Of the over 65,000 students that were surveyed, 58% of those who reported working less than 15 hours a week had acquired a college degree by the age of 30. On the contrary though, for every additional five hours that a student worked, their likelihood of finishing college diminished by 8%. Various reasons can be attributed to such a direct correlation. Students who work more than 15 hours generally boast sub-par grades and thus see work as a gateway from school as well as a chance to earn money early. Another factor that may contribute to such a statistic is the shear workload that a part time job puts on students. Simultaneously holding a job while participating in sports or other extracurricular activities can prove to be too strenuous for some students. Their desire to continue on with their education as a result diminishes which decreases their likelihood of completing college. With this said, parents are advised to monitor the academic status of their child after they've obtain a part time job to insure that they don't lag behind. If this is adhered to and the amount of working hours are kept at a minimum, parents are expect to see their child benefit from the positive results that a part time job can carry.
