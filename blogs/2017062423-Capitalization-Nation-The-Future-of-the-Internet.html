Capitalization Nation: The Future of the [I]nternet

dinosossi2

June 24, 2017

A rose by any other name would smell as sweet. ~ Romeo and Juliet


The U.S. Supreme Court ended up inadvertently debating an issue that may sound pedantic, infantile, superfluous, and, well, even anal to all but you most committed of grammarphiles.


In Framingham v. North Carolina, five of the justices capitalized Internet while the remaining four spelled it in lower case as simple internet.


So?


To summarize the competing arguments, the capitalized term Internet denotes that is a singular and specific place. In contrast, lower case internet implies that it is instead simply a generic tool regular people use.


Again, so what’s the hubbub, bub?


In short, if the Internet is an all-encompassing place, it becomes the digital equivalent of a public square. As a result, it receives a certain amount of legal protection similar in notion to a physical space or geographic place.


In contrast, a neutered lower case internet can be parsed, blocked, and restricted according to legal whims, whatever happens to be the sense of the day. Given our increasingly commercial world, it looks to be divided and sold, split up and auctioned off. The broad expanse of the Internet becomes much smaller, circumscribed and more trivial. In essence, it is a less place-based space and, as a result, generic. It no longer has the importance that capitalization connotes.


As further jurisprudence inevitably comes down the legal pipe, we’ll see how this all turns out. Just be prepared for an increasingly commercialized and limited space. If you want access, you'll have to pay. Goodbye free Internet, hello to the smaller, but more costly, internet.
