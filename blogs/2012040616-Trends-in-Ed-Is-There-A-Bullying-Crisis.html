Trends in Ed: Is There A Bullying Crisis?

Fred Rossoff

April 6, 2012

In response to growing media attention on student bullying, including the recent release of the documentary “Bully,” Nick Gillespie, editor of the libertarian magazine Reason, published an essay in the Wall Street Journal last weekend arguing that “our fears about the ubiquity of bullying are just the latest in a long line of well-intentioned yet hyperbolic alarms about how awful it is to be a kid today.” While freely admitting the dangers of bullying, Gillespie argues that the current anti-bullying crusade is part of a larger trend towards “paternalism run amok.” That is, our concern over bullying seems to have grown at precisely the time when actual violence in schools has been dropping. Of course, it's much easier to track violence than the more subtle effects of long-term bullying itself, but he argues that the numbers on that score are equivocal enough to give us pause. With the rise of social networks, more and more of student behavior is migrating online, where it can be recorded and analyzed in a more rigorous fashion. Regardless of the current severity of “bullying” in our schools, such a trend should provide us with better tools for analyzing and ameliorating this and other social issues in our schools.
