The New Seven Wonders of the World

Paola Uriarte

July 16, 2007

As a contemporary alternative to the now famous historical list of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World , the New Wonders idea was developed by the Swiss-based New Open World Corporation ( NOWC ). The NOWC nominated a number of global monuments through their website and began the voting process through the internet and text messages. With the slogan “Our Heritage is Our Future,” the popularity of the contest grew with nominated countries voting furiously for their own. On July 7, 2007 (07.07.07, how clever) the new winning seven were announced in Lisbon, Portugal in a ceremony with celebrities from around the world. Personally, my favorite contender was Machu Picchu. I mean, just look at it .That may, of course, be a biased opinion. But nevertheless each site has a wonder of its own. Let's break them down.

The Great Wall of China: Built around the 5th Century BC to keep invading Mongol tribes from China, the wall is the largest man-made structure, stretching over 6,400 km. It is even visible from space! Renovated through many years, it was the Ming Dynasty that rebuilt it with bricks and brought it to its current size. View picture here .

Petra, Jordan: This archaeological site lying on the Arabian Desert in Jordan was once the capital of the Nabateans, an ancient people of Southern Jordan who were experts in creating aqueducts within the desert city which in turn formed an oasis. The water reserves could therefore be employed during the severe droughts which affect the area. The city prospered tremendously in this way. In general, the site demonstrates the impressive value of Middle Eastern culture. (Of course visiting Petra might be a bit dangerous…). View picture here .

The Christ Redeemer Statue in Brazil: Yes, it's Jesus. But how cool does he look just standing randomly there in Rio de Janeiro? Thirty-eight meters (that's over 100 feet) in height and weighing in at 700 tons, the Christ Redeemer is located at the peak of the Corcovado mountain some 2,000 feet above the city. With its construction overseen by Heitor da Silva Costa and its design by Paul Landowski, the monument was opened in 1931. Its appeal lies far from Catholicism and more within the scenery surrounding it. The true winner is Rio de Janeiro. View picture here .

Machu Picchu in Peru: Now you KNOW this is the best one because obviously the Incas built this hidden city in the Andes and damn straight it's still around. Also, the Incas were ONLY the best ancient civilization of the Americas. Okay, enough favoritism. Here's the deal. The Taj Mahal in India Located in Agra, this mausoleum was commissioned by Mugal Emperor Shah Jahan in 1632. It is often considered the finest example of Muslim architecture. Built from white marble, the structure is highlighted by its vast garden. It is a symbol of Muslim culture and passion. The splendor of this Taj Mahal inspired Donald Trump to build his hotel aptly named Trump Taj Mahal in Atlantic City, New Jersey. View picture of the real thing here .

The Roman Colosseum in Italy Of course the Romans had to get in on this too. The Colosseum is located in Rome and was used as a giant amphitheatre for Gladiator contests and spectacles. Built around 70AD, the Colosseum was in use for nearly 500 years. It is a staple of the Roman civilization and its ruins have been preserved as best as possible since. Today, the Colosseum still stands as a symbol of Imperial Rome and the importance of Italy in history. View picture here .

The New Seven Wonders of the World are a mark of pride for the countries represented and will serve as excellent symbols of great ancient civilizations and architecture.
