Libraries Help Americans Deal With Learning Pressures

DebraLee

November 20, 2017


Americans have a complex relationship with learning. According to a new survey, our enthusiasm is predicated on two things: the level of trust in information sources and our interest in learning, particularly about digital skills. In short, if we trust the information source, we’re eager to learn, but if we don’t our interest wanes. Concern about the validity of sources conflicts with enthusiasm for learning.

The Pew Center survey explored five broad dimensions of engagement with information during telephone calls with 3,015 adults, 18 years or older, living in all 50 U.S. states and the District of Columbia. They combined respondent views and created an "information-engagement typology" containing five groups that fall along a spectrum ranging from fairly high engagement with information to wariness of it.

Roughly four in ten adults (38%) expressed relatively strong interest and trust in information sources and learning; about half (49%) were relatively disengaged and not very enthusiastic about information or about gaining more training, especially when it comes to navigating digital information; and 13% occupied a middle space: They were not particularly trusting of information sources, but showed higher interest in learning than those in the more information-wary groups.

The findings complement existing data on how Americans think about the role of libraries, particularly in helping to meet information needs in communities, like digital skills training for older adults and kids, and offering programs that teach about online privacy and security. Overall, about half (52%) of respondents visited or connected online with a public library in the past year, and two of the five groups were more likely than others to say they trust librarians and libraries as information sources.

Horrigan, J. B. (2017). How people approach facts and information. Pew Research Center. Survey, August, 2016.
