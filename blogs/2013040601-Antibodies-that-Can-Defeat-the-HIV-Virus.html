Antibodies that Can Defeat the HIV Virus?

Khalil Abubakar

April 6, 2013

When the HIV virus enters the body, the immune system develops ways to destroy the virus. Unfortunately the immune system is overwhelmed by the HIV virus due to multiple mutations before our systems can create a super weapon against the virus. Some immune systems can develop this super weapon called “broadly neutralizing antibodies” a few years after the initial infection, which some doctors believe could be key to defeating the virus. A closely monitored man was infected by the HIV virus and a few years later he produced this broadly neutralizing antibodies. Antibodies are Y shaped proteins that attach to the virus receptors it uses to bind to cells, but the broadly neutralizing antibodies defused 55% of all known HIV strains. According to this article, scientists have found dozens of the broadly neutralizing antibodies and a cocktail of these antibodies may eventually be able to take down all known HIV virus strains. An option is to spur a person's immune system to create the broadly neutralizing antibodies and wait for potential infection. Naturally occurring production of broadly neutralizing antibodies occurs only after the cells that produce them have gone through about 100 mutations. An effective vaccine would require a series of monthly shots that would accelerate the pace of these mutations.
