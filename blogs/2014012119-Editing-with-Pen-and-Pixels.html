Editing with Pen and Pixels

Fred Rossoff

January 21, 2014


Title: The Effect of Format on Performance: Editing Text in Print Versus Digital Formats

Authors: Sigal Eden & Yoram Eshet-Alkalai

Source: British Journal of Educational Technology

Study Design: As more reading and writing takes place in the digital world, it becomes more important to investigate what this digital format does to and with the reader. This study used a sample of 93 undergraduate students, each given two short papers to correct, one with pen and paper, one with Microsoft Word, to determine whether using a computer had any effect on their editorial accuracy.

Findings: The authors of this study found no significant difference in the accuracy of editorial comments between the two conditions, indicating that it there is not much difference between using a red pen or Wordâ€™s "tracked changes." On the other hand, the subjects more often completed the digital editorial task before the 20 minute time limit, indicating that, while perhaps no more helpful in terms of spotting errors, using a computer for proof-reading and copy-editing is more convenient and time-efficient.

Moving Forward: This study is small enough, in terms of the sample size and the conditions examined, that it should not be viewed as anything more than preliminary, particularly when previous research has reported myriad differences in digital versus print interaction. But the conclusion seems plausible. Autocorrect and ease in erasing and rewriting can only take an editor so far. The real game changer will be editorial computer programs that handle all of these corrections without error or human supervision.
