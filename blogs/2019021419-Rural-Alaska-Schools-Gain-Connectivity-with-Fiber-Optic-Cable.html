Rural Alaska Schools Gain Connectivity with Fiber-Optic Cable

saraehardman

February 14, 2019


As of 2015, over 90% of Alaska’s K-12 schools had internet speeds lower than the benchmark set by the Federal Communications Commission for in-school wifi. Connectivity was even lower in poor districts. In fact, the average speed of school connectivity across the state was 246 kbps (kilobits per second), which is one third of the connection it takes to stream a show on Netflix. With so many rural districts, some only reachable by plane or boat, it has been an enormous challenge to bring reliable internet access to all of Alaska’s schools.

In 2012, Anchorage-based company Quintillion pledged to increase connectivity by building a network of underwater fiber-optic cables that could reach some of Alaska’s most remote districts, such as Nome, perched on the Bering Sea. In 2017, the cable was finally complete. Teachers hope that this increased connectivity can bring distance education and testing to their districts. In rural areas, schools could greatly benefit from online education, which would allow for many more learning opportunities than students might otherwise receive, such as computer science and coding.
