"Digital Dangers," Dubious Coverage, and James Lipton

Molly Riordan

December 10, 2009

This morning's broadcast of The Takeaway featured a segment on "digital dangers" facing teens. The segment cited a survey from MTV and the AP in which almost half of teens said they had been involved in "sexting"--sending or receiving risque photos of each other on their phones. Another guest talked about the extreme effects of cyberbullying, including one case where a girl was forced to move to a new school to escape the "bullies." I had a few qualms with the report: first, the data from the various surveys was vague and conflicting--as with any survey, what is the chance that a participant will report their actions accurately? This, I think, might be even more true for teens, who are likely aware that what they're doing is inappropriate and wouldn't want to admit it to an adult, even a "researcher" from MTV. My other problem with the report was the report itself: who's listening to The Takeaway at 6:45 a.m.? Probably a lot of adults, who are more likely to believe that technology--whether it be the Internet, video games, or (now) camera-phones--are corrupting the younger generation. I was reminded of The Media Show's excellent episode on online predators and the disparity between how teens perceive threats to themselves and adult reactions to these reported threats. I think a particularly excellent take on phone-related "threats" like sexting and cyberbullying are the PSAs featuring James Lipton and his beard, sponsored by LG. The website, giveitaponder.com, asks teens to literally wear Lipton's beard and consider the consequence of their actions. Useful advice given with tongue firmly planted in Lipton's cheek that doesn't pander to teens, from a trusted voice in adult-news-entertainment. Now that's an episode I can get behind.
