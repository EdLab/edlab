Trends in Ed: A Good Compromise...

Fred Rossoff

February 17, 2012

The New York Times' popular “Schoolbook” blog reports that last night, after an all-night negotiating session and cutting it very close to the deadline, the New York State teachers union and education officials reached a compromise for a new statewide teacher evaluation system. The rush was, of course, so that the state could implement a teacher evaluation system before losing millions of dollars in federal money as part of the Race to the Top program. The conflict was largely over how much weight would be given to student performance on standardized tests. The final result is that only 20% of a teacher's evaluation is required to be tied to student test score growth, with the rest to include principal observations and other factors that leave room for more discretion on the part of the school or district. Another big issue addressed was how to handle dismissal of inadequate teachers; specifically, what sort of appeals process should be made available to teachers facing the axe. Under our current regime “the city has the burden of proof,” while under the new deal, an independent panel of observers will make the final decision. So the armies of reform march on. Where, exactly, their technocratic tinkering will finally end up is another question.
