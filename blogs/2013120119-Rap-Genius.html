Rap Genius

LauraCostello

December 1, 2013


Rap Genius began as a community for annotating rap lyrics, but has grown both in audience and in scope. The site, which now boasts over 24.3 million unique views per month, now includes news and poetry sections that extend the annotation action to breaking journalism and classic literature. The site and its founders are not without controversy (1, 2, 3), but the tool is solid, central, and rapidly growing.

Pros:

With fresh funding and new content, Rap Genius has shifted its focus to education and initial campaigns have shown extraordinary adaptability to classroom needs. The company hired Jeremy Dean, a former teacher, to head up educational outreach and the tool was recently featured in an MIT course on Romantic poetry. This course put the annotating power in the hands of the students, with instructors acting as moderators on the site. Rap Genius’s advantage over traditional marginalia is the ability to include the image, gif, and video media that is the vernacular of many born-digital students. This hyper-specific commenting adds a layer of meaning (e.g., London Cat Club – Best of Variety, 2013 Show) and the click-based reveal of the annotations is addictive.

Cons:

Rap Genius is user driven and saturated in popular culture, including a fair amount of NSFS content that may give some educators pause, but the highly collaborative structure of the annotations and a growing user base will lead to better suggestions and the moderating influence of the crowd.

Our Takeaway:

Students are spending their own time annotating on Rap Genius because of its functionality, hip community, and competitive points system. The site’s user driven ethos feels like a good fit for individualized or blended education and may let educators integrate the mashup creativity of the internet with a very low barrier to entry. Rap Genius’s content may make it a better fit for higher education at this point, but the site’s rapid expansion and response to educator feedback may soon make it a central tool for forward-thinking literature and media instructors at all levels.

The Bottom Line:

Rap Genius is so ahead of its time, its founders haven’t met yet.
