Cultural Technology Development Lab of New Mexico Visits EdLab

veronicablack

March 24, 2017

This week the Design Team hosted an afternoon with the Cultural Technology Development Lab from New Mexico. Jonathan Lee and Rianne Trujillo are educators and developers for CTDL at The New Mexico Museum of Natural History & Science in Albuquerque, New Mexico. They work with museums and cultural institutions throughout New Mexico and the United States, through a partnership with the Department of Cultural Affairs of NM and New Mexico Highlands University (NMHU) Media Arts program in Las Vegas. NMHU is my Alma Mater and where Rianne and I graduated with BFAs in Media Arts, and Jonathan and I worked on internship projects together.

They talked about their work using open source hardware they developed for large scale installation and introduced us to the Museduino.

Using the Museduino they brought to life the miniature model train for the Little Carlsbad exhibit at the Carlsbad Museum and Art Center.




The CTDL developed and installed a RFID tagging bird calling system at the the Acadia National Park in Maine. Using RFID tags and images of native birds in Maine visitors are invited to match the eggs to the right bird and learn about the different sounds they make and the environment they live in.



Rianne worked on developing a large scale interactive table at the Anderson-Abruzzo Albuquerque International Balloon Museum.


This interactive uses archived journals to learn about the failed attempt to float to the North Pole in a hot air balloon and the history of hot air balloons. When she’s not working on interactive projects she’s developing app with bluetooth beacons for museums in Roswell.

Jonathan focuses most of his time teaching NMHU Computer Science and Media Art students coding languages from JavaScript to C# Programming. His students also work with Rianne and museums to design and build iPads apps that use 3D objects with sensors to learn about the New Mexico Pueblos.


He also work with micro-controllers such as Raspberry Pi and wifi antenna to help local conferences and festivals gather data about their events.


The CTDL program is a rich community of students and educators that are invested in preserving the vast and wonderful culture of New Mexico through the support of technology. Rianne and Jonathan’s work supports students as they dive into the past and carry it into the future. It was inspiring to see all the amazing work my former program has been up to and to make a link between our creative work here at EdLab. I hope to see more of their work in the future.
