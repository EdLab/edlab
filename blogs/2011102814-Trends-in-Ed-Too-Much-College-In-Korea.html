Trends in Ed: Too Much College In Korea

Fred Rossoff

October 28, 2011

Continuing my world tour of credentialization, I was surprised to read in the Washington Post that South Korea is a world leader in college graduates. Fred Hiatt reports “more than 60 percent of Koreans ages 25 to 34 have higher educations, compare with about 40 percent in the United States…” Since that's a somewhat lagging indicator, maybe it will have more effect if I tell you that currently about 80 per cent of Korean high school students go on to some kind of higher education institution. It seems that they've gone so far that they're actually thinking about reducing college education. The President, Lee Myung-Bak, has announced a new campaign to support the hiring of non-college graduates, including subsidies and tax benefits for companies that get on board. Leaving aside how effective these reforms will be (like the ban on too-much studying, it seems to focus more on effect than cause), is this shift in official policy (when compared, for instance, to our government's commitment to sending more people to college) in our future as well, or will we figure out a different solution?
