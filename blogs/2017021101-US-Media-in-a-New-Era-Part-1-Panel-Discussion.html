U.S. Media in a New Era - Part 1 - Panel Discussion

dinosossi2

February 11, 2017

The following are notes from a discussion on media challenges in a new era. Panelists include Katrina vanden Heuvel (KV), Editor and Publisher of The Nation.



U.S. Media in the New Era

Date: Tuesday February 7, 6-8pm, IAB 1512 

420 West 118th Street, 15th floor


Overview:

What are the rights and responsibilities of the media in reporting the new presidency? Days after the inauguration, we will assess whether there have been fundamental changes in the relationship between the administration and the media and how the media needs to respond in order to effectively fulfil the missions of watchdog and agenda setter.


Panelists: 

Katrina vanden Heuvel (KV)

Editor and Publisher of The Nation


Karen Attiah (KA)

Global Opinions Editor of the Washington Post


Marcus Mrowka (MM)

Assistant to the President for Strategic Communications at the American Federation of Teachers


Moderator: 

Anya Schiffrin (AS)

Professor, Columbia’s School of International and Public Affairs 

Director, Technology, Media, and Communications Specialization 


Introduction:

Katrina vanden Heuvel (KV):

-there is currently an administration governing by lies and fear mongering

-we saw this type of governance back in the 1960s

-the media needs to expose these types of abuses

-we are in a different place today than we were back in the 60s…

-too polite to say we are feeling disdain with attempts to try to delegitimize the media

-40% of newspapers have gutted their newsrooms

-Why? Many newsrooms did better financially with paid ads than subscriptions

-the phenomena of cable television has led to “media malpractice”

-news and entertainment have become intertwined as never before

-there is a tendency to sensationalize the news

-for example, the Tindle report that there was 36 minutes in total of issue coverage in the election (!!!)

-this is a combination of the 3 main stations (e.g., ABC, CBS, NBC)

-media mergers and acquistions have led to a conglomeratization

-from this, a hollowing out of the news has developed

-there is a new FCC commissioner

-there is the potential for an end to net neutrality

-as a result, broadband will no longer be considered as a utility

-therefore, from this, we in the media have stunned by speed and cruelty of the administration over the past two weeks…

(e.g., executive orders)


-but there are some glimmers of hope:

-lawyers are seeing Band case

-lawyers from the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) have contributed to the resistance

-this resistance has not just erupted in the streets, it has also come from: 

-journalists who have come together to fight the autocracy

-foreign service officials who have stood up to the executive orders

-judges who are adjudicating the orders

-the resistance has come from “the streets to the suites”

-the opposition is not like it was in the 1950s or 1960s

-we are seeing the repoliticization of the people

-people understand what’s at stake in the current political landscape

-journalists are part of society…

-due to the speed of the internet, we are working in “nanosecond type of media coverage”

(e.g., something newsworthy seems to be happening all the time)


Anya Schiffrin (AS):

-there are some good things that have been happening for the media:

-greater amount of investigative journalism

-I had a personal encounter with the President during a town hall




Karen Attiah (KA):

-what is going on now almost feels like an overreaction to the current political situation

-our job as journalists is to challenge the President at every turn, where it is appropriate

-we went all in this year

-I believe his actions are a fundamental threat to democracy

-investigations need to call out every falsehood, lie, distortion, etc.

-it could be argued that he has been engaging in national gaslighting

-this is my first time in the mainstream media environment

-I spent a lot of previous time in digital media (e.g., Twitter, Facebook)

-my generation increasingly gets its news from social media

-this is professionally challenging, but also personally disheartening

-those who need truth exist in silos, 

-it will take an extra effort to reach across these personal silos

-we need to engage in media solidarity

-e.g., we need to fight against the erroneous claim that we have not been reporting on terrorist attacks

-we face a common threat that makes us like each other, at least for a little bit of time…


AS:

-I recently attended Davos

-we were talking that the journalism school needs to play a leading role in the new news reality

-for example, during his first press conference, we needed to not ask any other questions until he answered the first question leave

-being together is in the interest of the whole industry and whole country

-we also need to see a sharp increase in paid subscriptions to journalism


KA:

-at the Washington Post, we are now getting more and more subscriptions after the start of the new administration

-we are looking to fight

-however, deep investigative journalism requires great resources

-now more than ever, the public needs to take a sense of ownership in its media outlets


AS:

-everyone needs to pay for as many journalism subscriptions as possible


Marcus Mrowka (MM)

-the American Federation of Teachers (AFT) is involved in the current political situation

-for example, we were involved in the Muslim travel ban

-provided information regarding the confirmation process and vote on Education Secretary nominee Betsy DeVos

-these were both good examples of useful work that we could do…

-we knew we were going to lose the vote on the DeVos confirmation hearing

-however, we helped educate our AFT members

-we also tapped into the popular feeling of value of public education

-something was unleashed in the nomination hearing that we want to take advantage of…

-it was a good model for the resistance: 

-we need to change the educational narrative

-the journalists got it right away and the facts did matter

-we helped feed journalists what mattered


KV:

-a few points that came up from earlier in the conversation:


-Objectivity:

-the media is beginning to find values that have been baked into The Nation’s DNA

-the media is not adversarial for the sake of being adversarial

-instead, it acts this way for the sake of public accountability

-the media has traditionally preached objectivity

-however, that is beginning to shift

-you could argue that you are more honest if you describe your values

-this is more in line with European journalism practices

-but even if you describe your values, you also need to fact check…


-The Importance of Listening:

-we live in our own filter bubble

-many of the media members live and work in California, Oregon, and Washington

(e.g., traditional Democratic Party strongholds)

-we recently sent someone to Lordstown, Ohio

-we are now doing listening tours around the country

-Michael Massing wrote that the New York Times needs to think of new reporting beats

-perhaps they should pull staff from the Style beat and start new ones:

-Small Business, 

-Culture, 

-Religion, 

-Blue Collar, etc.


A Possible Solution:

-perhaps we need to rethink our hyper coverage of the President’s Twitter account:

-he “wins the morning” and drives the news cycle

-perhaps we need to think hard about that phenomenon…

-perhaps we could leverage various emerging media powers

-the power of social media,

-the power of journalism,

-the power of  satire (e.g., SNL’s parody of Sean Spicer)

-we should try to investigate these things to reach other communities

-however, we need to be careful. Reality may outstrip satire…




KA:

-post-election, there has been some sort of media reaction

-e.g., there has been a sense of self-flagellation that we ignored white males

-perhaps we created profiles that glamorized the alt-right

-these phenomena have leveled off a bit

-we have begun to editorialize more

-I was at Washington’s Dulles Airport for the Muslim ban

-it is important for journalists (and citizens) to go and see reactions to events

(e.g., lawyers barred from accessing detainees)

(e.g., see how people of all ages and stripes stand up/out)

-by doing this, we arrive at a greater amount of authenticity

-we need to go back to the ground level

-we need to get past irritation with politics

-we need to go forward to abandon the sense that we (e.g., the Washington Post) are the ultimate gate keepers for news and events

-I am looking forward to seeing people care about what is happening in the rest of the world

-we also need to prioritize outside of news regarding the current administration

-we need to make sure we are paying attention to things outside of the country, outside of the administration


MM:

-The American Federation of Teachers (AFT) has 1.6M members across the country

-our best advocacy and political tool is having our members be storytellers

(e.g., they can be the people who speak to parents, community members, etc.)

-we can also provide teachers with the right lesson plans

(e.g., how to filter out fake news)

(e.g., how to interpret news)

(e.g., how to be critical thinkers)

-we hope to have journalists work with us

(e.g., understanding, judging news stories)


KV: 

-Americans may begin paying attention to the rest of world due to the spread of “Global Trumpism” occurring across the globe

-(e.g., right wing uprisings in Germany, Netherlands, France, etc.)

-we are still trying to figure out all of the competing forces that have contributed to this right wing swing

-for example, we are seeing new autocrats in Hungary, etc.

-The Nation has begun been looking at “leaks” from the administration

(e.g., leaks regarding agencies clashing early)

(e.g., leaks regarding the draft executive order to legalize discrimination against LGBTQIA women

-apparently Ivanka had words with Pence, etc.)

-these types of battles will go on

(e.g., black sites and torture)

-leaks have been important journalistic tools in the past (e.g., Pentagon Papers, etc.)

-we need to continue using them when appropriate


AS:

-1. Perhaps leaks are meant as float trial balloons to shut down leaks or see how public debate will reach

-2. Leaks have become more dominant on the news cycle of New York Times digital

-you can surf there to go see a lot of leaks and anger


Q: facts still matter

-however, this leaves us with two perspectives on the issue:

-a. is it a supply-side problem?

-there is a greater amount of disinformation being circulated online

-b. is it a demand-side problem?

-why are people willing to believe disinformation?

-I am especially happy to hear your answers to this because…

-Global Muckraking course launches tomorrow on EdX


KV:

-media literacy is very important

-if you don’t believe in facts are important, we should pack journalism up…

-can’t run media enterprise that way

(e.g., ignoring or minimizing the fundamental importance of facts…


MM:

-perhaps we need to think about facts wrapped in values

-my hometown is Bristol, CT

-the local newspaper was bought by Sheldon Adelson

-he subsequently dismissed the city hall reporter

-from this development, where do people go for facts?

(e.g., especially, in this case, about local news)


KV:

-there was the recent incident of KellyAnne Conway speaking erroneously about the fictitious Bowling Green Massacre

-you needed to see the impact of city and state bloggers for accountability…


KA:

-there is a strange relationship between:

-(a) the facts v. 

-(b) what is “the truth” for people

-the Washington Post is beginning to look to become more of a national paper

-(e.g., there has been talk of the America Beat 

-this would help to report on stories they become national)

-there has not been enough talk about the decimation of local news

-need to read news to help expose stories…

(e.g., Greg Abbott: reported on shutting funding through sanctuary city laws)


KV:

-the local media is in such a weakened state that it is threatening to be shut down


AS:

-I used to cover school boards in rural Pennsylvania

-I worked in an old steel town that was pro-union

-everyone in the town started to turn against the teachers’ union

-people started denouncing the state’s Secretary of Education because he was a Jew…

-the local reporters were embattled within this combative environment

-as bad as it was, the situation has become even worse

-who will be people standing up for the mass media? Local media?

-will it be billionaires or zillionaires?

(e.g., Mike Bloomberg from Bloomberg, Jeff Bezos from Amazon)


JV:

-the new publishing funding models have made us start to consider co-publishing

-I’m not sure I know much about the gazillionaire model of funding…

-perhaps we should investigate partnership models or non-profit models 

(e.g., The Nation)

-there have been instances where local news has been involved in international collaboration

(e.g., the Panama Papers, Wikileaks transnational partnerships)

-there are also models around the world of public interest media

(e.g., government public financing)

-perhaps we could sell off part of the broadcasting spectrum to fund national-based public interest journalism

-funding will become a critical issue moving forward…

-e.g., Pierre Omidyar funds The Intercept


AS:

-is the Soros’ Open Foundation a good model for funding journalism?


MM:

-I hope that the Soros Foundation is not our model…

(e.g., Bezos has a track record against unions)

(e.g., Bloomberg has a track record against teachers)

-perhaps we should just take what we can get for now…


KV:

-perhaps as a funding model, we should look at the U.K.’s Scot Trust

(e.g., it has supported The Guardian newspaper)

-perhaps we need to have wealthy stakeholders plus independents


KA:

-at the Washington Post, we are given a runway to experiment

-we can experiment with robustness 

(e.g., if want to explore the breadth and depth of a story)

(e.g., can we partner with local media organizations?)

-we are all in this together

-we are also looking to partner with local media initiatives and collectives

-we are the Washington Post, but still can’t do this alone…
