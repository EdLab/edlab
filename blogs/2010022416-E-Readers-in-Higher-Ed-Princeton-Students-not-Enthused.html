E-Readers in Higher Ed - Princeton Students not Enthused

Stephen Asunka

February 24, 2010

Because

  • it was difficult to take notes on the Kindle
  • PDF documents could not be annotated or highlighted at all
  • it was hard to look at more than one document at once
  • the lack of concrete page numbers make citing sources a huge pain in the ...
  • you need to charge up the battery for it to workâ€¦ what book needs charging?
  • etc.
These are some of the findings of a semester-long (Fall 2009) pilot study at Princeton University. Read full report here With these student voices, researchers have noted that the Kindle was occasionally a tool that was counter-productive to scholarship. On a positive note, the study also revealed that the e-reader could be much greener (by way of reduced printing). Apparently an earlier conviction that the e-reader could speed up the use and embrace of electronic textbooks on campuses is not holding. But are e-textbooks in general even gaining any credence among higher ed students? If they are (not) why? And is the library perceived as playing any role in the situation? At Development & Research, we are in the process of rolling out a study that will seek answers to these and other related questions (from TC students for now). We anticipate that findings will not only be of value to us in the library, but will enable us contribute to the current scholarly discourse on this e-book phenomenon/phantom
