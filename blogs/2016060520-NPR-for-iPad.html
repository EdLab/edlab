NPR for iPad

dinosossi2

June 5, 2016


Somewhat ironically, a recent news article discusses the idea that podcasts like Serial encourage reading. Capitalizing on this trend, NPR for iPad turns your favorite public radio show into a virtual magazine, combining the finest in broadcasting content with arresting images and engaging text.

Pros:

NPR for iPad offers a diverse array of high quality content from sections such as Arts & Life, Music, and News. For regular listeners, the app includes many of your favorite news shows like All Things Considered, Morning Edition, Fresh Air, Planet Money, and On the Media. Arts programming includes TED Radio Hour, Wait Wait… Don’t Tell Me!, Radiolab, and for gearheads, the iconic Car Talk. Music aficionados can also easily access transmissions of All Songs Considered, Alt.Latino, and World Cafe.

NPR for iPad keeps its offerings in pint-sized packets, easily digestible for even those with the shortest attention spans. Programming is normally in the three- to five-minute range. The interactivity isn’t extravagantly immersive; it’s more stately and functional as you would probably expect from the country’s best public radio stations.

Cons:

The full slate of NPR’s broad wealth of national programming is not easily accessible. Unfortunately, you have to dig through a local station directory to find local gems like WNYC, KQED in San Francisco, WAMU in DC, KEXP in Seattle, and so many more sources of audio goodness, but the excavation is worth it.

The multimedia offerings of NPR for iPad are neither super diverse nor eye-popping. There is normally well-written text as well as small, visually arresting thumbnail photos that accompany most stories, although some pictures are missing. Video and animation don’t appear to be included in the app (but what do you expect, it’s radio).

Our Takeaway:

Given the nagging non-stop noise of the digital age, the serene sound of a person speaking directly to the listener may be a calming influence. The art of conversation is just that, an art. And what better way to learn how to have civil, healthy, and respectful dialogue than from the trusty stewards of the nation’s long-standing spoken word tradition.
