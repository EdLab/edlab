Good People - Ch. 6 - Pragmatism vs. Idealism

christophergu

June 22, 2017

Idealistic 
Versus being pragmatic
That is work struggle.

Our always creative and talented Video Producer, Jackie Heltz, inspired me to start my review of Ch. 6 of "Good People" with a haiku! This poem sums up the dilemma that Anthony Tjan presents in this chapter. 


People often think in idealistic ways, but their reality somehow prevents them from fully achieving and realizing their vision. This could be due to financial resources, human resources, or other barriers. Tjan argues that the key to success is finding the right balance between idealism and pragmatism. He believes that a grand vision is only as good as how it is executed. 


Here at the EdLab, we are constantly encouraged to think about innovative and ambitious teaching and learning goals. We visualize the future of learning spaces and learning tools and applications. These are lofty goals for a relatively small team, also tasked with operating a library within a premier graduate school. Our immediate reality is to provide top-notch customer service to patrons and the College community. 


Tjan states that what drives the purpose and vision of an organization is "a set of enduring and relatable values, or immutable truths." The question for most workers is how much they adhere to and align with these values. In the chapter, Tjan cites the 5 core values of a startup, Jumpcut:


1) Help people grow. 

2) Run into the wall. 

3) Bad ideas welcome.

4) Be a barrel. (Don't complain, solve!)

5) Sign your name on it. 


What do you feel are the shared values and truths of EdLab, as an organization? How can we continue to do the forward-thinking work that we are engaged in, while striking the balance between idealism and pragmatism?
