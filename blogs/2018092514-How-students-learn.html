How students learn

Rebecca.Sullivan

September 25, 2018

Inspired by a recent D&R, as well as a class I'm taking on embodied cognition, I've been spending time thinking about how students learn. I recently came across a resource for teachers that placed the puzzle in a new perspective.


The University of Chicago's Center for Teaching has curated resources to support professors and graduate student teachers in their teaching. The page

 is titled: "How Students Learn: Some Key Theories." Immediately under the title reads a headline: "What do we mean by 'learn'?"


The question seemed simple, yet I didn't have an immediate answer. The Chicago Center for Teaching offered two definitions:


  • At the most basic level, by “learn” we usually mean how students ABSORB, PROCESS, RETAIN, and UTILIZE information.
  • If we expand on what we mean by “learn,” we could also be referring to: memorization, process of constructing concepts, developing ability to think critically, learning as personal development, acquisition of motor skills, goal meeting, etc.

I was struck by the breadth of the second meaning of learning. Including personal development in learning seems to entrust education with promoting character and even moral growth. Furthermore, the meaning of learning appears inexhaustible (the small but powerful "etc." keeps open endless possibilities). 


After a brief paragraph on learning styles, the rest of the webpage is devoted to "The -ISMS of Learning:" behaviorism, cognitivism, constructionism, humanism, and connectivism. For each -ism, they offer specific ways the theory can be implemented in the classroom. None, however, seemed fully equipped to fully take on the second definition of learning, particularly learning as personal development. Is this definition of learning too broad, or are our theories too narrow?
