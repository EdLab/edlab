Same sex marriages

Carmel Addae

July 6, 2011

New York recently became one of a few states across America to legally allow same sex marriage. I think this could lead to a large election and political debate. The right to same sex marriage, as it stands now, is up to the states. That is, only state governments can decide whether or not to allow same sex marriage. States such as Massachusetts, Connecticut and New Hampshire allow same sex marriage. For their part, the federal government have yet to decide on the federal status of the same sex marriages, and I feel it has the potential to become a big political and election issue, especially with the presidential election just a year away. Like other major social issues, Democrats and Republicans are divided on the issue. Most Democrats and liberals favor same sex marriages while Republicans and conservatives do not agree on marriage rights to same sex couples. Voters on both sides will surely quiz respective candidates on the issue and with the legality of same sex marriage now a big issue, candidates may find that general answers might not work this time. It is also interesting to see how different parts of the country stand on the issue. Most of the states that allow same sex marriages or some sort of it are located in the Northeast while the middle of the country and Western states don't favor all out legal rights for same sex couples. The public and media's attention to California's Proposition 8 a few years ago highlights both the social, political and geographic shift when it comes to same sex marriage.
