Youth Gamers As Future Citizens

chingfu

June 18, 2014


Most research on youth and games focuses on their potential for academic learning or their associations with aggressive behaviors. In recent years, however, there are more studies investigating the relationship between video games and civic engagement. Kahne, Evans and Middaugh (2008) identified civic experiences in and around gaming activities and found that civic gaming experiences are positively related to youth civic engagement. Bagley and Shaffer (2011) found that Urban Science, an epistemic game that asks players to assume the role of urban planners, can enhance players' civic thinking skills. Undoubtedly, research and interests in this area are growing and would contribute to a better understanding of gaming for civic engagement.

In this video, Kurt Squire, a leading scholar in this field, talks about how to leverage video games’ compelling capacity to pique players' interest for civic learning. He also shares some interesting observations about the similarities between gaming activities and youth social participation, drawing on an example of one of the largest protests in Madison Wisconsin.

Have you considered the connection between gaming and civic learning? Do you agree with Professor Squire’s perspectives? Share your thoughts with educators in the discussion on Vialogues.
