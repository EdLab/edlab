Teaching and Tweeting

Ting Yuan

December 21, 2014


Can Twitter improve instructor-student relations in higher education? Does Twitter increase student engagement? A recent study asked these questions of undergraduate students enrolled in a twitter-enabled business and management course. In the course, the students and their instructor communicated via Twitter over a 12-week semester to complement their face-to-face interactions in class. At the conclusion of the course, students responded to a survey that investigated their attitudes and experiences.

The data analysis conveyed three dimensions of students’ Twitter use: interpersonal relations, engagement, and class attendance. First, the author found that Twitter usage was significantly correlated to student engagement. Students who used the platform used it for organizing their social lives and sharing personal information, as well as posting course-related thoughts. However, the usage was not correlated to instructor-student interpersonal relations or class attendance.

These findings are helpful for educational practitioners considering incorporating social networking tools into courses. In the Twitter space featured in the study, students naturally blended their academic and social worlds by sharing course reflections and personal lives. This fluid conversational phenomenon could inform researchers of new ways to conceptualize quality online discussion – an informal space for learning, with succinct instructor guidance, which might help to generate more robust student discussion than a purely academic discussion forum.

Evans, C. (2014). Twitter for teaching: Can social media be used to enhance the process of learning? British Journal of Educational Technology, 45(5), 902-915.
