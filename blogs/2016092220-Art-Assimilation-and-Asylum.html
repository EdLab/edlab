Art, Assimilation, and Asylum

Jenny Abamu

September 22, 2016


Syrian refugees in Canada who speak little English face the daunting challenges of assimilating to a new country, making new friends, and adjusting to a new lifestyle. And they're finding their way with the help of the Sick Muse Art Project, a program that seeks to build inclusive environments while promoting social justice through art. "For us it is important to be part of the integration, to welcome the kids to their new and safe home," says Paola Gomez, cofounder of the program and former refugee from Latin America.

The organization’s Our World a Thousand Colours Program is specifically tailored for refugees and children in shelters; it creates a safe space to learn and transition. Students in the program draw, paint, and create works of art for the class. Then they share their creations in English.

In what ways do you think art can be therapeutic and educational? Tell us about your unique experiences with art and development in the ongoing discussion on Vialogues.
