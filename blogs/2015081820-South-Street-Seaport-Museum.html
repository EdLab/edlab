South Street Seaport Museum

EdLab Studios

August 18, 2015


The South Street Seaport Museum boasts a vast assortment of paintings, captainâ€™s logs, and navigational tools in its collection, but one of the most impressive artifacts stands a towering 77 feet over the gentle waves and can hold over 43 gross tons of cargo. The 130-year-old schooner known as the Pioneer is one of six historical ships that compose the Street of Ships at Pier 16 and more often than not is manned by a crew of middle schoolers.

Through the Adventures Under Sail program, students grades 2-12 from the five boroughs and beyond explore the history of New York Harbor aboard the historic ship itself on the very waters where settlers ventured, trading flourished, and the Revolutionary War was waged. As teams of young deckhands work diligently to raise the sails, the ship leaves port, embarking upon a learning experience unlike anything landlocked classrooms have to offer.

In 90 minute to three hour sessions, students make their way around the mighty vessel to master the nautical vernacular, get their hands on nautical charts and navigation tools, test water quality, examine harbor wildlife, and conduct physics experiments using the many simple machines that allow sailors to complete tasks with ease. In years to come, one may not recall every detail of their childhood education, but it will be hard to forget the feeling of a cool breeze whipping across the East River, the image of halyard ropes serenely framing the horizon, and the sound of the tide lapping up against the wrought iron sides of the Pioneer.
