Website X

Anthony Cocciolo

September 29, 2006

I have my eye on the fourth column of the library website. According to our initial plans for the library website, the fourth column would be the place where we would feature a next generation research tool (the ads are completely lovely, but planned only as temporary). We have called this tool Library Website X. The prospects of Website X have led me to think about the McFarland and Klopfer presentation. As you may recall, McFarland and Klopfer proposed SPINE (Scholar Practitioner Information Networks for Education), which aimed to “create multiple network depictions of the educational knowledge domain that can be interactively explored via on-line system — that builds on prior work in related fields and seeks to extend and improve upon it by applying it to the field of educationâ€? (2). The paper took particular aim at Google style lists of results and advocated for a more visual scheme similar to Grokker:

Good search criteria can generate a long list of knowledge artifacts relevant to a topic, but the lists do not facilitate the viewer's understanding of a topic, its organization, and how it is developed — i.e., how types of knowledge span artifacts and are interrelated, and how social relations under-gird this body of developing knowledge.
At the time of the presentation, I thought that dismissing the list as not facilitating understanding as a bit premature. Interestingly enough, today's Wall Street Journal featured an article titled, “The Way We List Nowâ€?, which argues for the primacy of the list as a way of making sense of things. I mention this article because I think we should seriously consider the ways in which individuals cognitively process visual materials and not so quickly dismiss the list. I have also been looking at other web tools for doing research. Of course we have Super Search, which from my perspective is very cool. There is also Ottobib, which claims to “make bibliographies faster, easier, and more useful.â€? There is also CrazyEgg, which attempts to visualize web usage in some interesting ways. And although not quite a research tool but interesting visual experience, checkout the AMD Virtual IT Experience, which aims to create the impression of being at a high-end technology trade show.
