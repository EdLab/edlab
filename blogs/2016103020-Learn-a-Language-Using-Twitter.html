Learn a Language Using Twitter

saraehardman

October 30, 2016


tDict has a simple goal: to help people learn foreign language vocabulary in real contexts by seeing how people use these words on Twitter. The app is minimalist; it has only one function. When users search for a word or phrase in a specified language, the app displays the most recent tweets that include the term, most of which are from native speakers.

Pros:

tDict has one function, and it performs it relatively well. Users can search for words and phrases in over 150 languages, although the most commonly learned languages, such as Spanish, French, and Chinese, provide the most results. The app collects the world’s most recent tweets and displays them in a list that continues indefinitely. Users can star the tweets they find most helpful and refer to them later. Because tDict offers results for both words and phrases, it would be a particularly useful tool for learning slang and idioms in a foreign language.

Cons:

Despite the large number of available languages in tDict, some of them do not provide any results. For instance, when I searched for common words in Swahili, Luxembourgish, and Latin, the app did not find any relevant tweets. This could be because there is not much tweeting in these languages, or it could be that the app’s algorithm failed to find them. Still, since tDict gives users the option of so many languages, it should provide the results.

Our Takeaway:

tDict could be a useful tool for people who have a solid understanding of a foreign language looking to improve their vocabulary or grasp native slang. It’s said that immersion is the best way to learn a language because you encounter how words are actually used by native speakers. tDict was created with a similar philosophy, although downloading the app is much less commitment than living abroad. It’s great for the most commonly used and learned languages in the world, but don’t use tDict if you’re trying to learn a less-commonly spoken language.
