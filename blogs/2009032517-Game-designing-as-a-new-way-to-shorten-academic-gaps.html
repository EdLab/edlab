Game designing as a new way to shorten academic gaps

Ting Yuan

March 25, 2009

Today's edutopia issued a compelling clip named Games for Learning and Assessment. Part of the clip reports the application of game design within the McKinley Technology High School (Washington D.C.), a public school focusing on science, engineering, and digital media since 2004. The school immerses video game designing technologies to regular curriculum, and students are actual game designers under guidance. The school's 2008 record shows encouraging results of students' close performance from low and high socioeconomic classes. This case provides a innovative way to think about shortening academic gaps for students from different socioeconomic, lingual, and/or racial backgrounds. In a district where, on average, only 26% of students test as proficient or better in mathematics, and 29% in reading, 57% of McKinley students tested proficient or better in mathematics, and 68% in reading. Almost equally noteworthy: while economically disadvantaged students lagged behind non-disadvantaged students by large margins in both subjects across the district, at McKinley, the two groups performed at nearly identical levels.
