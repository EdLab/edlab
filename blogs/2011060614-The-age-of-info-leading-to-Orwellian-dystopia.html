The age of info leading to Orwellian dystopia?

Joyce Britton

June 6, 2011

An article by Jonah Lehrer in the Wall Street Journal discusses the advantages and disadvantages of our hyperconnected lives. On one hand, we have an overwhelming learning resource at our fingertips. On the other hand, we seem to be losing our capacity for independent thought: And yet, while the Web has enabled new forms of collective action, it has also enabled new kinds of collective stupidity. Groupthink is now more widespread, as we cope with the excess of available information by outsourcing our beliefs to celebrities, pundits and Facebook friends. Instead of thinking for ourselves, we simply cite what's already been cited. I suppose that, in a way, I am doing more of the same by linking to this article. Let's break the cycle! What are some ways we can continue thinking for ourselves, despite our inclination to adopt the opinions of the masses (much like the high school students do in The Wave, come to think of it...)? I love that most of EdLab's projects are based on the ideas of info reaction and interaction. Participating in such tools as Vialogues will certainly help combat "collective stupidity!" Other ideas: Subscribe to publications and blogs from all sides of the political, social and economic spectrum. Instead of avoiding debate, make an effort to engage in it in comment sections, message forums, etc. Maintain a personal journal that will force you to react to information, rather than simply absorb it. What else?
