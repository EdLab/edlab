Thinking With Clicks

Fred Rossoff

May 6, 2014


Title: An Innovative Approach to Enhancing Continuing Education Activities for Practicing Pharmacists Using Clicker Technology (2014)

Authors: Luke E. Grzeskowiak, Josephine To, Alicia E. Thomas, & Adam J. Phillips

Source: Educational Psychology Review

Research Question: Can an audience response system improve the experience of continuing education activities?

Study Design: It’s almost a cliché these days to note that people don’t seem to learn very much in lectures, and instead spend most of their time daydreaming or snoozing. Implementing some form of audience interaction or participation would seem a good way to ensure greater attention and retention.

For this study, the authors gave the 60 attendees of an Australian 2012 pharmacist symposium small electronic keypads, known as clickers, which allowed them to answer questions during presentations.

Findings: In post-symposium interviews, the participants overwhelmingly agreed that these clickers improved their experience by allowing for more interactivity. Audience members were able to see (anonymized) data on what other members were thinking, allowing them to compare their knowledge with the group and catch information they might have missed.

Moving Forward: As the authors point out, this study wasn’t designed to test whether the use of the clicker actually resulted in greater knowledge gain. However, by verifying that audience members find them unobtrusive and helpful, we should have greater confidence moving forward in attempting such tests, and other methods of improving interactivity during talks.
