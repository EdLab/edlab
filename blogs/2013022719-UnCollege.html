UnCollege

LauraCostello

February 27, 2013


UnCollege is a collection of resources for students thinking outside the college box. The site is heavily skewed towards the tech industry and highlights a $10,000, 1-year program that shuttles "students" through months of exploring abroad, working at a startup, pursuing personal creative projects, and building a brand. The site is trying to build a community around foregoing formal schooling and produced a "Hackademic" accelerator/conference in January 2013. Founder Dale Stephens, a 2010 Thiel fellow who was recently named by Forbes as a bright new talent in education, is releasing a book based on the site in March, called Hacking Your Education: Ditch the Lectures, Save Tens of Thousands, and Learn More Than Your Peers Ever Will.

Pros:

The site is a good starting point for a transition from student to un-student. It lists a well-curated group of fellowships and accelerators, a small but diverse group of interviews, and a reading list that is clearly a labor of love. The members of the UnCollege Team have a personal stake in the movement that shows through in the clear attention they’ve paid to the site, all staff either avoided college completely or dropped-out after a few semesters. The site is a positive resource for anyone interested in an unconventional education experience and includes practical things like tips on breaking the news to your parents.

Cons:

The site has weirdly collegiate imagery and may come off a bit too positive, given the challenging and murky path that some students forgoing college may experience. Downloading the manifesto is also a bit tricky; what appears to simply be an external download actually signs users up for the site. The manifesto itself is worth a read though. It is simple, but contains a good road plan and what seems like some hard-won wisdom.

Our Takeaway:

UnCollege is part of a small but growing culture of college skepticism. At this point there is precious little structure in place to accelerate a career outside of formal education and UnCollege is presenting a legitimate channel for growth. Though UnCollege seems to espouse a particular path outside of school, Stephens is a great model for this type of path. He’s extremely motivated, industrious, faithful in networking, and constantly involved in writing his own story. The site works well as a starting point and perhaps paves the way for diverse models of post-secondary education outside the college gates.

The Bottom Line:

UnCollege is an intriguing first resource for potential college ditchers.
