Trends in Ed: The Two Koreas

Fred Rossoff

July 1, 2011

Innovation in the field of education is not restricted to the American scene, and for today's Trends in Education post I'd like to talk about events in the Korean peninsula. First, the South Korean Education Ministry announced this week their goal of digitalizing all textbooks by 2015. Basing their plan on South Korea's traditionally high rankings in digital literacy, they believe that transitioning to electronic reading will not only be cheaper, but also better suited to the learning strategies of Korean grade and high school students. This program will include a "a cloud computing network," with which students will be able to upload and access textbooks from anywhere. Meanwhile, on the other side of the DMZ, North Korea has closed its universities until April 2012, and sent the students to build houses or labor in the fields. Universities tend to be centers of discontent and protest (as is apparent even in the history of post-partition South Korea), so that, although touted for its economic benefits, this policy looks more like an attempt by Kim Jong-Il to head off trouble. As a response to growing unrest, this resembles (in a minor way) Mao's “Down to the Countryside” movement, although one can't help but feel that somehow, Peter Thiel is to blame.
