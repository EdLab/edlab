Trends in Ed, 3.18.09

Arianna Choi

March 18, 2009

Bill & Ted's Excellent Adventure — ver.2009 Excellent! Party on, dude. Here's a two-minute refresher on learning and teaching history: Bill & Ted's adventure may not be as profound and moving as my other classic list of favorite movies like The Mission and Shawshank Redemption, but it still makes the list for me. I remember watching it and wishing that my history teacher would find some other assignment besides memorizing timelines. The closest I got to my ideal experience was reading primary sources and, later in college, making relevant connections (at least in my head) in the web of seemingly different events and perspectives. Since a time traveling phone booth isn't quite readily available yet, students today can't replicate an adventure as exciting (or just funny) as Bill & Ted's but how about using — and further developing — tools that are already out there? All kidding aside, Google Earth's Ancient Rome 3D takes users on a geographic and historic tour of the globe with maps and satellite images of land and sea. To make this tech relevant to learning and teaching, Google held a curriculum design competition and the result is the winning teachers taking their standards-based lessons and adapting them to interactive models of ancient and modern-day Rome. Another case-in-practice for archeology and anthropology studies is Tanzania Virtual Summer Camp (a collaborated project headed by the Field Museum of Natural History in Chicago).
