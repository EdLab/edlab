Experiencing with live web-conferencing technology and further integrating offline with online experiences?

Lin Lin

November 10, 2005

While experimenting with back-channel chat at a face-to-face meeting, it might also be worthwhile pushing us further to try out synchronous web conferencing tools. Personally, I am not a savvy or even comfortable user of web conferencing tools due to various reasons (FYI -- I attached a paper Patricia and I wrote, which included a story about my overwelming experience of using web conferencing tools). Yet, synchronous live web-conferencing seems to allow to do more than the back-channel chat at a face-to-face meeting: despite the fact that the participants sit at their own computers rather than sitting around a table (the ability to not have to fly to one location to have a meeting is a major reason for the development of such a technology), everything else can be the same or more: the presenter can speak, bring up a PowerPoint, video, or website, with everyone watching him or her on the screen; the participants can chat, “raise handsâ€? to speak, or simply speak (if given privileges), and project their cameras on the other participants' screens. There are other features such as whiteboard (to allow the participants to draw/write together), survey/pools (to allow the participants to vote on an issue quickly), application sharing (to allow instant editing of a file), archiving, etc. The following is a screenshot of a typical web conferencing environment: None of the technologies have to be used as intended by the designers, e.g., web conferencing tools to be used only at a distance, and often they are not. Experimenting with extremes is probably a good way to find out what distance technologies can be brought near and for local uses, what local needs can be enhanced with distance technologies, and what works best for different purposes.
