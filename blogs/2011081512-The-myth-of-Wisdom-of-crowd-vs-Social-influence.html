The myth of "Wisdom of crowd" vs. "Social influence"

Sharon Hsiao

August 15, 2011

In Sitaram Asur and Bernardo Huberman's study in 2009, they examined nearly three million Twitter updates over a period of three months. They were looking for tweets mentioning any of twenty-four movies released in that time, from Avatar to The Spy Next Door, to see whether the number of tweets said anything about each movie's success. It turned out that the rate at which a movie was being discussed correlated almost exactly with how well it would do at the box office the following weekend. The rate was correct 98 percent of the time! Also, 94 percent of the time, the ratio of fans-to-haters on Twitter indicated how far a movie would rise or fall the following week. There are more and more evidence of wisdom of crowds that groups can be remarkably accurate in estimating vaguely known facts. Controversy studies also demonstrate statistical data to show that using the wisdom of crowd effects multiple times creates collective overconfidence in possibly false beliefs. It might diminish the diversity of the crowd without improving its collective error. Crowds may become less reliable in providing expertise for external observers. How do we avoid the social influence pitfalls and harness the wisdom of crowd? I think, provide a more transparent user social model with adaptive support may be a way, such as adaptive information access, adaptive navigation support, personalized recommendations etc. One of the major benefits of OUM is to increase the awareness for the group or individual. With proper adaptive navigation support to assist in exploring suitable options for decision-making or serendipitous recommendation may resolve the problem of lack of group diversities.
