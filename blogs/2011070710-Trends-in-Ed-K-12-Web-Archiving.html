Trends in Ed: K-12 Web Archiving

LauraCostello

July 7, 2011

The Library of Congress and the Internet Archive have been working together to bring web archiving to the K-12 set. For the past three years, students in 14 schools across the country have been learning about primary sources and creating their own internet “time capsules” for the students and researchers of the future. Using digital archiving service Archive-It, students select, identify, and curate collections of websites they feel are important to the modern student. The collections are wide ranging and include resources traditionally overlooked by digital archivists, like the rare gaming websites gone unblocked on school computers, curriculum materials, and local information about restaurants, music, and recreational sports. 25 percent of the sites identified by the K-12 program had not previously been preserved by the Library of Congress and Internet Archive. Students were challenged to think critically about the evolution and connectivity of the modern web and empowered to discover that primary sources could be created in the digital as well as physical world. The collections range from a few web pages to many thousands and choices are explained by the student curators. The project is both an important step in digital archiving and an interesting peek into the web habits of K-12 students.
