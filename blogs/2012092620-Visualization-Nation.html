Visualization Nation

Sharon Hsiao

September 26, 2012


Amidst the information flood in which we are currently immersed, visualizations can be a well-placed treetop. Itâ€™s no surprise that these representations have quickly become ubiquitous across a variety of fields. The Journal of Marketing, perhaps with slight ulterior motives, recently published a paper investigating the effect of these familiar visuals on user thought and behavior. Essentially, visual representation abstracts data into values, and then converts those values into visual cues. This process involves selection, transformation, and presentation in a visual form to facilitate exploration and understanding of the data. Charts and graphs help viewers recognize patterns, identify outliers, and emphasize certain pieces of information.

Lurie and Mason propose a framework for examining how visual representations influence the decision process. The framework includes two key aspects, Visual Perspectives and Information Context, which describe the information a user extracts from the visual and how they use that information to gain insights and make decisions. The visual itself is also scored on five characteristics: interactivity, depth of field, vividness, evaluability and framing.

Interactivity encourages decision makers to consider multiple factors and to restructure the information environment; for example, by rotating or simulating movement around an image; adjusting the ranges of values, etc. Depth of field gives viewers the choice to zoom-in or zoom-out, making available different levels of details in the data. Vividness, the salience of particular information, suggests that more vivid visual information is likely to be acquired and processed earlier. For instance, bold fonts, contrast colors, special sizes and shapes can be used to highlight salient points and carve out a path to a final decision. Evaluability refers the ease with which information can be assessed and compared. Framing specifies the reference point against which the data are compared. As a whole, these components provide control over the displayed information and converge in a powerful, decision-shifting visual.

The rise of big data has the potential to inform decisions, and visual representations can play a crucial intermediate role in this information consumption. Currently, there are several dedicated visualization research groups investigating data processing technologies with novel data diagnostics and transformation visualizations. As the field of visual representation develops, we should pay serious attention to the function and power of visualization. In this new learning era, educators and learners should be particularly aware of the importance of communicating through visual representations.
