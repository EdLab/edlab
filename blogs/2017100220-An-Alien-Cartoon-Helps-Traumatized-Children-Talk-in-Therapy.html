An Alien Cartoon Helps Traumatized Children Talk in Therapy

DebraLee

October 2, 2017


Antennas, a fictional character from Antennopoulous, convinces children to open up about their problems by putting their minds at ease. The gender-neutral alien is being used in Mexican hospitals in real-time psychological interventions both to build trust with children and to facilitate an accelerated consultation process. Psychologists and health professionals are embracing the technology because it helps them to build a picture of a child’s life at home, and serve a larger number of vulnerable children.

Antennas’ creator and child psychologist Julia Borbolla has invented other gender-neutral "special agents" to work with traumatized children. Dulas is red and wears bunny slippers so children in burn units understand Dulas is a patient in the hospital too. Botsy, another puppet, helps figure out how best to approach individual children about court cases.

The Antennas initiative has faced criticism from some medical experts, who question the appropriateness of teaching children to trust fictional characters, and the intervention has yet to undergo the rigor of peer review. But Dr. Borbolla, who has worked with children for over 35 years, wants to see the technology more widely used as a tool to encourage children to speak up and disclose what makes them sad in the hope of helping them move forward.
