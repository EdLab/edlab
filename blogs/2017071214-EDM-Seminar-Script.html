Overview of EDM topics

There is an African proverb: It takes a village to raise a child. It means that the environment has big impacts on the human development. There are multiple factors involved in education. Now we have the technology to assist us to monitor the inputs from the environments, the status of kids and guide school district administrator, teachers, parents for actions. 


Figure 1. The graphic illustration to represent the major topics of EDM conference.


The data had been collected from the multiple learning environments either virtually or physically. There are lots of papers using the Coursera and EdX data sets to build models for knowledge tracing, prediction, and automatic grading. Some teams from the online course content generators such as Harvard University, they create adaptive learning system based on the online student data. Some other teams applied new machine learning techniques such as deep learning, facial recognition to detect the students’ emotion and engagement level, in combining with the learning behavior data such as clicking, video watching time, etc. 


All these data would also help us, the researcher and educators, to evaluate the educational theories, curriculum designs, and pedagogy environments, to facilitate the decision-making process, rethinking and trying to solve the ultimate problem:"how to raise the children in our village?"


Assessment automation

Adaptive Assessment Experiment in a HarvardX MOOC (speaker: Ilia Rushkin)

Current problem: some students don’t have the optimal path in learning.
Solution: adaptive guidance with a specific assessment to probe learners’ state of knowledge directly.

Experiment setup:

16 graded homework with assessment questions customized by javascript 

4 out of 16 homework were adaptive homework are used for the experiment.

Control group:  explore questions in the sequence of knowledge components (KC) once

Experiment group: explore questions in the recommendation system based on their mastery of KCs and levels of difficulties, Bayesian knowledge tracing was used to construct the recommendation system.

Conclusions:

Their preliminary findings suggest that the adaptivity assessment system they created leads to a higher efficiency of learning without an adverse effect on learning outcomes. Learners go through the course faster and attempt fewer problems since the problems are served to them in a targeted way.

The questions are tagged with 1 or more KCs. 

133 questions were tagged with 66 KCs, 39 adaptive homework questions with 25 KCs for learning curve construction.

During the talk, I asked the following questions to the speaker: 

  1. How do you determine the difficulty levels?
    Answer: it was determined by the course developers.
  2. Do you weight the question differently due to the difficulty levels?
    Not answered. 
  3. Why you choose Bayesian over cognitive diagnosis matrix.
    Answer: he just chose BKT naturally. BKT is dynamic for recommendation system

Knowledge Tracing

It is a method of modeling a learner's knowledge and skill mastery level. 

Example: funtoot (An India educational tech company)
data of 6th-grade math from date 2015−07−25 to 2017−01−30. 
Syllabus: 22 topics, 69 subtopics, 119 sub-sub-topics, five difficulty levels, 541 complexities, 176 schools with 7780 students, 442 skills, and 1523 problems. The student data has less than 100 problem attempts were excluded.
Finally, they have 5 million data points.
Their paper was about to build different knowledge tracing and prediction models based on these massive data and compare the accuracy of the model. 

Engagement in Education

In EDM, we saw our old friend Dr. Beverly Woolf from the University of Massachusetts. She gave a talk on the similar topic as she did in EdLab 5 years ago. Please check with the vialogues video for review. Dr. Woolf represents the group of researchers who care about students’ emotion and engagement, and how these would affect the learning outcome. 

As the online learning become a new trend in education, we also experience frustration, stress, confusion brought by the machine. The computer could not see our facial expression, could not talk to us to see if we are still motivated in learning. They could not get nor response to learners’ emotion, and therefore the drop out rate is so high in MOOCs. 

Dr. Woolf’s team created pedagogical agents could deliver different types of affective messages. 

Correlation Results

Students who received empathic messages become more confident, patient, with higher levels of interest, and valued math knowledge more. 

Students who received growth mindset messages, get more problems correct on their first attempt but valued math knowledge less and had lower posttest scores. 

Students who received more success/failure messages make more mistakes, to be less learning-oriented, more confused.

Conclusion:

Affective messages are powerful media to influence students’ perceptions of themselves as learners, as well as their perceptions of the domain being taught. The use of empathy to improve student affect and attitudes in a math tutor.
