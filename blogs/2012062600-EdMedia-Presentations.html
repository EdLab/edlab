Using Data Visualization Tools to Support Online Discussion Facilitation ID: 36212 Type: Best Practices Session Topic: Improving Classroom Teaching Room: 3 Tue, Jun. 26 2:45 PM-3:05 PM Authors: Bridget Arend, University of Denver, United States Abstract: Online asynchronous discussions can serve as an effective medium for reflection and critical thinking in online, hybrid and on-campus courses. However, text-heavy discussions can become cumbersome and facilitating online discussions can be a time consuming and arduous task for even the most conscientious instructor. Data visualization tools are available to support the instructor's facilitative role and allow quick and easy snapshots of student participation as well as discussion themes and paths. This paper will describe lessons learned from the use of open source data visualization tools over eight iterations of a professional development course for online faculty. ----------------- Game-Based Learning Instructional Design from Teachers' Perspective ID: 36217 Type: Full Paper: Conceptual & Empirical Study Topic: Teaching/Learning Strategies Room: 4 Tue, Jun. 26 10:00 AM-10:30 AM Authors: Jie Huang, Boise State University, United States DongUk Cheong, Korea National Univ. of Education, Korea (South) Abstract: The research focus on instructional design of using digital games and game characteristics in classroom by investigating how Game-Based Learning instructional design (GBLID) might differ from traditional instructional design (TID) based on ADDIE model from teachers' perspectives. Considering of pervious researches, and results of survey and interview of 36 elementary and secondary school teachers, we re-organized GBLID principles and insert into ADDIE instructional design model, form a framework of GBLID by considering teachers' views of the differences between GBLID and TID, and how these differences might be informed and integrated into the existing framework of instructional design. ----------------- Coordinating Professional Practice with Course Pedagogy in the Design of a Serious Games course ID: 36273 Type: Brief Paper: New Development Topic: Improving Classroom Teaching Room: 3 Wed, Jun. 27 11:35 AM-11:55 AM Authors: Dennis Charsky, Ithaca College, United States William Ryan, Ithaca College, United States Abstract: The design of serious games is having a enormous impact on the employee training (Aldrich, 2005) as well as on the education of future instructional designers (Charsky, 2010b). The presentation will detail the results of a study that explores the practices of professional instructional designs that have created a serious game for employee training. The data from the interviews will be analyzed to determine the methods and processes for communicating content, ways to measure effectiveness of both instruction and game play, utilizing representations, and employing game features. The results of these interviews will inform the design of a undergraduate level serous game course. Assessment of the course project, where students have to design a serious game, and surveys of students on their learning and design process will be presented. The study has the potential to influence the theory and practice of serious games and the pedagogy for college level serious games courses as well as the professional development of instructional designers. ----------------- PD for ML in HE: Embedding mobile learning in a university context ID: 36960 Type: Brief Paper: Other Â  Topic: Instructor Training and Support Room: 11 Authors: Anthony Herrington, Curtin University, Australia Judy Schrape, Curtin University, Australia Kim Flintoff, Curtin University, Australia Jacqui Kelly, Curtin University, Australia Kuki Singh, Curtin University, Australia Diana Taylor, Curtin University, Australia Abstract: Mobile devices are becoming ubiquitous in university settings. While online learning using desktop and laptop device has seen significant growth over the last decade, the next decade will see an increased use of online learning via mobile devices such as tablets and phones. However, the spread of mobile learning in universities is patchy, and thought needs to be given to approaches that will foster widespread uptake. In this paper, we highlight the potential for mobile learning (ML) in higher education (HE) and the ways in which professional development (PD) can be enacted to develop guidelines for mobile learning and embed practices across an institution. ----------------- ALICE: Adaptive Learning via Intuitive/Interactive, Collaborative and Emotional Systems ID: 37104 Type: Corporate Showcase Â  Topic: Multimedia/Hypermedia Systems Room: 2 Authors: Santi Caballe, Open University of Catalonia, Spain Nicola Capuano, University of Salerno, Italy Abstract: ALICE is a project co-funded by the European Commission under the 7th Framework Program. IWT is a complete e-learning and knowledge management platform that delivers personalized courses based on learner's previous knowledge and preferences, allowing learners to learn only required concepts through the most feasible learning resources. ----------------- Exploring the Application of Gaming and Gaming Principles in Education ID: 36183 Type: Panel Â  Topic: Implementation Experiences Room: 2 Authors: Lisa Heaton, Marshall University, United States Jake Bolen, Marshall University, United States Amy Carlson, Marshall University, United States Kay Lawson, Marshall University, United States Debra Lockwood, Marshall University, United States Rikki Lowe, Marshall University, United States Melissa Rhodes, Marshall University, United States Sherri Shafer, Marshall University, United States Abstract: What do you think about gaming and what does it have to do with education? The potential ill effects of gaming and video game content are widely discussed. However, a few authors like Gee (2007) in What Video Games Have to Teach Us about Learning and Literacy and Johnson (2006) in Everything Bad Is Good for You consider the other side of the story. Johnson suggests that we look beyond the surface (content) of video games to the underlying “cognitive workout.” Gee discusses this workout in terms of 36 learning principles that he asserts are present in some of the most popular video games. After participating in a pre-reading blog regarding their opinions of gaming, discussing the work of Johnson in class, and reading Gee's book, doctoral students in a Technology and Curriculum course were asked to experience gaming by interviewing an avid gamer or playing a cognitively challenging game. Panelists will discuss their resulting research findings and implications for teaching and learning.
