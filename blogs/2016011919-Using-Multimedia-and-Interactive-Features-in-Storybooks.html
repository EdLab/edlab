Using Multimedia and Interactive Features in Storybooks

dinosossi2

January 19, 2016


Technology is making inroads in diverse new avenues—even something as old as children's storytelling. A meta-analysis of technology-enhanced stories on young children's literacy development compared the impact of technology on storytelling with stories shared in more traditional settings like good old-fashioned storybook reading time.

This study surveyed experimental studies involving preschool and elementary school students. It compared results along two main outcomes—the development of literacy skills and behavior, while listening to these stories.

The research demonstrated that there was a small, but significant, benefit when using technology to improve story comprehension and expressive vocabulary. Particularly useful multimedia features included sound effects, music, and animated pictures. However, elements like dictionaries, games, and interactive hotspots were found to be distracting.

Given the ongoing incorporation of technology across learning settings, including storytelling, this research can help a diverse array of educators learn to integrate multimedia elements in a variety of formal and informal educational environments.

Takacs, Z. F., Swart, E. K., & Bus, A. G. (2015). Benefits and pitfalls of multimedia and interactive features in technology-enhanced storybooks: A meta-analysis. Review of Educational Research December, 85(4), 698–739.
