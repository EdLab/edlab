Designing Games for Learning at Cultural Heritage Sites

csd2126

February 27, 2017


Games can be a fun way for visitors to learn at cultural sites like landmarks, historical places, or museums. For example, Rugged Rovers is a game that connects players to site-specific content. These types of spatial games merge interactivity with spatial dimensions to encourage exploration. New research reviews literature on spatial game design that engages players with content relative to cultural sites.

The aim of this study was to review literature that explores how cultural content is presented through games-based learning at cultural sites. The research looked at the most recent trends in spatial games that use a variety of game mechanics including trivia, puzzles, and role-playing. Cultural games most often integrate several game genres to provide players with factual and conceptual knowledge. Using multiple game genres gives players different ways to interact with cultural content. The researchers found that cultural games usually tied game outcomes to learning tasks. Most of the games reviewed turned out to be for cultural tourism and were designed to be played on mobile devices.

Many of the games reviewed weren’t suitable for use outside of the cultural site they were designed for. This made it difficult to capture players’ engagement long-term once they’ve left the museum or landmark. While games are an increasingly more prevalent way to connect museum-goers to content, the effectiveness of games for cultural learning is a research direction that needs further exploration.

Malegiannakia, I., & Τhanasis Daradoumisa, T. (2017). Analyzing the educational design, use and effect of spatial games for cultural heritage: A literature review. Computers & Education, 108, 1–10.
