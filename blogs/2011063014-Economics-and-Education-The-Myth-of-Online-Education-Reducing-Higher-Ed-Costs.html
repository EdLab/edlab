Economics & Education: The Myth of Online Education Reducing Higher Ed Costs

Skanda Amarnath

June 30, 2011

Matthew Cameron, a writer (and I believe intern) on Matt Yglesias' ThinkProgress blog argues that with the potential for online education to reduce tuition costs, the real problem will be persuade those who do not wish to attend college that attending is in their self-interest. First problem I have with this post: everywhere where I've read about the use of online learning used in the higher ed setting, tuition costs have nevertheless increased. I am not convinced that online learning applications are good enough yet to serve as a perfect substitute for attending an actual university. Second, as the econ-blogosphere debate about higher ed indicates, I am not so sure we need to start convincing everyone that they will be more productive if they attend college. Sure, the private earnings are there for the taking, but if higher ed is in many areas zero-sum and socially valueless, Cameron "might as well stand up at a concert to "prove" that everyone at the concert will see better if everyone stands up," to quote Bryan Caplan. Secondary school education needs to be improved and high school diplomas ought to mean something again before we start making a big push for students to attend institutions of higher learning. Then we need to start running some value-added analysis to answer that age-old question, but this time about college: Is our children [young adults] learning?
