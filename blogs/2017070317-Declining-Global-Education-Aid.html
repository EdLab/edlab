Declining Global Education Aid

Samuel.Fordjour

July 3, 2017

The value of education cannot be overstated. Educated individuals earn more, live healthier lives, and contribute to civic life. In the West, there are plenty of access to K-12 education. Public education is almost entirely free as well. As important as education is, many countries around the globe are still fighting to provide this fundamental right for their citizens. For example, 50% of children in Burkina Faso are unable to attend school. For developing countries, free and accessible education represents a cost that cannot simply afford. The onus has usually fallen on wealthier countries and nonprofits to make up the deficit.

UNESCO’s Global Education Monitoring Report recently reported that the countries most in need are not receiving enough educational aid. Take the example of the aforementioned Burkina Faso. The cost of enrolling the 50% of school-aged children out of school would be $180 million. However, they received only $17 million in total education aid in 2012. Although total development aid has risen 24%, education aid has been on the decline for the past six years. Many point to the refugee crisis as one of the primary reasons. The crisis has taken both attention and resources such as food and health from education.
