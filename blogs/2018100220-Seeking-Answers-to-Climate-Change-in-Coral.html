Seeking Answers to Climate Change in Coral

saraehardman

October 2, 2018


To understand the earth, so much of what we need to know can be found in the ocean, at least according to paleoceanographer Nathalie Goodkin. Goodkin’s research focuses on collecting data from biological fossils within coral skeletons living in the ocean to analyze how the environment has changed in the past 1,000 years and project how it will look in the future.

Technology advances have made it possible to collect samples from deep within the skeletons of centuries-old coral, which are later analyzed in Goodkin’s lab at the American Museum of Natural History. With climate change such a threat to coral reefs, this is important work to accomplish while it’s still possible. Some scientists expect 90% of coral reefs to die by 2050. Yet for the same reason, perhaps causing any danger to the coral reefs, even for data collection, isn’t worth the risk.

Do you think invasive environmental procedures like this are worth the scientific gain? What other ways can we use technology to look at the past and better understand the future? Join the discussion on Vialogues.
