The Sounds of Science

jvazquez

April 19, 2015


Even while in diapers, humans can appreciate music. What if a love of science was equally universal across ages? It’s a relationship Dr. Praga Chordia, former assistant professor at Georgia Tech and graduate from Stanford’s Center for Computer Research in Music and Acoustics, has been developing for years.

Music sparks creativity, but what are the neurological roots of the creative process? For example, what is happening in your brain when you improvise a jaunty, jazzy solo? It turns out brain scans show distinct differences within the same musician based on whether he is playing a composed piece of music or if he’s making it up as he goes. This supports the theory that you enter a uniquely creative state during improvisation.

Parag sees music as a catalyst for scientific thinking. He’s demonstrated this by developing a suite of music intelligence apps aimed to entice not just music lovers, but tech lovers, too (who are often one in the same). The video above highlights one of his projects, an app that composes background music to your acapella stylings. It’s too good to miss-- the app you never you needed.

The intersection of music, science, and technology has inspired endless creativity. Maybe Parag is on to something here…
