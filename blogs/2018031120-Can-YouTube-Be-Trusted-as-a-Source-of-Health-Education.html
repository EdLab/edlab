Can YouTube Be Trusted as a Source of Health Education?

Melanie.Hering

March 11, 2018


Due to its ability to provide abundant, easily accessible, and nonjudgmental information, the internet is often the first place we turn to with a question. The field of medicine is no exception, with about 35 percent of American adults consulting the internet for medical advice. Concerns about accuracy led a group of researchers to evaluate the educational quality of seasonal influenza videos on YouTube.

Between January and February 2017, researchers performed internet searches to accumulate all available videos on influenza. A scoring system was developed based on current Centers for Disease Control and Prevention guidelines, and each video was given a rating based on the quality of information provided. Negative points were assigned to videos with misleading information. Each source was further classified based on who produced and posted the video.

After analyzing 300 videos, results revealed that, while most videos were made by professional societies and healthcare providers who disseminated accurate information, these sources did not fulfill the studyâ€™s criteria for thoroughly educating patients. Additionally, videos that centered on alternative medical solutions propagated misleading information, which was exacerbated by antagonistic viewer comments about standard medical practices.

Though this study should provide some relief for any skeptics of the online medical world, it does demonstrate that educational medical videos are not a replacement for direct care or consultation. The medical community needs to create videos held to a higher standard if they want thorough healthcare information to be accessible to the masses.
