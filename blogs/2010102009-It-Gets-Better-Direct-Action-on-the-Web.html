It Gets Better: Direct Action on the Web

Eric Buth

October 20, 2010

There's been a lot of talk this week about Malcolm Gladwell's contrarian piece on the ineffectiveness of the internet as a meaningful tool for activists looking to bring about social and political change. There are endless reasons why his argument is weakly supported, patently false, regressive, and over-all indicative of Gladwellian thinking, but I was presented with a simpler counter point just the other day. This is Google employees' contribution to the It Gets Better Project. The ability to speak directly to gay youth in this case, without the filter of educational institutions, parents, and peers or the need for anyone to overcome their fear of being physically present with outreach workers or at community centers, is unbelievably powerful and possible only on the internet. Because the EdLab is very much in both the video and internet businesses, what can we do to leverage this kind of direct communication with learners? So much of our work, grant based and otherwise, is in the service of institutions, initiatives, or indeed Teachers College itself. So much of our content is behind pay-walls or registration requirements. Is their a more powerful tack we could be taking, one that cuts mediators out of the loop? If we agree that the internet is an important tool for activists, how can we think like activists to improve the educational potential of the internet?
