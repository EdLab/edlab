Curriculum Development for Films

Nantwi

January 13, 2009

The latest World War II epic, Defiance, is not only causing a stir with critics but with teachers as well. The film, starring Daniel Craig (the latest James Bond), is about two Jewish brothers who helped save more than 1,200 Jews by organizing a hidden, mobile village in the Polish forest. High school teachers across New York City are using the forthcoming film to teach a special unit about the Holocaust. “Very often students, when studying this history, think of Jews as been very passive,” explained Alice Braziller, a teacher at Manhattan's Satellite Academy High School. Organizations such as the Jewish Partisan Educational Foundation are hoping the film does shed light on resistance fighters during the Holocaust. The group has even developed an online curriculum for the film and believes that “young people can stand up against tyranny and make a difference.” EdLab's research and development team spent a great deal of time last term developing curriculum for movies with an historical theme such as Boycott (about the Montgomery Bus Boycott), Sand and Sorrow (conflict in Darfur) and Iron Jawed Angels (suffrage movement), among others. I have seen a growing trend in the development of classroom materials for historical movies and documentaries. Amnesty International, PBS and Time magazine have all developed curriculum for various films and documentaries. I am wondering if this is going to be a continuing trend in education (with heavily funded corporations getting in the act).
