Trends in Ed 03.24.10 Online Publishing and Revenue

Jeannie Crowley

March 24, 2010


Both commercial and educational institutions are struggling to develop new revenue models for online publishing. I outlined the New York Times paywall strategy in a previous Trends in Ed. Ars Technica and Nature News rolled out two very different online revenue models this week.
Nature News: Pay or Leave
Following a click from my RSS feeder, I was greeted with the first paragraph of the article and the image above. Nature News decided to roll out its new model (pay $8 per article or $199 per year) with no warning and no access to any free content. I revisited the article today and all content is free again. It seems the "pay or leave" model was not as successful as Nature News had anticipated.
Ars Technica: Please Turn Off Adblocker
Due to the technical nature of the blog, Ars Technica has a larger number of subscribers who use adblockers than other blogs. Since most of the blog's ads are pay-per-view not pay-per-click, adblockers significantly decrease revenue. Ars Technica decided to explain how the site's revenue is generated to the readers and ask the community to whitelist the blog to keep it alive. The vast majority of the 2019 comments on the article were statements from users that they would turn off adblocker. This simple approach of honesty with the reader may have a big impact on the site's ability to generate revenue. In addition to the pay-per-view ads, Ars is offering a pay as you go subscription for $5 per month. This feature (which is particularly attractive to users who don't want to pay a big yearly subscription at one time) gives subscribers:

  • Exclusive access to insightful Ars Technica content.
  • Live chats with Ars editors, industry innovators, and a seat at the editorial roundtable.
  • Ad-free layouts, single-page articles options, downloadable PDFs and access to full text RSS feeds.
  • Subscriber only forums with HTML markup and special notification options.
