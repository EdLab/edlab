Land Science, an Epistemic Game

Mable Yiu

August 29, 2013


Epistemic games have become a great way to incorporate project-based learning for students both in the classroom and at home. Recently, professor and game scientist David Shaffer visited EdLab to discuss how we can teach and test innovative thinking using epistemic computer games. Not only do these computer games put players in the shoes of engineers, journalists, lawyers, and more, epistemic games teach students the digital and communication skills they need for their futures.

In the epistemic game, Land Science, middle school and high school students play as interns at an urban and regional planning firm. Using a variety of tools such as iPlan and Google Maps, players work together to solve economic, social, and environmental problems in their cities. To do this, they work to develop plans and write proposals to please different stakeholders in the game. Some of their tasks include making changes to fix urban problems and to attract more people into the city. Players are also given the option to ask questions and get advice from mentors through the chat tool. This video demonstrates how project-based learning incorporates collaboration, problem-solving skills, and technology to help facilitate learning in classroom and home environments.
