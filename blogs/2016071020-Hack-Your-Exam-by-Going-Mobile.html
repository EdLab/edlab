Hack Your Exam by Going Mobile

dinosossi2

July 10, 2016


College students always look for an edge during finals. And high-stakes assessments might tempt desperate test takers to seek a little help from their phone. A recent study investigated potential flaws in exam situations that allow mobile devices.

The study revealed five hacks that might give professors pause before allowing phones on test day. First, copying contents of USB to hard disk allows examinees to copy and take the test out of the room. Second, a USB keyboard hack retrofits a typical USB keyboard with additional features, such as prohibited study notes, when plugged into a laptop.

Third, a virtual machine runs on another computer’s operating system where a connected third party can see screenshots over a cell network and provide answers through a Skype call. Fourth, modifying software runs a parallel version of the exam software, but includes pre-made essays. And the the fifth hack is a cold boot attack, when exam content is dumped onto a hard disk during a computer reboot and later resold. In short, hacking exams is as simple as it is cheap.

As educational institutions explore exam options, they'll need to weigh tech features (like providing students with multimedia tools and added support for learning disabled students) against their vulnerability to hacks. For tech savvy students, mobile devices giveth and taketh away, even entire exam papers.

Dawson, P. (2016). Five ways to hack and cheat with bring-your-own-device electronic examinations. British Journal of Educational Technology, 47(4), 592–600. doi:10.1111/bjet.12246
