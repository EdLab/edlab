Research Digest: Automation and the Modern Library

LauraCostello

September 27, 2011

Breeding, M. (2011). Transformations in Academic Libraries Demand Transformed Automation Support. Computers in Libraries, 31(4), 27-29. Description: In this paper, Marshall Breeding explores the growing rift between the needs of the modern academic library and the technology available to support those needs. He begins by examining the point of impact, the move from physical to digital materials served with outdated organizational models. Our ILSes are no longer the comprehensive systems they once were and libraries are increasingly relying on a splintered suite of resources to deal with the increasing complexity of our collections. The library user is now also presented with a fractured interface, a multitude of search bars and discovery platforms sprawled across a rapidly diversifying field of resources. The information and immediacy of these collections is richer than ever, but it's much easier to get lost and the proliferation of options may discourage the scholarly rigor of a limited collection. The author goes on to consider the potential of rethinking library automation, with the resources available today libraries could create a greater structure of support for long-neglected areas like public service and rethink the requirements and capabilities of our current systems. EdLab Relevance: Clearly, the ideal solution to this issue is for librarians to work closely and be involved with the planning and building of library-specific technologies! Most libraries are not so lucky. I'm not so certain that fracturing these systems from the back end is all that detrimental to our profession. Certainly the interface the patrons see should be data-rich, unified, and simple to navigate. It's an efficiency issue, but change and variety are good for learning and good for librarianship.
