Latino children's literature: A brief history

Veronica Garza

January 26, 2012

In 2008, 79 children's books were published with Latino themes.  Not bad, you say?  Well, it is when you come to realize that about 15,000 children's books were published in that year alone. The world of Latino children's literature is pretty much unknown to me, which is why I am taking a course on this very topic this semester.  It's somewhat of a calling to fulfill a knowledge-void I experienced while going through my teacher education program in California.  As you might have guessed, the time period between the 1920's through 1970's were  filled with books portraying Latinos in stereotypical ways--whether it be in writing or through the book's illustrations.  There were a few exceptions, of course.  One exception was Pura Belpre, a Puerto Rican librarian who worked at the New York Public Library and made it her mission to bring culturally authentic literature to the children in her community.  One of her most notable books is Perez and Martina: A Portorican folktale, which we have here at the TC library.   During the 1980's and 90's a surge and push for multicultural children's literature began to bring more authenticity to Latino children's literature, as well as an increase in the number of Latino themed books. If you want to learn more about Latino children's literature, you can check out the publishers below or the websites pertaining to Latino children's literatire awards.
