Budding Entrepreneurs Share Their Take-aways

Janice Joo

December 13, 2012

Last night, I was at Google's NYC office, where IncubateNYC had one of their MVP Fairs. The MVP Fair marked the end of a 5-week bootcamp for a cohort of about a dozen budding entrepreneurs. Their final task was to pitch their incubated ideas! The format was like a networking event: beer, pitches, and more ideas. I saw cut and paste bristol-board presentations... iPads loaded with photographs, landing pages, and even functional websites! I even saw a good old printout of Powerpoint slides. People pitched with whatever they had. Originally, I went in looking to see if there were any new edtech ideas, but I didn't see any education-focused ideas. Overall, this cohort was comprised mostly of fashion, real estate, and lifestyle startups. So I pivoted my own agenda, and began asking the bootcamp graduates about what they thought was the most valuable lesson learned in the last 5 weeks. Here's what some said: Invest in customer development. Make something the consumers really need and want. And don't be afraid to pivot when you find out it just won't work. One of the startups, I learned, began as a digital soap opera, and only 7 days ago was pivoted into a lifestyle app. This makes me even more eager to try to get on the list for Steve Blank's Lean Launchpad block course at Columbia. After all, he's the guy who came up with "Customer Development" in the first place... Get a mantra (not to be mistaken for slogan). A mantra is sometimes not even a sentence-long, but a catchy phrase that guides many of the daily decisions of a company. I learned that Nike, whose slogan is, "Just do it", has a completely different mantra for its employees: "Authentic Athletic Performance". Check out this recent Fast Company article about mantras. I think we ourselves need a mantra, for EdLab and even for individual products, which each act like its own startup. This comment is an extension of Laura Scheiber's recent takeaways from Social Enterprise Boot Camp.
