Philanthropy Brings Humanity to Technology

DebraLee

September 25, 2017


A prosthetic arm can cost upwards of $80,000, making the technology unattainable to many amputees. Easton LaChappelle, who made his first robotic hand out of LEGOs, fishing wire, and electrical tubing at 14, has invented a 3D-printed, brain-powered arm that he operates with his mind.

The RoboArm caught the attention of President Obama, and led to an invitation to NASA where LaChappelle worked on the Robonaut project. Now, LaChappelle has started his own robotics business, Unlimited Tomorrow Inc., with a mission to empower amputees using an intuitive, scalable model that creates custom prosthetic limbs. By making his designs freely available online, bigger companies can bring his innovations to market at an affordable price, and ensure the life-changing technology reaches those who need it most.

Open source sharing also means that engineers, students, teachers, researchers, and anyone who is interested can visit Unlimited Tomorrow’s website, download the files, and print the parts on a 3D printer. LaChappelle’s prototypes are built for home printers and his modular designs enable the prosthetic joints to be interchangeable, so users of the technology can create a limb that executes multiple movements. This generosity is philanthropy at its best. Should big companies be taking a page out of LaChappelle’s book? Share your thoughts in the ongoing discussion on Vialogues.
