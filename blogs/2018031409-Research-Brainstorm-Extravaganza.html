Research Brainstorm Extravaganza

jvazquez

March 14, 2018

This week's D&R focused on our future research initiatives, and it turned out some fantastic ideas. Here's what we came up with! 


Part I. Research Questions 

Prompt: "What topic would you like to explore, and how might you go about doing so?" 


1. Library Website/App


  • What are the demographic differences between in-person requests for library information from users of the app versus the website? 
  • Are there differences between the types of requests that come to the library via the app versus traditional face-to-face requests for information? *
  • Do researchers prefer using the library website (desktop) or the app (mobile) in conducting literature reviews?

  • Suggested metrics: Users that visit the site 4x/week

  • Suggested variables: Age, location, job, articles read, topics, time
  • How can we encourage patrons to keep using the app even after they've graduated from school? 
  • How can the app trace users' activities and then push tailored content to them? 

    • Suggested method: Data parsing tool; machine learning chatbot to trace activities 

  • What are the instructor behavioral habits over an academic year on the library app vs the library website? 


2. Learning Theater

  • How can configurable space facilitate learning engagement? * 

    • Suggested variables: interaction time, biometric data, space configurations, etc

    • Suggested method: Statistical modeling
  • Are people more cognizant of their movement when they are being tracked? * 

    • Suggested method: 2 groups, 1 being tracked and 1 not being tracked. Any difference?

3. TCR

  • What are the demographics of TCR readers who stay on an article for longer than 1 minute? 
  • Using a dataset of TCR articles from 1967-2017, what trends in educational research have fallen out of favor in recent decades? **

    • Suggested method: Simple stats, crosstab

  • How do topics vary throughout TCR's history
  • Do topic variations correlate to TCR editorship, historical events, etc.? *

    • Suggested variables: Editorship, historical events

    • Suggested metrics: Topic modeling (TM)

    • Suggested method: TM then calculate TM correlation with editorship and events


4. NLT

  • What are the characteristics of NLT power users? *

    • Suggested metrics: Users that visit the site 4x/week

    • Suggested variables: Age, location, job, articles read, topics, time
  • How would readership on NLT be affected if we did not have the sign in page? ** 

    • Suggested method: Take away sign in page for a month and compare numbers to previous months. 



* Crowd favorite 

** Voted "most impactful" 


Part II. Research Outreach

Prompt: Aside from the traditional academic routes, how can we communicate our research findings to interested parties? 


Traditional routes, defined

  • Peer-reviewed publications
  • Conferences
  • Classes
  • Social media
  • Mainstream media 
  • TED Talks
  • Blogs
  • Communities of Practice


Problems with tradition

  • Often behind a paywall
  • Not accessible (either because of the dense nature of the material or is literally not acquirable due to licensing issues)
  • Homogenous audience
  • Mainstream media often simplifies results to appeal to the masses, without taking into account more complex dimensions
  • Publishing in a prestigious journal is honorable but the process is time- and effort-consuming
  • News promoted via social media can be biased and is subject to misinformation 


New ideas!

  • Multi-pronged approach: While appeasing the academic masses is still a priority, we also want to appeal to non-academics who appreciate learning new things about learning. So in addition to standard publications, this approach may include a YouTube series and Wikipedia entries. 
  • Tinder for research: Swipe left or right on suggested articles to get a truly personalized reading (and learning) experience. 
  • Research ads: Create short, sweet, and entertaining summaries of our research and post these in places where the unassuming masses would most appreciate the reading material; places like train cars and subway platforms. 
  • Create fun media: Infographics, animations, cartoon series, video series (like America's Next Top Researcher), trivia/game shows. 


Thank you to the D&R team for getting us thinking about new avenues to explore both in terms of research topics and the way in which we share our work. 


I now turn to the rest of you! What are you curious about? Is there a research project you think we should take on? Do you have creative ideas on how to connect to the larger education research community and/or the general interested public? If so, let me know in the comments below!
