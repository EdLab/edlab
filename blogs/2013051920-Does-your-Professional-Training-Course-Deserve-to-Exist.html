Does your Professional Training Course Deserve to Exist?

Laura Scheiber

May 19, 2013


Does getting information into employees’ heads really change work behavior, and ultimately improve the way an organization functions? While expert design instructor, Cathy Moore , does not underestimate the power of information, she advocates for a paradigm shift when thinking about e-learning for professional training. Too often, organizations think problems can be solved by dumping information in employees’ heads and consequently they come up with an information-oriented online course.

Moore argues for a less information-centric focus and more activities designed to maximize behavioral change in the work context. She encourages employees to ask some fundamental questions when designing a professional development course, such as: What is your goal? Why does your course deserve to exist? How can we measure if your course has an effect? She encourages the development of courses that focus on practicing behaviors and activities that participants need to embrace in the work setting. She believes that instructional designers are responsible for designing an experience, not information. Have you ever designed or participated in a professional training course that was activities-focused? If so, let us know about it on Vialogues.
