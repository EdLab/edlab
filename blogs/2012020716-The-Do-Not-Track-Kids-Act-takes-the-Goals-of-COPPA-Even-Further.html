The 'Do Not Track Kids Act' takes the Goals of COPPA Even Further

Kate Meersschaert

February 7, 2012

H.R. 1895, or the Do Not Track Kids Act of 2011(1), if passed, will stop marketers and companies from tracking the behavior of children and young adults both online and on their mobile devices. The European Union recently introduced a "Right to be Forgotten" and "Right to Data Portability" law aimed at protecting adults, and this new US legislature is one of the first, along with Children's Online Privacy Protection Act (COPPA) in the late 90's, to be aimed specifically at children and teens. COPPA was sufficient for the pre-social-networks online world, but with the advent of Facebook and Twitter the rules of the online playground have changed. The Campaign for a Commercial-free Childhood (CCFC) outlines the following goals for H.R. 1895: -Prohibit internet companies from collecting personal information from anyone under 13 (and young adults) without parental consent. -Stop companies from sending targeted advertising to kids and young adults. -Require websites to offer an “eraser button” that allows parents to control their kids' information, and young adults to control their own information.
