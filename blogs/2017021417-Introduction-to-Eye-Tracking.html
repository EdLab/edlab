Introduction to Eye-Tracking

xk2120

February 14, 2017

Below are some observations from a recent workshop at TC on eye-tracking. What is eye-tracking? Eye tracking is a technique to measure when and what we are looking at. It takes advantage of infrared technology to show the pupil by reflecting the light of the retina. Why are we using eye-tracking? In comparing with other enterprise resource planning (ERPs) methods (EEG, fMRI, etc.), eye-tracking is natural with no disruption and provides information on anticipatory and late processing. It allows researchers to address subtle questions. How does eye-tracking work? It sends out infrared light and uses a high-speed video camera to pick up the light reflecting from the pupil. The camera signal is sent to the host laptop, which then provides real time feedback from participants with image analysis, data recording and eye event parsing/configuration. What is eye-tracking for? Eye-tracking serves as a direct control medium rather than data collection. It is used in marketing research such as advertising, website and application user face interaction, sports, game design, and product package design. It is also used in academic studies, such as linguistics, reading disabilities, neuroscience, and psychological development. For a personal perspective, eye-tracking could indicate cognitive load change by measuring the blink frequency and pupil size (need reference to confirm this information). Limitations of eye-tracking It is very expensive to get a good eye-tracker (costs range from $10,000-$40,000) and buy the software. It also takes a lot of effort to prepare a valid experimental design. The output of the data is very complicated to analyze.
