2011: A Bad Year for Dictators

Nantwi

December 29, 2011

The death of North Korean leader Kim Jong Il wrapped up a very bad year for dictators and despots all across the globe. Laurent Gbagbo-former World Bank economist turned politician who refused to give up power after losing in presidential elections in fall 2010. Was captured by rebel forces in April and is now on trial at the International Criminal Court in the Hague. Muammar Gaddafi-was one of the longest serving leaders in recent history (42 years) until he was moved out of office by a rebel led NATO supported uprising in August. Was captured and killed by rebels in October. Hosni Mubarak-took power after assassination of then ruler Anwar Sadat in 1981 and ruled until nonviolent protests drove him out of power in February. Currently on trial for a host of charges including murder. Ali Abdullah Saleh-Yemen's ruler for the past two decades. Resigned nearly a month ago following widespread demonstrations. Survived an assassination attempt earlier this year. Ben Ali-Tunisia's ruler for the better part of the past 20 years. Was ousted by a popular uprising that heralded the start of the “Arab Spring”. Currently living in exile in Saudi Arabia. Than Shwe (Myanmar/Burma)-head of Myanmar's military junta since 1988, he finally ceded power to civilian rule, paving way for national elections and the release of Nobel Peace Prize winner Aung Suu Kyi, who had been placed under perpetual house arrest for the past 20 years. Manuel Noriega-former Panamanian dictator who was arrested by US troops over 20 years ago. Had served the majority of his prison sentence in a Miami prison until he was transferred to France to serve out charges he faced there. Finally returned to Panamanian authorities a few weeks ago to serve the rest of his life in prison.
