The Matthew Effect: Distance Learning Edition

Fred Rossoff

October 17, 2013


Title: Student Perceptions and Achievement in a University Blended Learning Strategic Initiative (2012)

Authors: Ron Owston, Dennis York, & Susan Murtha

Source: The Internet and Higher Education

Study Design: Although the low cost and accessibility of online learning has led to a great deal of optimism over its egalitarian possibilities, thereâ€™s a growing realization that things might not be so simple. Without the structure and support of a classroom, many students sink. The authors of this study took advantage of a major expansion of blended learning at York University to examine the relationship between student achievement in blended learning courses and their perception of the ease and usefulness of such courses.

The authors administered a questionnaire to a sample of 500 students in 11 blended learning courses, and then compared their answers to their final course grades and cumulative GPAs. The questionnaire was designed to discover if students felt that the blended learning courses offered better experiences, increased engagement, and improved learning.

Findings: The consistent results were that high-achieving students found the blended learning courses more convenient, more useful, and more engaging than regular courses, whereas low-achieving students generally preferred traditional face-to-face courses.

Moving Forward: The challenge moving forward is to find a way to make use of blended or online learning courses, with all the scalability, personalization, and cost reduction that implies, without leaving behind a substantial portion of the student population. Further investigations that delve into differences in class size, different combinations of online and face-to-face content, and so on, should prove useful.
