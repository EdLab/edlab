Trends in Ed: Juvenile Cyber-Criminals

Fred Rossoff

December 9, 2011

It always takes time for bureaucratic regulations to catch up with social and technological advances. A textbook example is in Minnesota, where online students are playing hooky left and right, and the truancy law, as written, doesn't technically give the state the power to reign them in. Of course it's much harder to self-motivate as an online student, and without strict oversight, slacking off seems almost inevitable. The issue, as The Republic writes, is that “The definition for a "habitual truant" is a child under 16 who has unexcused absences on seven days in elementary school, or for one or more class periods on seven days in the higher grades. There's nothing about the flexible scheduling of online schools nor any provision for academic progress.” Online schools in Minnesota have developed a formula, with time spent on lessons and academic progress as inputs, to determine who should be classified as truant, but some county attorneys are going to require more explicit legal rulings before they'll put them to use.
