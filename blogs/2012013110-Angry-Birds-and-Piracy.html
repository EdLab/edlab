Angry Birds and Piracy

LauraCostello

January 31, 2012

Maybe it's because he's taking out all his frustrations on those smug pigs, but chief exec of Rovio Mikael Hed has a surprisingly healthy attitude about the rampant merch and app piracy around Angry Birds. During a speech at the Midem conference in Cannes, Hed described the change moment when his company stopped thinking of its target market as “users” and started thinking of them as “fans.” The process of growing a community of infectious appreciation embraces piracy that increases visibility and sends signals of affection and identity around a product. It's important to note that Hed's major concern isn't pirating of the app itself, it's currently free, but his attitudes around adaptation seem to be gaining traction in the production industry. It seems increasingly short sighted to resist when so much can depend on the right parody video and views become profitable, even as the product itself loses value. This is the theory behind DigSin, a newly developed music label centered around a free mailing list with daily free music. The price is, of course, paid to advertisers with your eyes but the model might suit an internet accustomed population. In bombarding users with content, something just might stick.
