Trends in Ed: Plucky Orphans

LauraCostello

August 25, 2011

Cornell, Duke, Emory and Johns Hopkins University have recently joined the Orphan Works Project, headlined by HathiTrust and the Copyright office at the University of Michigan Libraries. HathiTrust now works with 50 university partners to offer student access to digitized collections. The Orphan Works Project aims to identify and digitize works in these collections that have no clear or contactable copyright holder but are still under copyright. John Wilkin of the HathiTrust believes that orphan works may represent half of the 9 million volumes currently accessible through the Trust. The digitized orphan items will be available to university communities and aim to replicate stacks viewing privileges to align with the wishes of copyright holders, should they be found. The Michigan project is important in establishing a standard of dealing with orphan works in the digital age and in the wake of the Google Books ongoing settlement(s). Issues of access and copyright are becoming more complex with the possibilities of permanence and availability of physical items entering the digital realm. The differences between a dusty box in the basement and Pocket Knowledge or the HathiTrust are excellent in terms of access, problematic for copyright especially in the case of non-book items. The materials team has recently been looking into the copyright of a VHS series held by our archives and I've been surprised at how much more complex it is to locate copyright holders and understand copy making provisions in the case of film. So complex indeed, that it's a post for another day.
