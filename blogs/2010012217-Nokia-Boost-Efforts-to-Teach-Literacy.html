Nokia Boost Efforts to Teach Literacy

Adrienne Garber

January 22, 2010

Nokia has recently provided 450 cell phones and a research grant to Carnegie Mellon University's Mobile and Immersive Learning for Literacy in Emerging Economies project (MILLEE) in effort to teach reading and writing skills to students in rural India. Started by Dr. Matthew Kam in 2004 as his Ph.D. thesis at the University of California, Berkeley, the MILLEE project staff develops mobile educational games that teach Enlgish language skills which are deployed on cell phones. The rationale behind the project is that cell phones are ubiquitous, even in poverty-stricken villages that do not have access to personal computers or the Internet. Nokia's donations will allow MILLEE to conduct a controlled study involving 800 children in 40 villages. Researchers hope to understand how to design phone-based games for developing countries. In addition to India, MILLEE is planning to develop games that teach Mandarin in China and English in Kenya. For more information on the MILLEE project, ways to donate, or get involved, visit their website at www.millee.org.
