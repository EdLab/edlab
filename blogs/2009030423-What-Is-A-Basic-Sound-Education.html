What Is A Basic Sound Education?

Nantwi

March 4, 2009

I've been doing some research on the legal battle for fiscal equity for New York City's public schools on the Campaign for Fiscal Equity (CFE) website (created and hosted by the Gottesman Libraries) and it got me thinking about landmark legal decisions around school finance. The first landmark case to argue for financial equity for public schools occurred in 1978 when poor school districts in Long Island and New York City filed a suit challenging New York's education finance system. The case, Levittown v. Nyquist, was decided four years later. In their decision against the petitioners, New York State's appeals court argued that the state's constitution does not mention equal funding when it comes to education though it does guarantee every student a right to a “basic sound education.” There are a lot of sound bites when it comes to the legal community (i.e. habeas corpus), and a “basic sound education” is one of them. It sounds good and meaningful, but what exactly encompasses a “basic sound education?” Since many of us here at the EdLab have experience teaching in some capacity, I would like to hear some thoughts about exactly what constitutes a “basic sound education.”
