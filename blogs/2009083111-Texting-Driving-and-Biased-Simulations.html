Texting, Driving and Biased Simulations

Jeannie Crowley

August 31, 2009

A recent study by the VirginaTech Transportation Institute found that truck drivers who texted while driving were 23 times more likely to be in a crash or near crash event. Drivers were observed while driving (naturalistic driving) instead of asked to simulate driving habits in a lab. According to the study, there are large discrepancies between data collected during simulations and data collected during naturalist driving studies. This NY Times text while driving simulation (unintentionally) highlights a few of the reasons for that disconnect: the designers of the game expect you to make drastic lane changes ever few seconds, it assumes the use of full words instead of texting shorthand, and ignores the fact that many texters have key locations memorized. While it does support the goal of the game (to scare you out of texting) the simulated driving patterns are not very realistic. This opens up the discussion of designer bias and simulation design and how those biases affect study data. Once you play the NY Times game ask yourself if the data collected from the simulation could be used to make generalizations about texting and driving. Of course I don't support dangerous driving habits, but I'm leery of data derived from poorly designed simulations. (Yes, I know they call it a game but it's really a simulation. .......that is a whole other discussion!)
