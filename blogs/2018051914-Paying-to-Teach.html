Paying to Teach

ABARRY

May 19, 2018

For many public school teachers school supplies are another monetary cost added to their spendings. Pencils, pens, crayons, and pair of shoes are supplies often purchased by high school and middle school teachers to ensure students have the essentials tools to learn. Unsurprisingly, teachers in high poverty schools spend more than those in prosperous schools. A report by the National Center for Education Statistics found that 94 percent of teachers pay for classroom supplies, accumulating $479 a year. In addition, about 7 percent of teachers spend more than $1,000 a year. The cost can be demanding for teachers who make insufficient salaries. Educators in some states are among the lowest paid employers in the country. Many teachers state the number of needy children seems to be growing. Many of them spend several dollars buying winter clothing and birthday cakes for children whose parents cannot afford them.


Students should not rely on teachers to provide school supplies. Congress is currently sponsoring an effort to expand teachers tax deduction for classroom supply spending. Giving a tight classroom budget and low salaries, increasing this deduction could be a start.
