The Dark Web and the Erosion of the Presumption of Innocence

dinosossi2

July 5, 2017


Security threats seem to prompt the worst impulses of the state. And as an example of the proportionality of force, the greater the threats, the greater the diminution of liberty. It’s an old chorus to an even older hymn of overexuberant nationalism.


In response to recent terrorist attacks in the UK, law officials are turning to the dark web as an ecosystem of particular interest.


Long the domain of web-based scoundrels (think drug dealers, arms dealers, and other shady characters), this less-trafficked portion of the internet is causing the law to increase its efforts in surveillance. In this case, officials are asking people to become informers to identify possible terrorist threats.


For those who are concerned with their personal privacy, Tor is a dark web portal that can help provide them with some sense of privacy on the web.


And given the relentless incursion of corporations and government into our private lives and personal liberties, having a few places of privacy left to find some sense of refuge seems like a reasonable response.


The loss of a presumption of innocence by simply visiting a dark web site is another example of the loss of our privacy writ large.


I guess we can still send printed private messages with pigeons.


At least for now.
