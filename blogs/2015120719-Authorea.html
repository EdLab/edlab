Authorea

Jenny Shen

December 7, 2015


Google Drive and Microsoft Word both have commonly used features that allow for writing collaboration, but Authorea seeks to add another featured element to the mix—collaborative academic writing. With its web-based, blog-like interface and array of unique features specifically for researchers, Authorea provides a new way for collaborators to work together when conducting research.

Pros:

Authorea includes a wide variety of formats and modalities. It allows users to write with LaTeX, Markdown, HTML, Javascript, and basic Word documents. It also gives users the option to mark drafts with different tags, such as rough draft, working draft, and peer-reviewed. Collaborators can also chat in the sidebar while working together, and Authorea will archive the chats so that they can be referenced later on. Authorea is also specific with its word count options—authors can choose to count by full article, abstract, figures, references, or any combination of these options.

Users can also browse other people’s articles, which are organized by subject—like epidemiology and climate science—and provide good examples of how others have utilized some of Authorea’s unique features. From these articles, it is clear that Authorea offers opportunities to embed everything from equations to infographics to interactive diagrams within academic writing.

Cons:

As most edtech companies do, Authorea uses a freemium model that gives users the opportunity to make an article private—but just one. Those who wish to keep all their working drafts private will have to either upgrade to premium (which runs at about $5 a month) or invite three or more colleagues to get an extra free private article.

Our Takeaway:

Authorea is uniquely situated to provide users with academically-oriented collaboration software including features that cater specifically to researchers who need to juggle citations, integrate graphics, and work with numerous coauthors in their research. Though Authorea may not be geared toward grade school students, it can be introduced to them early on to provide a glimpse of what academic collaboration looks like in the realms of authorship and research.
