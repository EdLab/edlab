Research Digest: The Science Standards of the States

Fred Rossoff

February 6, 2012

The Thomas B. Fordham Institute has released a report on the “State of State Science Standards” in primary and secondary education. They evaluated each state's standards for content and clarity, concluding that the majority of states have fairly poor expectations for their schools' ability to teach science. The major problems seem to be: A. The ongoing culture war over evolution. B. Vague criteria. C. Too little attention to the process and culture of science d. Not enough mathematics. Every state gets its own report card, and the scores cluster in the C's and D's range. New York, luckily, does rather well, with an overall score of 8/10. I'm not sure, however, why the causal relationship is assumed to be all one way. Presumably the variation in state performance on educational metrics implies that some states are more capable of living up to high standards than others. Therefore, the variation in standards quality might simply reflect different states' estimates of what their students are capable of learning. Then again, California and the District of Columbia are the only ones who got A's… Edlab Relevance: As the report details, a common set of Science Standards (analogous to the “Common Core” standards for English and math) is in development, and already endorsed by 26 states. Resources for these Science Standards will therefore face from the start a large and eager market.
