"Keep blowing my mind": Demo Night SSK Preliminary Results (Part 1)

Kate Meersschaert

December 16, 2013

Private! We (Brian S. & Kate) sent-out three SSK surveys the week after Demo Night, to invitees, attendees and presenters to gauge their experience and gather feedback. So far (the surveys close 12/30/13) we have received an 11% completion rate for those who attended and a 7% rate for the survey to those who did not attend. What feedback stands out from the "attended" batch as most relevant to you? Here is some of the feedback we received from attendees by question (# sample 67, # of respondents 8): Q: What was your favorite aspect of the event? What was most valuable to you?

  • I loved that the event was right in your offices. It really felt like participants had real access to what you're doing and the people that are doing it.

    Q: How can we better facilitate networking after the demo portion of the evening? What other models have you experienced and liked?
  • Make sure to position someone at the door catching people as they leave.
  • Either give participants a chance to stand up and very briefly introduce themselves - or at least ask anyone in the group who wants to - people looking for collaborators or possible clients would probably stand and make a statement this works well at one group event I attend regularly. The other option is to have topics posted in different corners of the room - or at tables - and have a moderator at each location to facilitate conversation - the goal is to give people a way to start talking to each other. Could do 15 minutes blow a whistle and people switch to a new group for another 10 or 15 minutes. The real goal is to get people talking and learning about each other.

I think also identifying who the job seekers are versus the people with open positions ahead of time would be helpful so people know who to seek out before the start of the event. Not exactly sure how this could be effectively implemented at the moment, but the identification is important as many people come to events such as these to network for jobs, look for people the could potentially work with, or find people to fill certain positions.
