Trends in Ed, 3.30.09

Arianna Choi

March 30, 2009

Teaching Evolution: a debate that's larger than the size of Texas The long-contested curriculum debate over what extent should Darwin's evolution theory be taught in American science classrooms is back under the spotlight. Last Friday, the Texas state board of education yet again tried to settle the debate on whether or not Texas teachers should continue to teach both “strengths and weaknesses” of the evolution theory as required. The board agreed to delete the 20-year-old controversial phrase written in the states' curriculum standards, declaring a victory for evolution proponents. But later on the same day, the same board voted 13-2 to pass an amendment that requires teachers to scrutinize “all sides of scientific theories.” So it now sounds like a win that might actually be a loss, especially considering that the board also approved a requirement that challenges the widely accepted Big Bang Theory. Texas students will now be expected to learn that there are conflicting theories on the origin of the universe as well. Some important implications of the new standard are clear and present: - Texas science curriculum standards will likely resonate across the US since the state only approves a selected set of textbooks for the entire state. - According to the Wall Street Journal, this makes the lone star state the nation's single largest market for high school textbooks. - The next textbook selection is scheduled for 2011.
