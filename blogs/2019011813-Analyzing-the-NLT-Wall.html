Analyzing the NLT Wall

Rob.Crawford

January 18, 2019

Here are some NLT usage stats for the past two years:

Total visits, not signed-in                               33,209     53,847               +62%

Every metric related to NLT overall is increasing, often by impressive numbers, while the metrics related to accounts and the wall specifically have been decreasing. It may be that the way people browse online is changing, as they become increasingly wary about signing up for site-specific accounts.


In our recent analytics meeting we discussed how readers need to feel a sense of trust before creating an account on a website and noted that a single article view may not be enough to win that trust, especially in light of changing user preferences. One idea was increasing the number of articles that readers can see, perhaps to 2 or 3 per day, or 10 per month. In general, I think we may also want to make some small adjustments to  the sign-up process, to emphasize it is a “free account,” and to reduce the number of clicks (by sending readers directly to the sign-up page) down from three to two.


Another idea was adding comments functionality for readers with EdLab accounts. (“Create a free account to comment.”) The bounce rate of the wall has increased, and the percentage of users who are signed-in has decreased. By restricting comments to those with accounts, we could keep our account functionality, with all of its benefits, while lowering the wall for the rest of our readers. Because commenting would require an account, I don’t think there would be too much spam, and the writers would be in charge of deleting any spam posted to their articles. Comments could also help build trust and a sense of community because we would be welcoming feedback and, where appropriate, responding to our readers. 


In addition, we are currently gathering data on users through other tracking means, and this data is also showing an extraordinary increase. Lowering the wall would vastly improve the quality of this data as well, because users could then navigate the site much more freely.

I hope this data provides some useful context as we consider the future of NLT in the year ahead.
