Economics & Education: Education and the Housing Bubble

Skanda Amarnath

June 27, 2011

As a student at Columbia, I am constantly confronted, and often agree, with the view "New York City is the greatest! Why would anyone live elsewhere?" But then the conversation devolves into "I mean really...why would anyone live in New Jersey?" Well there's the obvious reason too many New Yorkers forget about why plenty of families, including my parents, decided it was best to move to New Jersey from New York City: education. Simply put, suburbia, more often than not, offers more public educational opportunities than public schools in urban areas. It's a sad fact, given the advantages urban areas offer otherwise and the communities that cannot afford to move to the suburbs for educational opportunity. That's why the piercing of the housing bubble in suburbia should not be met with too much despair, as this WaPo article notes. As blogger Matt Yglesias constantly emphasizes, housing policy and education policy are inextricably linked. The quality of public education received in a school district is largely a function of property values in the district. Unfortunately, it takes a housing bubble of extreme magnitude to ultimately result in educational opportunity for a family like Liza Jackson's.
