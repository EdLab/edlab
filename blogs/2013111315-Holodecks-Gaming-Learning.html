Holodecks, Gaming, Learning...

Jamie Renee Smith

November 13, 2013

Hey all! I know Kate mentioned them in her post on #NYEdTechDay, but if you haven't yet checked out VoxieBox, it seems worth considering. How would we adjust today's designs if we could make their virtual aspects 3-D (if, sadly, still lacking the haptic technology to make them tactile/physically interactive)? What are the possibilities and limitations of gaming/gamification/game-based learning in a holographic environment? Immersive environments and game-based learning certainly share a lot in terms of theories surrounding motivation, engagement, authenticity, surprise, cause/effect modelling, etc etc. How can games address the unknown, however? When outcomes (of policy choices, cultural exchange) are not known, how do we structure environments/games to feel authentic and offer a sense of real consequences, without being reductive or predictive? I suppose, ultimately, this is all leading to the issue of Thornburg's "Un-Googlable Questions." Real-world learning often involves unpredictable or inconclusive results - or results that will only unfold with time and ultimately be subjective and interpretive. At the least, hypotheses or models are merely that before real-world tests. So how can we structure virtual world learning to allow for surprise, creation, and, sometimes, indeterminate conclusions? Is that beyond its scope?
