Trends in Ed: The Unwinding of Cursive

LauraCostello

July 21, 2011

The exclusion of cursive from the English curriculum outlined in the Common Core Standards has been making news and raising ire of late. School Library Journal mourns it as a skill loss, but celebrates the end of penmanship shaming in schools. The exclusion of script raises questions about the diminishing importance of writing by hand and its potential effect on language learning. Some studies (1,2) have found learning advantages in physical script over the typed word when building early language skills. The differences in movement when writing letters are said to assist in memory formation over the similar movements for each letter in typing. These studies focus on early and second language learners working in print. The pro-cursive argument stems from tradition mingled with the notion that learning to fill a space with your marks is an essential human skill even in the digital age. Some states have chosen to retain cursive in their curriculum in a bid to ensure future access to script historical documents, but this is a message in itself: The way we communicate has always been changing.
