Trends in Ed 9.16.09

Jeannie Crowley

September 16, 2009

This post is a follow up to the online privacy concerns mentioned in Trends in Ed 9.9.09. Recent actions by Facebook suggest your information may be more secure than the President and news pundits have led us to believe. The Virginia Workers Compensation Commission (VWCC) tried to force Facebook to divulge the complete contents of a user's account to settle a dispute over workers' compensation. The user had supposedly posted vacation photos on her account that would help the VWCC prove she was fraudulently collecting workers compensation. Facebook responded by stating they are prohibited from releasing subscribers information without consent even if they are presented with a court order or subpoena in civil cases. What does this mean for the average user? As long as you use privacy controls to guard your own information, no one can ask a social network to divulge information about you. Not your university that suspects you of cheating, not your boss who suspects you of workers compensation violations, and not your neighbor who thinks you are the one who really stole his gnome law ornament. In contrast to the "be afraid" message being pushed about your digital DNA, perhaps the message should be "be in control". Educating students about how to manage their online presence seems like a more successful option than trying to keep them away from social networks.

One big caveat: The right to a user's online privacy mentioned above applies to civil cases. If you find yourself embroiled in a criminal case, don't expect Facebook to say no to the federal government quite so easily.
