NASA’s inspiration: Children’s toys

jvazquez

September 3, 2012


If we learned anything from the Apollo 13 mission, it was that life-threatening situations can be circumvented with quick thinking, creativity, and duct tape. While maybe not a universal truth, it brought to light the problem solving skills now representative of the most learning-centered and innovative government agency in the United States.

Today, NASA continues to build a culture of innovation through internal platforms (i.e., NASA@Work) and external collaborations (e.g., the NASA Innovation Pavillion on Innocentive). It presents complex problems and solicits ideas from employees, contractors, and volunteers. One such challenge was to create pioneering avionics software for NASA’s X-38 escape ship for the International Space Station. The escape ship is meant to bring crew members safely back to Earth in an emergency when there is no time to launch a shuttle to come to their rescue.

The challenge was met by a group of young engineers who were part of a Johnson Space Center (JSC) innovation team. Members of this team came from various departments within JSC: Orion, the Mission Operation Directorate, Robotics, and others. Each member added value to the team by bringing with them a unique set of skills.

The team put together a two-page presentation and a 60-second pitch for the JSC Innovation Board. The board liked what they heard and encouraged the team to create a rapid prototype of their idea. The culminating project is an example of quick work executed from an ad hoc team that came together based on a shared desire to solve a complicated problem. NASA’s continued encouragement and support of such initiatives contributes to its longstanding tradition of serving (and nurturing) a community of innovators. It is an exemplar for all institutions, organizations, and companies desiring to invigorate creative and entrepreneurial ideas that are so crucial in today’s modern times.
