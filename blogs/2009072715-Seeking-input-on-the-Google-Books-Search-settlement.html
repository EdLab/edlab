Seeking input on the Google Books Search settlement

Gus Andrews

July 27, 2009

My friend Dr. James Grimmelmann, a professor at New York Law School (whom you may remember from his appearance explaining fair use on The Media Show) has started a really ingenious project. Called on to write up an amicus curiae (friend of the court) brief offering his professional legal opinion on the Google Books search suit, James decided to seek the advice of a broader range of interested parties. He's doing so using a blog and a wiki. The suit has been brought by numerous publishers and authors, who claim that Google has violated their copyright by scanning tremendous amounts of published material and putting it up online. James's critique of the settlement as it stands has been that it puts monopoly power over publishing in Google's hands. It tries to solve what should be a legislation problem with a class-action lawsuit (to ensure that people whose books Google has scanned get paid). And it's likely to give Google unique control over "orphan works." But because James and his students have put their proposed brief up on a wiki and blog, anyone can recommend changes to the brief, raise questions, make additions, etc. As far as James knows, this is the first time anyone's had an amicus curiae brief vetted by the public online. For a simpler intro to the settlement, there's a series of videos up on YouTube.
