Search Engines

Jamison Judd

October 19, 2006

We've been talking a lot lately about pushing the envelope in different areas, especially in the context of research tools. As a starting point for finding information, one of the tools we use everyday is the search engine. I've compiled a selection of different "search engines" that I find innovative... but maybe not always useful. What do you use when doing online research? Google Scholar Google has a search engine specifically for academic (scholarly) research. A couple of the nice features are the ability to quickly search for articles that cite the selected article, and to see related articles. {example} Web of Science Web of Science is a search engine for published articles covering social science, natural science, and arts & humanities. A great place to start looking to define previous research done in an academic area. AlltheWeb AlltheWeb is an interesting twist on traditional search. As you enter your search terms, the engine guesses what you are searching for and automatically starts filtering based on category. This can help locate related topics that are worded slightly different than what you've entered. Kartoo Kartoo builds a visual map of the pages that match your query terms, showing the relevance using color and links to other related pages. Try searching for "edlab". Ms. Dewey Search the web by entering questions. Similar to Ask Jeeves, but with a saucy female search guide. Grokker Grokker provides an interesting way to visually organize and navigate search results in an outline format, or using a zoomable cluster map. Flickr Color Picker Academic, perhaps not... but this tool allows you to search images on Flickr based on their average color and tags (by category).
