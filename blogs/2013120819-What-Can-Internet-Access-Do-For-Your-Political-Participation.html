What Can Internet Access Do For Your Political Participation?

Fred Rossoff

December 8, 2013


Title: Digital Inequality and Participation in the Political Process: Real or Imagined?

Authors: David S. Morris & Jonathan S. Morris

Source: Social Science Computer Review

Study Design: In recent years, there has been a great deal of triumphalism about the democratic possibilities of new technologies. This narrative has always been long on hype and short on evidence. In this recent study, two sociologists used data from the Pew Research Center 2012 Political Communications Study to examine whether increased internet access had any effect on knowledge of, or participation in, the political process. This survey provided data from 1,500 respondents on their backgrounds, voting habits, internet usage, and political knowledge.

Findings: The authors of the study found through analyzing correlations in the data that higher socioeconomic status and greater internet access were both associated with more political knowledge and more regular voting habits. Delving further into the data, they found that the boost in both political variables from greater internet access only applies to those of low or medium SES. Even with the highest levels of internet usage, however, a gap still remained in political knowledge and participation between those of low, medium, and high socioeconomic status.

Moving Forward: As the authors point out, this study draws on data from the run up to the 2012 presidential election, and may not be applicable to times of lower general political engagement. In general, although this particular study offers little that is definitive on larger questions about the democratic impact of new online technologies, it does indicate that internet access can play a role in equalizing the political landscape.
