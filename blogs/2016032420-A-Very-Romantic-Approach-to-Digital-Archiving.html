A Very Romantic Approach to Digital Archiving

Rebecca Beeson

March 24, 2016


Students may not even know it, but they create and access digital archives every day, even outside of scholarly settings. The digital information ecosystem that they live in today is changing the way we talk and interact in the physical and digital world. Our language evolution is documented in digital records. Every retweet at @Kanye or romantic heart emoji text is a source of archive material, capturing the changing nature of language and communication.

The authors of a recent study had students create their own archive of evolving relationship language. By assessing and logging the differences in language between old love letters and the truncated text talk of modern romance, students explored how communication transforms over time. It encouraged them to appreciate the history of archiving and the changing organization of information. It also confirmed that the best way to teach students about archives is to get them to actively create and use them.

Students may not consider their interactions on Facebook, Pinterest, or LinkedIn as part of a digital narrative. The authors of the study hoped that this process would make students aware of their role in scholarly conversation and give them a greater understanding of their power in transforming language communications online.
