The Living New Deal

Ryan.Allen

October 3, 2017


"The Living New Deal aims to inventory, map, and publicize the achievements of the New Deal and its public works across all fifty states." (About The Living New Deal)

A Forgotten Legacy

At the height of the Great Depression, President Franklin Roosevelt initiated a mass push for public work projects across the United States, which is collectively called the New Deal. Despite almost every town, city, and county having infrastructure, artworks, or other buildings constructed through these programs, there is no national list or registry for all of these projects. The Living New Deal is a California-based non-profit, affiliated with the University of California at Berkeley, with a mission of digitally archiving and publicizing the memory of the New Deal. The organization is tracking every public work project throughout the U.S., archiving stories or other experiences related to the projects, and digitizing photos of artwork or architecture connected to the New Deal. The group hopes that their database and other resources will help to keep alive the legacy of these often forgotten government investments, as well as advocate against their destruction or privatization.

Interactive Mapping

The Living New Dealâ€™s interactive map is its most intriguing feature. Over 13,000 projects are overlaid onto Google Maps, allowing users to explore every location. Each project is clickable, with a photo, information on completion date, and current status. The map helps to illustrate just how pervasive the New Deal was for American life. Even as these works are all over 70 years old, they are not just gone and forgotten relics of the past. Incredibly, many public projects are still actively in use throughout the country today. This mapping tool is a great resource for teaching history or government policy through tangible, real-world examples.
