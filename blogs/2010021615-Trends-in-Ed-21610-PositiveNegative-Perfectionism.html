Trends in Ed., 2.16.10: Positive/Negative Perfectionism?

Ting Yuan

February 16, 2010

Are you a perfectionist? What do you think is the perfect way to teach or to educate your children? Are you an adaptive perfectionist or maladaptive perfectionist? There is an interesting paper just published in the January issue of the journal Personality and Individual Differences, in which the researchers describe an experiment that illustrates the importance of distinguishing between types of perfectionism. According to the study, perfectionism comes in two varieties: adaptive and maladaptive. And one of the key determinants of the type of perfectionism someone displays is whether the quest for perfection is intrinsic motivation or extrinsic pressure. The researchers surveyed 216 psychology students to assess their perfectionistic tendencies, as well as their psychological well-being and satisfaction with life. For example, if someone is driving hard because of his/her desire for excellence, that can actually lead to greater satisfaction, psychological health, and further improvement. But if someone is pursuing perfection because of pressure from others – parents, bosses, peers – that's likely to take this person to dissatisfaction and reduced well-being.

Adaptive perfectionism is an internal standard for achievement. Maladaptive perfectionism is an external concern — wondering what other people are going to think. It's kind of a thinking habit: ‘I made a mistake there.' ‘Someone will notice I didn't do that right.'
