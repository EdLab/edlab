How Do Adolescents Learn In A Fan Fiction Forum?

Ting Yuan

September 1, 2014


Title: Fangirls as Teachers: Examining Pedagogic Discourse in an Online Fan Site (2014)

Author: Jayne C. Lammers

Source: Learning, Media & Technology

Research Question: How is knowledge communicated within an online fan fiction discussion forum?

Study Design: This study was developed based on a two-year virtual ethnography following pedagogic interactions between moderators and members within The Sims Writers’ Hangout (SWH), a videogame fan forum of adolescents who read and write Sims fan fiction. The authors examined interactions and artifacts of members within and beyond SWH as well as guiding texts created by moderators. They further conducted virtual interviews with eight SWH members. Bernstein’s pedagogic discourse theory was applied to the data analysis of this study; it distinguishes two types of pedagogic discourse: instructional discourse that focuses on skills in a subject area and regulative discourse to create social order, relations, and identity.

Findings: Major findings point to the dominance of regulative discourse in SWH and the re-contextualization of content from other sites as digital literacy pedagogy in SWH. The authors argue that informal learning communities such as SWH create a culture of their own by regulatively communicating the norms and expectations to members using available web technologies; members of the site develop collective official knowledge that is constructed through the re-contextualization of content from multiple sites, including media genres, schools, and fan practices.

Moving Forward: This study contributes to the understanding of adolescents’ informal, collective learning in affinity spaces. It also shows the possibility of using Bernstein’s pedagogic tool for examining informal online learning, distinguishing instructional and regulative discourses and understanding the fluid knowledge repertoire created in such popular culture sites as SWH. In addition, the study highlights the need to include popular digital culture in school curricula as a means to bridge students’ learning experiences in and out of school.
