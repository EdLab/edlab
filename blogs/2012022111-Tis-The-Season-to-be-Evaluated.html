'Tis The Season to be Evaluated?

Janet Jang

February 21, 2012

Tennessee, Michigan, Oregon, Delaware, and New York are just a handful of more than a dozen states following a nationwide movement to overhaul current teacher evaluation systems. It was only last Thursday when New York State teacher's union and education officials came to an agreement on how the new system will look like: push more impartial, third-party independent observers in the classroom to rate teacher quality. The inspiration came from New Haven, Connecticut's new system that somehow effectively encouraged teachers to leave without being fired. "Thirty-four New Haven teachers designated “in need of improvement” – less than half of whom had tenure – exited the system last year, but they had chosen either to retire or resign, according to the officials." EdLab Takeaway Whether or not this new system will make a difference in evaluating teacher effectiveness is still skeptical. Other school districts that implemented similar systems have been struggling with logistic and philosophical issues. In Tennessee, teachers feel pressured to change their lesson plans to meet the evaluation criteria - which may not necessarily be a bad thing - but this approach is STILL encouraging the "teaching to the test" type of philosophy that has failed education so far.
