Autonomic Nervous System

Bismark Appiah

April 21, 2013

Today's blog is a continuation of the function of the human nervous system. So far, I have shared information about the central and peripheral nervous systems and how they operate. In the PNS, there are two subdivisions: the somatic nervous system, which connects to sensory and skeletal receptors, and the autonomic nervous system, which regulates the internal organs and glands. Just like the PNS, the autonomic nervous system also has two subdivisions: the sympathetic nervous system and parasympathetic nervous system. These two systems are very essential because they help adjust the body to changing circumstances. The two systems work together but in opposing ways. The sympathetic division prepares the body to expend energy. For instance, when there is a situation whereby you have to fight or flee, your sympathetic nervous system comes into action. In this case, sweating, breathing more deeply, and the increase of your heart rate and blood pressure is controlled by the sympathetic nervous system. On the other hand, the parasympathetic division of the autonomic nervous system restores and conserves energy. An example will be that if you jump out of the way of a speeding motorcyclist, the sympathetic nervous system increases your heart rate. Afterwards, parasympathetic nerves slow your heart rate down and restores it to the normal rate. Basically, the sympathetic system acts like the acceleration of a care, while the parasympathetic acts like a brake. However, it does not stop the body but slows things down.
