Designing Online Learning For Student Collaboration

Ting Yuan

November 9, 2014


What are the types of online interaction designs that students experience in online courses? How do educators and institutions promote students’ active learning through those designs? These are the lingering questions facing higher education in the digital age. A recent study applied quantitative and qualitative analyses to examine students’ online interactions in 19 university-level online courses as a way to understand the instructional designs for students’ online interactions.

The study revealed that there were three types of designs focusing in turn on students’ peer communications, student content reflections, and instructor-student communications. Among them, the design that promoted students’ peer interactions led to more interactive, reciprocal online discussions. This pattern was found consistently across different academic disciplines, indicating that the content of discussion did not impact the deep-learning effect of students’ online interactions. In addition, a balanced and inclusive design which included all of the three components was found in particular to promote students’ reflective learning discussions.

Students across different age and culture groups were found to demonstrate similar levels of participation and performance in their online discussions. The findings support the idea that online distance education has the potential to provide equitable learning experiences to students of diverse backgrounds. For designing online courses and the digital components of blended courses in higher education settings, this study is a helpful resource in considering different instructional designs.
