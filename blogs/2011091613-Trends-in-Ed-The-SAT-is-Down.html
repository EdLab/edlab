Trends in Ed: The SAT is Down

Fred Rossoff

September 16, 2011

Trends in Ed: SAT down On Wednesday the College Board released the scores for the 2011 high school class, and, unfortunately, they seem to be down a little bit. Paradoxically, this decline in average scores is paired with an increase in high-end scores (students scoring above, for instance, 700 on the math section). The College Board argues that the expanded pool of SAT test takers easily explains the decline. They have substantially increased their provisions of fee-waivers for low-income test-takers, which may pull in more marginal or under-performing students. To give an example, “roughly 27 percent of the 1.65 million test-takers last year came from a home where English was not the only language, up from 19 percent just a decade ago.” One explanation for the increase in high-end performance is that, unlike other racial groups, Asians have seen substantial gains over the past 10 years. I initially thought this might mean the Asian population in America has become more “native,” and thus would do better on the verbal or writing sections, but it looks like the gains have been across the board. The College Board has its own rough “College Readiness Benchmark” of 1550 out of 2400 (if you do that well or above, you have a 65% likelihood of getting at least a B-minus average freshman year), and only 43% of test-takers reached that score. So if we want a population of this size to benefit from college, we may all have to learn something from the Asians.
