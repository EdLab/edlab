EdLab Review: Alice 3

Kate Meersschaert

December 5, 2011

Not to be confused with Columbia Universitie's student health Q&A site, Alice 3 is a free, decade-old computer programming teaching aid (the third version recently launched & is reviewed here), geared towards young women. The virtual learning environment is the brainchild of Carnegie Mellon University faculty, in particular, the famed and much missed CMU professor Randy Pausch, as well as an assortment of other institutions, including Stanford. Learn more about the team behind Alice 3 here. Inspired by a need to engage young women in computer programming, Alice was created and still endeavors to allow users to develop, animate and interact with 3D worlds using simple drag & drop, object-oriented, production-focused and text-based programming languages and actions. Learn more here. Pros: Alice is a great introduction to computer programming for young women, as the STEM subjects continue to experience a decline in female graduates, this is still, a decade later, an important initiative. The drag & drop method of interacting with the interface to "program" your avatar to make a variety of movements all but eliminates the frustration of coding for learner who might otherwise be turned-off by the intricate rules of programming. Instead, users are exposed to basic high-level concepts and through a fun, engaging interface, can receive instant design gratification and feedback from the system. Cons: The interface feels dated and seems a bit more like a clunky version of Second Lifethan an engaging world for young learners. Also, the Alice logo reminds me a bit of a Bratz doll & is perhaps not a great, universally appealing image for young women. I know that this project is over a decade, old but as we move into an era when we need to encourage young women to participate in STEM subjects even more, perhaps CMU and their partners could re-invest design time in further updating the look & feel of the program. EdLab Relevance: As we consider ways to engage learners in virtual learning environments, this is yet another example of the benefits of low-barrier to entry design and the value of simplifying a normally complex process for the sake of an immersive introduction. Our Takeaway: Alice provides a great example of multi-university collaboration, and shows us how a long-term project can grow and continue to be relevant when the basic premise is relevant to a growing population of users and the basic feature set is strong... 10 years is a long time for a learning environment to exist in any form! Perhaps this is a great model for EdLab to follow as we develop and track out projects and eventually seek-out partners at other universities.
