Engineering Better Engineers

Fred Rossoff

May 7, 2014


Title: Integrating Cognitive Science and Technology Improves Learning in a STEM Classroom (2014)

Authors: Andrew C. Butler, Elizabeth J. Marsh, J. P. Slavinsky, & Richard G. Baraniuk

Source: Educational Psychology Review

Research Question: Can cognitive science principles improve college STEM instruction?

Study Design: Although the research literature provides numerous examples of effective classroom interventions for improving student learning, few of these pedagogical practices are simple or cheap enough to scale effectively. By using lessons from cognitive science, the authors of this study hoped to identify a few simple and easily implemented instructional tools that improve student learning and allow for mass adoption.

The study involved 40 college students enrolled in an upper-level engineering course at Rice University called Signals and Systems. The authors identified three cognitive science principles (repeated retrieval practice, spacing, and feedback) to use in modifying the courseâ€™s standard pedagogical practice. This resulted in students receiving more homework, revisiting past assignments over a several week period, and receiving immediate feedback on their work, all mediated through the OpenStax online tutoring system. The students were split into two groups, each group receiving the enhanced instruction only on alternating lesson modules.

Findings: For lessons in which the enhanced pedagogical tools were applied, students scored significantly higher on the relevant exam material. The effect size, although not enormous, seems to indicate increased learning, along with a greater willingness to view (and take to heart) teacher feedback.

Moving Forward: The increased attention, feedback, and coursework this study recommends could conceivably end up being too costly to implement. However, as the authors point out, the widespread availability of instructional technology, like the OpenStax software used in this study, should allow for greater individualized attention and instruction, and at less cost.
