News Presentation in the Age of the Dynamic Web

Nara Kasbergen

December 20, 2012

Ever since news websites starting supplanting newspapers as a news source, publications have struggled with what this means for how they should present their content. Initially, this led to a direct translation of print content to online content, often with an accompanying transfer of the visual styling; over time, the trend has moved towards a greater focus on legibility and other optimizations for screen-based consumption, as well as adding interactive media content such as video and data visualizations. But with the increasing availability of HTML5, the rediscovery of JavaScript as the most powerful language for dynamic web content, and a general surge in new client-side and server-side technologies, there's still a largely untapped well of possibilities for rich interactions with content on news sites. Today the New York Times, always pioneering and working to stay ahead of the curve, launched one of its newest experimental designs to accompany a story about a deadly avalanche: Snow Fall: The Avalanche at Tunnel Creek. I really don't want to give any spoilers, so just check it out for yourself! What are your thoughts? Do you think the design adds to the content in a meaningful way, or do you view it an overly flashy attempt to show off new technology? Do you get the impression that this design will transfer well to other content, or is it too specific to this story? And most importantly, are there any take-aways for how we can design our pages and our content for articles on New Learning Times (NLT)?
