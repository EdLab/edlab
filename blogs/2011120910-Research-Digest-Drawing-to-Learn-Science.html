Research Digest: Drawing to Learn Science

Janet Jang

December 9, 2011

Ainsworth, S. Praise,V. & Tyler, R. (2011). Drawing to Learn in Science. Science, 333, 1096-1097. This study argues that student drawing should be recognized alongside writing, reading and talking as a key element in science education. The three-year study shows that when teachers help students generate their own representations of â€˜expert' science concepts, the students' understanding of science can move from the superficial to the sophisticated. However, in the current science classroom, students mainly focus on interpreting others' visualizations; when drawing does occur, it is rare that learners are systematically encouraged to create their own visual forms to develop and show understanding. I remember from my own high school science classes that drawing only extended as far as making line graphs, drawing chemical structures or sketching microscopic cells. EdLab Relevance: For the development of future tools, one area we can explore is how learning with new technologies can be connected with drawing. Technology is also broadening our concept of drawing as learners create animations or use cameras and clay models on drawn backdrops to generate 1-s stop-frame movies of science processes. Just wanted to throw out there that Vialogues is a great tool for giving feedback on "video-d" content, so could there be a user-friendly platform where freehand sketches of students can be automatically marked to provide timely feedback as well?
