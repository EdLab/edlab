Talking About Problem-Solving Can Increase Math Scores, but Not Necessarily Confidence

Melanie.Hering

April 23, 2019


Math requires a lot of mental problem-solving that isnâ€™t necessarily reflected in the final answer to a problem. This is why math educators often require students to show their work to demonstrate that they fully understand important concepts. In online courses, there are even fewer opportunities to observe problem-solving in action. While online educators can talk about problem-solving, researchers wanted to know how this discourse translated to math learning and performance for elementary school students enrolled in online courses.

In order to determine how participating in discussions about math problem-solving affected students' math abilities and confidence, researchers analyzed platform data from two fully online virtual elementary schools in the United States. Nearly 900 students in the third, fourth, and fifth grade participated.

Participants engaged in small-group online sessions where they encountered new math problems and discussed reasoning and problem-solving with a facilitator. At the end of each discourse session, participants completed reflection assessments that measured their math confidence, self-efficacy, and growth mindset. The researchers also collected participant course grades and state assessment scores.

Results revealed that though student participation in the discourse sessions was associated with higher final math course scores and state assessment performance, math confidence, self-efficacy, and growth mindset scores did not change significantly. This shows that while discussions about problem solving can have learning benefits, more work is needed to determine how best to increase student confidence.

Choi, J. & Walters, A. (2018). Exploring the impact of small-group synchronous discourse sessions in online math learning. Online Learning, 22(4), 47-64.
