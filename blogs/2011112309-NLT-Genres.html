NLT Genres

gnatriello

November 23, 2011

Keep Private For the New Learning Times we imagine a portfolio of genres of material that allow us to achieve the substantive goals of the publication in a financially feasible manner. Our initial set of genres at launch will include the following: A) Trends in Education — articles that identify current developments that suggest the transformation of the education sector is underway (1000 words max) B) EdLab Review — a highly structured review of new products and services in the education sector. The components of the review and the evaluative criteria should be shaped to be consistent with the NLT agenda. (500 words max) C) Research Digest — brief synopses of academic research reports relevant to the transformation of the education sector (500 words max) D) Start-up Spotlight — descriptive accounts of new companies being started to provide products and services relevant to the education sector, possibly including interviews with the founders/leaders (1000 words max) E) Picture Perfect — still photos suggestive of some aspect of the transformation of the education sector F) Budget Buster or Saver? — short articles assessing the financial impact of new products, services, practices in the education sector (500 words max) G) Repost — brief intros to pieces appearing elsewhere worth bringing to the attention of NLT readers (200 words max) H) Feed — information sent automatically from EdLab apps (e.g., latest video loaded into Vialogues, just published on Pressible, newest member of EdNode community, new in the PK archive, etc.) The experimentation with content genres should be planned from the outset with a serious data gathering component. Data should be collected on both the costs of production and the impact on readers. We can begin with some simple metrics such as the dollars per view to indicate the dollar cost to generate a single view of one of the content pieces. Over time we can develop more sophisticated measures that include the spillover effects generated by a piece that attracts someone initially and then causes them to move to other pieces. The same kind of analyses suggested here for genres can also be applied to topics.
