You Don't Rush for 296 Yards by Worrying About Failure

HSC

November 7, 2007

This past Sunday, Adrian Peterson, rookie running back for the Minnesota Vikings (and former Oklahoma Sooner), set a new single-game rushing record with 296 yards against a much heralded San Diego Chargers defense. So how does a running back, a rookie running back nonetheless, gain 296 yards on only 30 carries (that's almost 10 yards a carry) in only his eighth professional football game? There are the obvious answers: athletic genius, good line play, wide receivers blocking downfield, smart play calling, missed tackles, luck, etc. But I found this explanation in ESPN to be just as important. In the article, Easterbrook describes how Peterson's mental approach to football is just as important as his physical abilities. Rather than worrying about everything that might go wrong on a play, Peterson focuses his gaze on the endzone. Instead of being paralyzed by thoughts of the collapsing "box" or would-be-tacklers, Peterson is "always looking at the goal line and driving his legs, ignoring tacklers." Peterson's accomplishment reminds me how important it is to seek success rather than avoid failure. I am not suggesting individuals or organizations seek out failure or "run without a good gameplan." But an overwhelming fear of failure potentially links us more closely with the likes of Ryan Leaf rather than HOFs (Hall of Famers) like Walter Payton.
