Profit Maximizing

Bismark Appiah

April 3, 2013

In the business realm, the ultimate goal of every firm is to generate profit. To gain profit is also one reason why people make investments. In my macroeconomics class, I learned that the amount of goods firms produce in a given period is to generate the highest profit possible. For instance, if a gloves company generates $10,000 from producing 5,000 gloves for a specific time period, then the gloves company will produce more than 5,000 gloves during the next production period, in hopes of obtaining a profit higher than the $10,000. This concept may seem obvious, however it is not, because for firms to perform profit maximization, they have to consider the level of supply and demand and costs of production. Thus, profit maximizing states that if profit is greater than marginal cost (the increase in total cost that results from producing 1 more unit of output), the firms will produce more of their goods. On the other hand, if profit is less than marginal cost, firms will cut back production. But if profit is equal to marginal cost, firms will leave output alone. In other words, firms will maintain the amount of goods they produce for a given period.
