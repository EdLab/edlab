Does YouTube Have A Social and Political Responsibility?

Nantwi

March 10, 2009

Bangladesh's government has blocked access to all content on YouTube with immediate effect after a recording of a meeting between the country's Prime Minister Sheikh Hasina and senior army officials was posted on the site. The meeting occurred in the days following a mutiny by border guards a few weeks ago that ultimately led to the death of some 70 or more people, including the mutinous guards and soldiers in the army. The government claims the audio recording of the heated meeting will further complicate the situation and it's in the “national security” interest of the nation to block YouTube for the foreseeable future. In a volatile region such as South Asia, the move will have its fair share of proponents while opponents will view the move as a violation of freedom of speech in a country that practices democracy. The news in Bangladesh is reminiscent of a blog post last year where I asked whether YouTube should contact the proper authorities when “questionable” material that could potentially jeopardize or further inflame a situation is posted on its site. Is YouTube morally and socially responsible to take such an action? Is the Bangladeshi government just in its decision to block YouTube?
