Thinking about Thinking

Evans.Asamoah

June 4, 2017

Learning, like most things in life, is a solo endeavor. We’ve all spent countless hours and nights tugged away in a quiet place slowly and meticulously going through notes, textbooks, and other content. This can be a very difficult process. We do this sometimes without a clear strategy about how to approach learning. When we learn in this matter, we sometimes gain very limited knowledge from the resources. I personally suffer from this as I’ve hours attempting to gain knowledge from resources provided by my teacher. However, I end up gaining very limited knowledge. 


An interesting study by researchers at Stanford hopes to shed some light on how to effectively learn and raise student grades in the process. The research team, led by Patricia Chen, a post-doc, wanted to have students think about thinking, metacognition. They essentially wanted students to strategize how to study. As the saying goes, failure to plan is planning to fail. Chen and her team believed that if students think strategically about thinking and tackle studying effectively, they will see a rise in their grades.  


Metacognition and feedback have been declared by the Educational Endowment Foundation as one of the two most effective interventions in trying to close the achievement gap. Metacognition is growing as an intervention tool due to its relative cheapness (to test as an experiment) and the potential benefits. Students (and everyone for that matter) can all benefit from metacognition.
