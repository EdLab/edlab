Robots Imitate Cognitive Development Capabilities

Jenny Abamu

October 24, 2016


New research shows that robots have the ability to demonstrate cognitive evolution. Using theories on child development, researchers have run tests with robots to grasp a full understanding of their programmable learning abilities. The positive results show that robots have the ability to recognize both humans and avatars from their behavioral imitations. Simply put, this means they now have the ability to demonstrate the learning process that takes place in the minds of most infants.

The researchers programmed the robots to scaffold recognition through the physical attributes and imitation processes of 25 adults, 30 children and 15 avatars. The robots were able to extract visual features, posture recognition, and novelty detection by having the participants imitate the behaviors of the robots (learning phase), engage in imitation games with the robots (validation phase), and then have the robots try to recognize the participants by reintroducing themselves (recognition phase). The research showed that the robots demonstrated the ability to detect novel behaviors using sensory motors and an auto-evaluation mechanism based on prediction error.

The researchers credit the coding in our bodies in relation to others that allows our behaviors to be imitated, novel, and recognizable. This experiment in developmental science using robots is an exciting (and intimidating) leap forward for the confluence of science and engineering in research and development for the future.

Boucenna, S., Cohen, D., Meltzoff, A. N., Gaussier, P., & Chetouani, M. (2016). Robots learn to recognize individuals from imitative encounters with people and avatars. Scientific Reports, 6.
