Economics & Education: The Limited Use of Peer-Review in Teacher Evaluation

Skanda Amarnath

June 9, 2011

Earlier I posted on Dana Goldstein's report on the glut of studies coming out against test-score based teacher evaluation and downplaying the importance of value-added. In her report, one of the suggested alternatives is peer-review teacher evaluation, in which fellow teachers help critique and identify a teacher's performance. Andrew Rotherham, of Eduwonk, writes on the limited use of peer-review since it can only help in identifying observable poor teaching (unable to control a class, unfocused students, etc), but not unobservable poor teaching. Rotherham seems to have this right. Getting back to my original point with Goldstein's report, educational output is not observable and will not be for a long time to come. Peer-review has its use, as does test-scores. Yet ultimately, we still do a very poor job of observing output in education. Standardized tests have far too many fixable flaws to be considered the absolute centerpiece to all employment and salary decisions. I'm for using all the tools possible, but ultimately there has to be a real reform of how we test students (more on that to come...)
