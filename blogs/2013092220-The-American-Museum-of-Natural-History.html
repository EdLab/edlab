The American Museum of Natural History

EdLab Studios

September 22, 2013


The resources at the American Museum of Natural History run much deeper than what the public eye sees. For the lucky participants in this acclaimed New York institution's Lang Science Program, these resources are opened up - and with them, a world of possibilities. For every Saturday and three weeks each summer between sixth and twelfth grade, a select group of would-be scientists participate in this intensive program, learning about the museum's foci - biodiversity, astrophysics, and anthropology - along the way. Their learning is led by museum researchers, and takes place in a collegial environment; all are there because they love science, and work is oriented toward greater understanding, so questions are encouraged and working for a grade simply doesn't exist. Groups develop yearlong research projects that, by the time they are in 11th or 12th grade, can be quite advanced in the field. Whether in the archives looking at artifacts from a recent archaeological dig, in the lab working on a research project on binary stars, or chatting with a museum scientist, these young researchers are experiencing one of the great science institutions of the nation in a deep and scholastic way that few people do.
