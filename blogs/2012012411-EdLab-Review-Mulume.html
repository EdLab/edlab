EdLab Review: Mulu.me

Kate Meersschaert

January 24, 2012

Mulu.me creates an intersection between a Pinterest-like, social pin-board and social-good-linked revenue generator. How it works: users "curate" a page with links to products and services they endorse and recommend. Users who generate traffic to these sites are then assigned "guru points" and given a percentage of sale if a purchase is made by this unique visitor. The social good component comes-into-play as participants are given the option to choose what percentage of this monetary reward will be given to a charity of their choice. Pros: The site is graphically pleasing and reminds me of a well-curated online shopping experience. The "guru points" seem like an interesting way to reward active, knowledgeable users without relying on the potentially over-played badge. Cons: This seems like a combination of Etsy, Tumblr and Pinterest... with the main differentiator being the "social good" aspect. I think the curatorial trend is here to stay, but perhaps there is a clearer connection to how both users and "curators" would benefit listed more prominently or explained through an infographic. At first glance, or without reading this AllthingsD.com piece I would have thought this Mulu.me was simply another slick e-commerce site. EdLab Relevance/Takeaway: As we move into the e-commerce realm with ResearchBroker, and continue in the world of information and identity curation with both Vialogues.com and Ednode.com, I feel there are important lessons to be gleaned from this site. 1) "Guru" status and points could be more powerful than a complicated badge ecosystem 2) Site design, especially in the Beta stage, needs to express clearly and immediately the value for the end user and the "curator" of information 3) A social-good component built-into e-commerce could be a great way to help various causes while supporting your own mission, whether to make the world more stylish or to make re-imagining education a more enticing cause.
