Goodbye, books

Michelle Pronovost

January 20, 2011

{{unknown}}
{{unknown}}

Get out your industrial shredder: humanity is officially over books. This is the popular perception I have seen cropping up in numerous news articles and other media outlets. Nicholas Negroponte says the physical books will be gone in 5 years (see above). My librarian sensibilities were particularly rattled by an International Business Times article which must have been written by a lazy teenager, predicting the demise of libraries. The writer states, "For starters, with Google around, who still needs to go to the library to look up information? Just type in any subject and a list of relevant websites will roll out" and "Besides search engines, a neater organiser of online information exists in the form of Wikipedia, which, with more than 3.4 million subjects, provides quick reference for just about any topic, issue or personality." I'm a Google devotee, but the majority of scholarly research literature is not free and discoverable; many libraries struggle to afford expensive databases that give students and faculty access to journals. I've explained my feelings about Wikipedia in a previous post. Happily, no one is making the argument that we will stop reading, unless people like Kanye (“I am not a fan of books. I would never want a book’s autograph. I am a proud non-reader of books.”) and Eminem ("Well, reading is the worst thing in the world for me to do. I hate it.") are able to wield their influence over the younger generations. An intriguing Wall Street Journal headline tells us that e-book readers are causing people to read more, even while kayaking. Good thing there are already waterproof cases on the market. The study on e-readers, conducted by Marketing and Research Resources Inc., found that 40% of device owners said they read more now than they did before purchasing their digital devices. The WSJ also points out an impressive figure from Amazon, that e-reader owners buy 3.3 times as many books after purchasing Kindle. Although this spike of e-book sales may be attributed in part to the novelty factor, e-reader owners also praise the convenience of toting a multi-volume collection on a thin, lightweight device.
