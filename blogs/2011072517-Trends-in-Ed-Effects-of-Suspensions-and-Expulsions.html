Trends in Ed: Effects of Suspensions and Expulsions

Nantwi

July 25, 2011

A recent study of Texas area high schools shed new light on the rates of expulsion in schools and its overall effect as a form of a discipline. One of the primary finds of the study is how identical school (size, demographics, etc. ) expelled and suspended students at different rates. Though researchers used over 80 variables such as test scores, attendance, race, expenditures per student and teacher salary and experience in their overall analysis of the students and schools, there was not a predominant conclusion, or any conclusion for that matter, on why identical schools produced different outcomes. The study started in 2000 and examined every seventh grader in the state for that year 2001 and 2002 and followed them for the following six years and more. In order to paint a fuller picture of the entire system, researchers matched their education data with juvenile justice records. The study concluded, among others, that suspensions and expulsions significantly increased a student's chance of dropping out of school and ending up in the juvenile justice system.
