"The Wave" washes onto U.S. shores

Joyce Britton

May 26, 2011

Independent film blogs are all atwitter about German filmmaker Dennis Gansel's The Wave, a teen political drama about a high school teacher's classroom experiment with totalitarianism. To sum up: During a lesson about Nazi Germany, Mr. Wenger's students express disbelief that thousands of Germans could blindly follow Hitler's fascist regime. The next day, he constructs “The Wave” - a student movement that emphasizes discipline and diminishes individuality with dress codes, a hand salute, and socialist sympathies. To Mr. Wenger's surprise, The Wave catches on immediately, but not without serious repercussions... Historically, The Wave has seen several incarnations, but it was originally inspired by a real-life experiment (not unlike the Stanford prison experiment) in the late 60s called “The Third Wave” Like the movie, the movement swept an entire California high school in a matter of days. A 2010 documentary called Lesson Plan features testimonies from the experiment's unwitting participants. Parts of Lesson Plan, including the trailer, can be found online. Does anybody remember watching the ABC Afterschool Special version of The Wave? It was shown to my junior high social studies class over several days, with no prior explanation from the teacher. I recall that my peers actually began to participate in the Wave movement (…albeit pseudo-mockingly), mimicking the hand motions and shouting the three mottos, "Strength through discipline! Strength through community! Strength through action!" It was then, of course, a bit disquieting to see the events of the film unfold, particularly at the climactic ending - when the students' "leader" is revealed to be none other than Adolf Hitler. The Wave, actually released in Germany is 2008, is finally nearing a limited U.S. release. View the trailer here.
