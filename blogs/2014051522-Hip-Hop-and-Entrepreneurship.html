Hip Hop and Entrepreneurship

Carmel Addae

May 15, 2014

Rap icon Dr. Dre and Apple are on the verge of finalizing a deal worth $3.2 billion to buy the former's Beats headphones. The deal, if as expected goes through, will make Dr. Dre hip-hop's first billionaire. The consequences of the deal could be far reaching for hip-hop. There are positive advantages that come along with this deal. It could make hip-hop an entrepreneurship powerhouse and inspire current and aspiring rappers to start their own business in addition to the music. For instance, Jay-Z and Diddy are well-established entrepreneurs with several businesses outside of music.

The ultimate goal is to promote hip-hop as a brand. More than any other artform in recent history, hip-hop (e.g. clothing, slang, etc.,) has captured the public's imagination for well over three decades now. Early founders of hip-hop proceeded as entrepreneurs and they used those entrepreneurial spirits to move the culture. They didn't promote themselves as professional musicians but to advance rap and hip-hop as a musical genre and cultural force. They did this with no supports from other institutions.
