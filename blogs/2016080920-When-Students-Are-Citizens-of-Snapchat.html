When Students Are Citizens of Snapchat

saraehardman

August 9, 2016


"I am as much of a citizen of Snapchat as I am of New Zealand," says the teenaged narrator of this video. And it’s true; if citizenship means spending time in a place, knowing its culture and rules, and speaking its language, then teens today are citizens of many social media sites, often with greater populations than the countries they live in. If Snapchat were a country, it would be the ninth most populated in the world; Facebook would replace China as the most.

The teen in this Vialogue speaks about the differences between a person’s online and offline self. Offline, we can’t edit mistakes or flaws; our unpolished personalities make us human. But online, we curate our identities. We crop, filter, and retake our photos until we have the perfect snapshot to share with our followers. The teen in this video urges others to be aware of this personality divide so we can be more ourselves and less of a likes-fueled brand.

This could have interesting implications for online learning and for classrooms, where teachers increasingly incorporate social media into their curriculum. How can educators work with this reality both in classrooms and online platforms? Does the same offline-online personality disparity arise in online classes? Join this important discussion on Vialogues.
