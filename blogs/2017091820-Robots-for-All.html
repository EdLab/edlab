Robots for All!

DebraLee

September 18, 2017


Georgia Tech’s Robotarium (Robots + Terrarium = Robotarium) lets researchers anywhere in the world write a computer program, upload their code into its robots, and receive video evidence of the experiment as their commands are carried out. The 725-square-foot lab, funded by the National Science Foundation and Office of Naval Research, houses a swarm of around 100 robots. Motion capture cameras cling to the facility’s ceiling peering down at the lab’s centerpiece: a white, bowl-shaped arena that looks like a 12’ x 14’ hockey rink where the palm-sized robots, which are wirelessly activated by researchers, roll, fly and scoot over the smooth surface day and night.

The Robotarium’s creators wanted to build a world-class, multi-robot lab for budding roboticists and overcome the cost barrier that prevents entry into the robotics field by using open access, enabling the development of the next generation of robots and robot-assisted technologies. The lab will soon add autonomous quadcopters to the family: the size of small dinner plates, the quadcopters will undertake remote flying experiments, whizzing through the air. A retractable net will keep them from slamming into walls or people if things unexpectedly get out of control, as the 3-D-printed robots work below.

User groups so far have been mostly roboticists and biologists without access to swarm technology. Georgia Tech’s Institute for Robotics and Intelligent Machines expects the Robotarium to foster greater collaboration within the robotics community, allowing scientists and engineers to share their findings more widely and build on successes.
