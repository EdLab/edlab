E-books vs. Hardcovers at Amazon

Ting Yuan

July 29, 2010

Reported by a recent New York Times article, Jeff Bezos announced e-books are outselling hardcovers at Amazon. One of the MIT libraries began to offer an iPad check-out option in May, 2010. During my recent trips to a local public school, I noticed that people read on their iPad while riding on the subway. My own reading experiences on the iPad is very joyful. There are a number of free e-reader applications available and each of them offer multiple types of free e-books, such as the Jane Austen collection, which reminds me of my teen years carrying shabby Chinese-version hard copies in a big backpack.

Printed and e-books have different affordances. The Myth of the Paperless Office (Sellen & Harper, 2001) mentioned with papers, mainly, readers can constantly see where they have been, spread out texts on a desk, and more conveniently find a spot in a text where they may want to return. For me, it also serves better, for example, whenever my adviser gets a book from her big book shelf and lends it to student. The conventional book reading experience may also make me "nostalgic", with the old but sweet memories of grandma's bed-time picture book reading, the thrill of holding new books at the beginning of each grade, and the childhood scribbles on hard copies. Will we soon forget the feeling of thumbing through a printed book? Will our old stories/memories happen differently for the new digital generations? The portable and searchable features of e-books are obviously convenient for readers, together with other evolving writing functions. I look forward to seeing the gains (and loses) as we transition toward new reading experiences.
