Limor "Ladyada" Fried

Kate Meersschaert

May 1, 2014


Engineer, "DIY/Maker" crusader and electronics evangelist, Limor "Ladyada" Fried, has done more to support STEM education than almost any other entrepreneur twice her age. After graduating from MIT with a Masters in Electrical Engineering completed at the Computing Culture Group in MIT’s Media Lab, Fried went on to found Adafruit Industries, one of the world’s first "community-led" electronics companies. Founded in 2005, Adafruit now has a team of over 50 with a 15,000+ sq ft. factory space in which the group creates original educational content and video tutorials while shipping thousands of "DIY" electronics supplies to home "makers" and institutions around the world. Fried also completed an Eyebeam fellowship, was the first female engineer to be featured on the cover of WIRED magazine and was named Entrepreneur magazine’s Entrepreneur of the Year in 2012.

EXCLUSIVE NEW LEARNING TIMES INTERVIEW

Question: How did your educational trajectory (background) affect your current work?
Answer: Working in and around open-source really influenced all of my work. The idea that you can publish and share software & hardware and others can build upon and improve is the how Adafruit was started.

Question: What professional experiences have been most formative to your current work?
Answer: Teaming up with Mitch Altman on the TV-B-Gone (amongst other projects) was, and is, great. Mitch goes around the world teaching electronics at hackerspaces using the kit and project we worked on together.

Question: How do you hope your work will change the learning landscape?
Right now if you're an electronics company a community of makers seek you out if you have great tutorials, videos and customer support - I would like to see more companies outside our industry adopt this approach. It's seems counterintuitive.

Question: What broad trends do you think will have the most impact on learning in the years ahead?
Answer: I think learning will need to move more and more towards online + video with projects as part of the goal. At Adafruit we're teaching electronics which is a really challenging subject; we've found that the combination of a well designed learning system (learn.adafruit site) along with a frequent video publishing schedule (visit Adafruit’s YouTube page) - all centered around completing a project - gets the most number of people learning skills.

Question: What are you currently working on & what is your next big project?
Answer: We're currently working on the new version of learn.adafruit; with the new updates we'll be able to have more people sharing their projects and interacting with hardware directly while they are learning!
