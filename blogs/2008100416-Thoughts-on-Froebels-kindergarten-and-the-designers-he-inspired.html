Thoughts on Froebel's kindergarten and the designers he inspired

Stephanie Hunt

October 4, 2008

With the October 8 seminar focussed on the design process (check out the "events" section for more info), I guess I've had design on the brain lately. I just ran across this interesting article by Bill Lucas, a user experience designer, called "Studying the Creation of Kindergarten." What a pleasure to see this exploration of Friedrich Froebel's educational approach coming from a modern design community. Indeed, the connection is appropriate. Froebel introduced a new way to formally educate young children, and many of the world's most famous designers were among the pupils of the first ever kindergartens. Students of these kindergartens included Le Corbusier, Piet Mondrian, Paul Klee, Frank Lloyd Wright, and Buckminster Fuller. (Read more after the break.) Who knows whether their experience in the Froebelian classroom can be credited with the professional success of these design heros. But I do think the observation provides an entry point for contemplation: how did Froebel's gifts (one is pictured above), and this focus on creativity and play, create an environment that supported a "designerly understanding" of the world? How might we learn from and build upon Froebel's century-old approach to learning and exploration, taking into account new tools, new technologies, and the new types of understanding inherent to our post-industrial world? If you're interested in learning more about Froebel's kindergarten, the Froebel Foundation website is a good place to begin. A couple of years ago, The Institute for Figuring organized an exhibition called Inventing Kindergarten. Their website includes some lovely documentation of period artifacts. And last, I want to share what I believe to be a nice starting point for considering my last questions regarding a design-based approach to learning in the modern day. Check out Constructionism in Practice.
