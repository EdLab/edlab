Young Children’s Digital Landscape At Home

Ting Yuan

September 8, 2014


Title: Preschool Children Creating and Communicating with Digital Technologies in the Home (2013)

Authors: Joanna McPake, Lydia Plowman, & Christine Stephen

Source: British Journal of Educational Technology

Research Question: How do children use digital technologies to support communication and creativity in their families and communities?

Study Design: This study is based on data collection around preschool-age children from 54 families at various points in the socioeconomic spectrum in Scotland between 2003 and 2011. Specifically, the data included field notes, interviews, mobile phone diaries, video recordings of families’ day-to-day lives, and informal conversations with children. The authors examined young participants’ encounters with a full range of domestic, leisure and work technologies at home, including their technological toys in comparison with traditional toys.

Findings: The authors developed vignettes of children’s digital literacy activities and how they played with digital technologies (e.g., TV, computer, video game, camera, film, karaoke) in creating multiple visual, oral, and physical texts at home. Contemporary technological toys were often naturally added to and remixed with traditional toys at home, and often served as a literacy toolkit for the participants in constructing multimedia, multimodal knowledge before they entered the official learning space—school. Major findings also suggest the evolution of the "digital divide"—rather than any binary judgment of access to technology, young children across the socioeconomic spectrum know and use digital literacies at home for various playful learning purposes.

Moving Forward: The authors argue that to meet the communicative and creative landscape in which children grow and operate today, there is an urgent need for educational developers and school administrators to bridge young children’s digital learning in and out of school. The "new" digital learning and the "old" traditional learning do not have to be segregated in the school curriculum; the new is neither an add-on nor an extension; the two learning modes can be remixed and integrated together in order to better address the needs of today’s digital generation.
