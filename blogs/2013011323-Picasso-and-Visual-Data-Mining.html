Picasso & Visual Data Mining

Sharon Hsiao

January 13, 2013

I just visited the special exhibition of Picasso Black and White at the Guggenheim Museum. It reminds me of the idea related to my earlier post about Visual data-mining: What Makes Paris Look like Paris?. If you are not familiar with the stream of Picasso's work, Cubism painting, let me succinctly describe it here in a couple sentences. It is a pioneer, an abstraction modern art of the early 20th century. Basically, cubism utilizes geometric shapes to depict objects and scenes. It was a revolutionary painting style at that time, as a new way of extending the boundaries in art. Later, historian actually categorizes Cubism into two phases, analytical cubism and synthetic cubism. So, what's the analytical cubism? The artists analyze the subject and reconstruct it within a geometric framework. I know art work is very subjective. But the analytic cubism art, is the first something that I feel like I can understand mathematically. The style itself is based on artists' analysis with object shapes. Can we transform those into features? Can we classify those features and reengineer a cubism painting? This just requires basic data mining techniques. So I did a little research, but so far, I do not see many applications out there, to develop and invest functional art. I think visual data mining is still a very new and hip topic and deserves more attention and endeavor. And it could be just a fun project to do.
