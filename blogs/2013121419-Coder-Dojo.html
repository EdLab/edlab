Coder Dojo

Kate Meersschaert

December 14, 2013


CoderDojo makes development and learning to code a fun, sociable, kick ass experience. CoderDojo also puts a strong emphasis on open source and free software, and has a strong network of members and volunteers globally. (From the CoderDojo site)

Training Future Coding Ninjas
Coder Dojo is a global movement to run free coding clinics and clubs for children and young people throughout the world. With participants in cities ranging from Tokyo to New York, the program aims to match interested learners with adult mentors who are willing to donate their time and share their programming skills. Languages taught include HTML, CSS, JavaScript, PHP, Python and more. Inspired by the traditional Japanese "Dojo" or designated place for instructors of martial arts to teach their students, the program aims to emulate this master/trainee relationship. In addition to mentorship, the program offers participants the chance to sign-up to assist with online projects on popular open software repository GitHub, experience industry speakers, tours of technology companies and collaborate "in-person" with other learners and mentors via Skype. Mentors are deemed "CoderDojo Champions" and award virtual badges and "belts" to participants who successfully gain new skills and advance their mastery of programming. The program clearly outlines the basic requirements and guidelines for programs to be officially labeled a "CoderDojo."

Unique in a Sea of Online Courses
More than the recent influx of for-profit online learning platforms, from Treehouse Learning (check-out the NLT Treehouse review here) to Udemy and even Courseraâ€™s new "Signature Track", CoderDojo stands-out as one of the only grassroots, free programs for young people to emphasize learner-led training. The program was co-founded in Ireland by James Whelton, who started the program in his school after gaining notoriety for hacking-into an iPod Nano, and Bill Liao, an entrepreneur and diplomat focused on sustainable development and learning. Both co-founders hope that the CoderDojo movement will help to "assist young people to learn how to be creators not just users." This goal to both teach and empower young people to become "masters" and help lead their own technology learning, is truly a unique proposition in the growing field of informal technology education.
