The following are notes from a discussion on media challenges in a new era. Panelists include Katrina vanden Heuvel (KV), Editor and Publisher of The Nation.



U.S. Media in the New Era - Part 2 – Q&A session

Date: Tuesday February 7, 6-8pm, IAB 1512 

420 West 118th Street, 15th floor


Overview:

What are the rights and responsibilities of the media in reporting the new presidency? Days after the inauguration, we will assess whether there have been fundamental changes in the relationship between the administration and the media and how the media needs to respond in order to effectively fulfill the missions of watchdog and agenda setter.


Panelists: 

Katrina vanden Heuvel (KV)

Editor and Publisher of The Nation


Karen Attiah (KA)

Global Opinions Editor of the Washington Post


Marcus Mrowka (MM)

Assistant to the President for Strategic Communications at the American Federation of Teachers


Moderator: 

Anya Schiffrin (AS)

Professor, Columbia’s School of International and Public Affairs 

Director, Technology, Media, and Communications Specialization


Q&A Session: 

AS:

-financial difficulties are making legacy newspaper houses collaborate…

-this development isn’t necessarily a bad thing…


Q: 

-non-profit news publishers, such as ProPublica, are seeing a rise in donations

-does this pose an ethical dilemma for non-profit publishers?

-are the lines being blurred?


A:

KV:

-The Nation has always been a for-profit news organization

-however, it has one made profit for 5 of 150 years…

-The Nation Institute is a non-profit institute

-it has turned to funds for specific issues like reproductive rights

-it also includes 17,000 small donors

-the Open Society Foundation has also been successful

-however, it keeps me up at night… 

-the idea of a wealthy proprietor who has a vendetta…


Q: 

-there is no apparent fact checking tool on the Facebook homepage

-has there been an effort to start changing digital outreach?


A: 

KA:

-to “decloud” the information environment, we share the Washington Post opinion pieces and label them as “opinion”

-but this type of practice is not always razor clear…

-digital outreach includes being as clear as possible regarding what the content of an article is

-it also includes an investigation at the fact checking level 

(e.g., the Washington Post hosts a “Can He Do That?” blog)

-we have become a lot more responsive regarding what people will be searching for…

-internally, we are more conscious of how we use social media

-regardless of what we post, we always represent the Washington Post

-if we have energy, we are looking to do more, we are looking to do altitude

-unfortunately, we can become trapped in minute-to-minute push alerts


Q: 

-what do you think about the idea of “framing”?

-for example, I am an international journalist based in New York and I have to report to my country regarding the U.S.

-how do we capture reality? 

(e.g., the Muslim ban is impacted by the intelligence ban)

-how do we ensure news covers both perspectives?


A:

KA: 

-we all need to continue offering various perspectives on important issues

-newsroom diversity is extremely important 

(e.g., we need to have journalists who are Muslim, women of color, come from different strata of income inequality, not just Ivy Leaguers, etc.)

-for example, my mother was a refugee
