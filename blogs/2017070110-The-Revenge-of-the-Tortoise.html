The Revenge of the Tortoise

dinosossi2

July 1, 2017


During the internet age, everyone is expected to be a hare.

First to post.

First to prototype.

First to market.

First, first, first. The tyranny of a relentless push for speed.

But is there a cost to this incessant focus on moving ever faster?


Malcolm Gladwell seems to thinks so.

In a recent debate, he advocated for the indispensable role of the hare in modern society. Especially for professional roles where the costs of errors are incredibly high.

Think airline pilots. Soccer goalies. Or surgeons.

And closer to home, think academic publishing. 

In Gladwell's language, the high performers in fields where mistakes are inexcusable are invariably tortoises. Those people who are highly conscientious and obsessed with attention to detail. Those who are error free, which is incredibly important with infinitely complex material that does not convey its meaning readily, but needs time to reveal its inner hidden secrets.

If you are measured by performance, speed is simply a single measure of process, but not of product.

When you watch a movie, enjoy a play, or read an article, you usually measure it by whether you think it is enjoyable or not.

You usually don't think about the speed with which it was made.

I don't think I've ever heard, "Boy, that film sure was produced quickly!" 

As the author notes, Apple is rarely the first product to market.

But it’s usually the best.

The tortoise strikes back.
