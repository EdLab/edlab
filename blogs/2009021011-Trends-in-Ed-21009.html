Trends in Ed, 2.10.09

jvazquez

February 10, 2009

Does this computer make my butt look big? This story blows my mind. A group from MIT's Media Lab, lead by Pattie Maes and student Pranav Mistry, have created a computer that can turn any surface into an interactive display screen. It is being toted as a “wearable computer”. The prototype consists of a webcam and a battery-powered 3M projector with a mirror attached. Mistry dons four different colored marker caps on his fingers so that the program (that he developed) can recognize and distinguish between them. This device allows him to do remarkable things: Want to know the time? Draw a circle on your arm and a watch face appears. Need to check email? Draw an “@” sign in the air. I can see this technology being used readily in classrooms to summon eBooks, newsclips, or even to follow through on a threat to call a student's parents (you can project a phone pad wherever you want…even on the student!). “The world at your fingertips” has never been so literal–and so inspiring. Youth and Web 2.0 What can young people get out of this fantastic web culture, besides facebook and memes? If they don't know, they should be shown. According to Howie DiBlasi, retired district CIO and a speaker at this year's FETC, technology needs to be embraced so that students are not only readers and writers, but collaborators and editors as well. Teaching “21st century skills” will allow students to be competitive. DiBlasi makes several suggestions for teachers to incorporate into their curriculum: collaborate via google docs, go global, use interactive video conferencing (via Skype) to connect to different classrooms. Another speaker at the FETC, Gail Lovely, recommends that teachers introduce products like Glogster and Voicethread as well as blogs and wikis. Says Lovely, “Ask, 'What are you going to do with these things? How are you going to use these tools?' iPhone App-a-Day iBlueSky allows users to mindmap and brainstorm ideas and thoughts. It has a very simple yet intuitive interface. Users can easily add new topics and reorganize links. The maps are easy to share with others, too; a touch of a button allows you to email the project. A mere once-over at my notebooks would suggest this is the product for me. If only I had an iPhone!
