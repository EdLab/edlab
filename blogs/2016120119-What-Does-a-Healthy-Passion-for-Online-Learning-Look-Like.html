What Does a Healthy Passion for Online Learning Look Like?

Jenny Abamu

December 1, 2016


The average teen spends around nine hours a day on the internet. The danger associated with extreme internet use is cause for concern. As online activities become more popular, new research points to a distinct differentiation among various types of internet usage that can have diverse effects on self-worth and social competence.

The researchers observed two types of internet passions within learning settings: harmonious and obsessive. Harmonious users engage in internet activities for the sake of personal enjoyment. They interact for the love of learning and participating in class and are not simply interested in rewards. Being online is an activity within their world, not the world itself. In contrast, obsessive users constantly seek validation through their internet usage. They engage with online learning as a means to seek approval by getting good grades or accolades for completing a task.

The results of the research show that obsessive passions negatively correlate with self-worth and social competence, while harmonious passions have a positive correlation. These conclusions are understandable as seeking approval from others online can be damaging. Fortunately, researchers note that educators can play a role in helping students shift from obsessive to harmonious passions. By emphasizing the importance of gaining long-term knowledge and encouraging autonomy in learning, educators can help students find their own strengths and pathways through online learning.
