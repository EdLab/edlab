Trends in Ed 02.10.10 "What is Reading?"

Jeannie Crowley

February 10, 2010

Yesterday's Trends in Ed explored the impact of digital resources and tools on a users' reading attention span. A 2008 study of online research habits suggests that the skimming of blogs and other online media has impacted the way we conduct research online. Users typically:

  • Hopped from one source to another.
  • Rarely revisted a source they'd already skimmed.
  • Read one or two pages of an article before bouncing to a new source.
  • "Power browse" through multiple articles rather than read any one article in its entirety.
    • If this is how users are accessing online research materials, how can universities change the way they publish articles online to fit with these new reading behaviors? What sort of tools can we create to make "power browsing" more meaningful? There is always the argument that we are enabling the skimming behavior (and therefore the lack of deep understanding of an article) by adapting our publishing methods, but sticking with the old methods won't force users to read full articles. Users will simply go to a new site that better supports their preferred method of research.
    Note: I didn't have the patience to read the full report. I skimmed this guy's summary. I willingly admit to "power browsing".
