Digital vs Analogue: Old Book Lending for New Content

LauraCostello

December 14, 2011

BookBrewer, an e-book publishing platform aimed at self-publishing, recently launched a nice rant to its authors in defense of libraries. Perhaps in response to the Overdrive hoopla* of late, BookBrewer has taken matters into its own hands and partnered with a small public library to launch a new e-book lending program meant to replicate print. BookBrewer's step is revolutionary in that it relies on direct publisher/library negotiations and effectively bypasses the vendor model born of the pre-Amazon days of print acquisitions. The public library in question will purchase, own, and host content provided by BookBrewer and use it in line with BB specifications using library-provided DRM. The library will lend the content and purchase additional copies based on demand. The Million Dollar EdLab Question... Do we have to reinvent the wheel for lending electronic content? It's been clear ever since the advent of e-books that there are major differences in the way users interact with these resources compared to physical books, though early Overdrive agreements operated on physical book principles like “damaged” copy replacement. Time will tell whether p-book based perpetual access purchases made in the early days of e-book lending will hold true in iterations to come. It's still unclear that digital content can be owned at all or if lending is the right action for content many users see as disposable. Physical/digital book equality was seen as a way to establish print library allowances as we began to think through the implications of this new content, but this way of thinking may be limiting as we move towards new arrangements in purchasing and lending born-digital content and digitization of physical collections. Previously unimaginable models like pay-per-use and demand driven acquisitions may characterize the digital lending of the future.
