One School at a Time

Nantwi

March 30, 2015


In some regions, one of the biggest challenges to education is just providing students with access to a classroom. In too many communities throughout the world, it’s not unusual to find students learning in run-down and makeshift learning spaces. This is compounded by other problems like weak government education policies and infrastructure, especially in remote villages where delivering supplies can be difficult. Often the responsibility of meeting basic educational needs is taken on by nonprofit organizations.

Pencils of Promise is one such for-purpose organization that is hard at work ensuring students in developing countries enjoy safe and clean learning environments. Initially focused on countries in Asia and then Latin America, the program now extends to Africa. In fact, the scope of their work has grown so much that they are able to build a school every 90 hours in impoverished communities internationally—an enormous task that is largely financed by individual donor contributions.

How are organizations such as Pencils of Promise able to build so many schools? And should this be the work of nonprofits, governments, or a combination of both? Join the discussion on Vialogues.
