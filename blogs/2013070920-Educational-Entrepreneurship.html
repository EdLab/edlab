Educational Entrepreneurship

Fred Rossoff

July 9, 2013


Title: Education and Tech Entrepreneurship

Authors: Vivek Wadhwa, Richard Freeman, & Ben Rissing

Source: Innovations

Study Design: Stories of innovative, immigrant entrepreneurs in Silicon Valley are a dime a dozen. Men like Elon Musk (born in South Africa) and Sergey Brin (born in Russia) have exercised enormous influence on the American economy. This paper tackles the less often discussed situation for U.S. born tech entrepreneurs. By studying the characteristics of these natives, we can get a better handle on what domestic policies help or hinder tech entrepreneurship in general. The authors of this paper gathered information from public records and interviews on U.S.-born founders of technology and engineering companies from the period 1995 to 2005.

Findings: Although, as expected, these U.S.-born founders tended to be highly educated in relevant STEM fields, there were a few surprises in the data. As the authors point out, the myth of the "20-year old wunderkind is the exception, not the rule." Most founders tend to be older, and to have spent a significant amount of time in the business world before launching their startup.

Moving Forward: The characteristics that seem to be correlated with successful tech entrepreneurship, including working in an innovative region, acquiring a serious post-secondary education, and having a great deal of work experience, all point to the critical importance of the environment we provide for would-be entrepreneurs. Without the educational and industry institutions to provide the knowledge, skills, and experience required, many innovative tech companies would never have been born.
