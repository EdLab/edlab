Powering Up iPad-based Learning

Ting Yuan

October 22, 2014


Title: New Directions for Early Literacy in a Digital Age: The iPad (2014)

Authors: Rosie Flewitt, David Messer, & Natalia Kucirkova

Source: Journal of Early Childhood Literacy

Research Question: Can iPads improve early literacy instruction?

Study Design: The authors explored how iPads might offer innovative opportunities for literacy learning in three early childhood settings in England, including a classroom of three- to four-year-olds in a city nursery, a classroom of four-to five-year-olds in a suburban primary school, and children aged above seven with learning disabilities in a suburban special school. Over two months of data collection, the authors conducted videotaped observations and pre- and post-study interviews with teachers examining their perceptions and practices on early literacy learning and new technologies.

Findings: Major findings suggest that young learners valued selected iPad apps as highly desirable artifacts that offered simulating and varied pathways into literacy as well as empowered them in both mainstream and special education. Specially, the young participants were motivated, concentrated, and independent in different ways when working with selected iPad apps.

In addition, the authors found that the boundary between "expert" and "novice" in the classroom became fluid; outside the constraints of traditional learning experiences, students used the touch screen to presented new identities such as "good speller," "good reader," "good drawer," and "speaker."

Moving Forward: In addition to using iPad apps for individualized learning center activities, further research and practice need to address the formation of a local curriculum and pedagogy to integrate the creative use of this and other digital tools as well as the creation of new classroom routines that highlight the daily use of interactive digital technologies.
