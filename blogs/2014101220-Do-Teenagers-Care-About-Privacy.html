Do Teenagers Care About Privacy?

Ting Yuan

October 12, 2014


Title: Networked Privacy: How Teenagers Negotiate Context in Social Media (2014)

Authors: Alice Marwick & danah boyd

Source: New Media & Society

Research Question: How do teenagers consider issues of privacy in social media?

Study Design: This study focuses on teenagers’ online privacy and sharing. The authors drew data from an ethnographic study that included 166 semi-structured interviews with teenagers and a series of participant observations accumulated across 17 states in the U.S during 2006-2011. The authors conducted multi-layered qualitative data analysis using interview transcripts, field notes, and ethnographic memos.

Findings: Major finding suggest that privacy is no longer an individual phenomenon; privacy among social media users cannot be entirely controlled due to users’ social participation as part of the networked public. Nevertheless, the authors found that the participants used various strategies to maximize their personal privacy, including segmenting their audience to restrict access to content and "covered writing" to achieve a narrower audience (e.g., deleting comments, logging on and off via deactivation in Facebook). The authors believe that information norms and contexts are co-constructed by participants and frequently shifting; instead of simply thinking of protecting privacy as individuals, there is a need to adapt the concept of privacy to collectives, groups that share friendship or other links.

Moving Forward: In a networked world where privacy is no longer an individual issue, group dynamics and online social norms influence the processes of information disclosure, concealment, withdrawal, and interpretation. Adolescent’s relationships have always offered opportunities for teaching and learning. Online social worlds present new learning alternatives. Further research can inform developers and educators to help students understand the complex privacy possibilities available online.
