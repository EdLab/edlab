Better Living Conditions for Lab Animals Isn’t Just a Moral Question

Ryan.Allen

May 8, 2018


Lab test animals often live in prison-like conditions, such as in small boxes with little to no social interactions or mental stimuli. These creatures are often more obese, have weaker immune systems, and are at a greater risk for cancer than their wild counterparts. For years, scientists thought that keeping test animals in simple conditions minimized the number of variables, making extermination easier. New research now suggests that these unnatural living conditions for rats, guinea pigs, or other lab animals can alter test results, possibly connecting to research replication problems.

When scientists in Australia began to put toys and other stimuli in their lab rat enclosures, they found that their rodents tended to be healthier. Researchers are now saying that companions, toys, and puzzles allow lab animals to be more physiologically like wild animals. The University of Michigan has even opened a research center specifically on the study of these enrichment practices, called the Refinement & Enrichment Advancements Laboratory.

Some scientists are still skeptical of this new trend and worry that these interventions will negatively impact replication. If these skeptics are right, should we return to the old methods of housing lab animals? Should we even be conducting scientific tests on animals at all?
