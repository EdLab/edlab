Calling All Online Assessment Enthusiasts and Critics

Jeannie Crowley

August 7, 2009

Whether you love them or hate them, online assessment systems (OAS) used to collect, track and analyze student data are growing in popularity. What makes some sites sublimely intuitive and others maddeningly counterproductive? I would like to look at several of the most popular systems in use in NYC public schools and develop an informal design guide to help revamp existing sites and shape new sites we may develop. I believe some of the questions I've included below could also be used to investigate educational tools (not just assessment systems) used by teachers. I would greatly appreciate feedback to develop a more comprehensive list of questions for investigation.

1. What is the primary method of data collection? (How do educators collect the information before they put it into the OAS?) 2. If the primary method is not direct input into the OAS, why not?

  • Primary (paper, video, etc) versions are needed for legal or other reasons
  • Limited access to resources (computers)
  • Network connection issues prevent reliable site access
  • Primary method is more efficient than site
  • Users do not have confidence that information will be saved in system and want to keep a hard copy
    • 3. What is the purpose of using the OAS instead of traditional methods? (Just because we can put it online does this mean we need to?) 4. What is the added benefit of the online assessment database? 5. Is the added benefit of the OAS perceived by educators? Why or why not? (Do they know why they are doing it?) 6. Is the added benefit of the OAS used by educators? (Are they simply adding the data or are they using the tools in the site to analyze the data?) 7. If the OAS is not being used, why not?
  • Inadequate training
  • Too time consuming
  • The underlying purpose of the OAS is not valued by the educator
  • Usability issues
  • Universal design/universal access issues
  • Emotional design issues
