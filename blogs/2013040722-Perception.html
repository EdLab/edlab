Perception

Bismark Appiah

April 7, 2013

Perception is defined by psychologist as the interpretation of the sensory information by the brain. In essence, the brain of a human being is able to make sense of information that it gathers from the world. One principle that explains how humans organize what they see into figures and ground is known as the Gestalt Principle; it is a principle that describes the brain's organization of sensory information into meaningful units and pattern. Through Gestalt Principle, I learned some strategies that our visual system uses to group sensory building blocks into perceptual units. For example, proximity states that things near each other tend to be grouped together. Closure demonstrates how the brain tends to fill in gaps and thereby perceive complete forms. Similarity shows things that are alike in some way (as in color, shape, or size) tend to be perceived as belonging together, while continuity perceive things naturally as continuous. These are the four strategies the brain uses to interpret sensory information.
