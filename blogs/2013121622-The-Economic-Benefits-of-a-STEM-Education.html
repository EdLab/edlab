The Economic Benefits of a STEM Education

rendy

December 16, 2013

The recession's effect on unemployment has been well documented. Though our economy continues to show signs of revival, unemployment still remains disturbingly high. There've been a high unemployment rate lately, but in most cases, available jobs require a background in a STEM subject area. According to an organization that supports STEM education, there are over three million unemployed workers in the country, which compares to one STEM worker for two unfilled STEM jobs. Many occupations can't find people with the right skills and the right educational background. As Raytheon Chairman and CEO William Swanson said at a Massachusetts' STEM Summit last fall:

Too many students and adults are training for jobs in which labor surpluses exist and demand is low, while high-demand jobs, particularly those in STEM fields, go unfilled.
A report from Georgetown University Center on Education found that 65% of bachelors' degree graduates in STEM have a higher income than graduates with masters in non-STEM occupations and 47 percent of bachelor degree graduates in STEM earn more than PhDs in non-STEM occupations.
