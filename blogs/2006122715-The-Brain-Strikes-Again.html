The Brain Strikes Again

Dan Mallinger

December 27, 2006

Adding to a long list of modern technologies the brain isn't really equipped to deal with (tv and the visual cortex, cities and social interaction modules, etc.) scientists are finding mounting evidence that "virtual" worlds aren't all that virtual at all. In an interesting study, scientists recreated the Milgram shock experiments with a virtual set up where the participants knew a priori that they were not shocking a real person and that responses were computer generated. The participants still showed stress responses! It seems (not surprisingly) that the brain is ill equipped to handle "virtual" worlds - ones which could not have existed in the EEA. However, one finds the same effect with baby screams: playing a tape of a baby crying can elicit the same biological response as a real live screaming baby in an adult subject. Baby's adapted a rather cruel negotiation strategy: I'll make you miserable with horrible sounds and attract predators if you don't feed me! The next step in virtual experiments is to control for the sounds of the computer screaming. Is the elevated stress resultant from the stress of conflicting values and authority command (the Milgram claim)? And how much of it is explained by cognitive wiring that says "Screaming and begging should make you nervous?" And do I really need to argue that screams make you nervous?
