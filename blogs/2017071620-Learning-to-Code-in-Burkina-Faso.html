Learning to Code in Burkina Faso

Nantwi

July 16, 2017


Access to education remains elusive for many children in developing countries. Often times, parents are too poor to afford school fees and other education-related costs such as transportation and books. Even those fortunate enough to be enrolled in school have to contend with poor instruction. The result is a never-ending cycle where school-aged children are often at work instead of the classroom, laboring for their daily existence. But a technology driven program in the small West African nation of Burkina Faso is inspiring a new generation of students.

With support from the TuaRes Foundation, Girls Tech is an exclusive program that teaches young girls basic coding and other technology related skills. The goal is for the girls to translate these skills into their future learning and ultimately professional life. The tech gender gap often noted in Western countries is even more apparent in places like Burkina Faso, where cultural norms mean female students are discouraged from pursuing certain academic interests.

How can developing countries integrate technology in their classrooms? How can we foster interest in coding among traditionally underrepresented groups? Join the discussion on Vialogues.
