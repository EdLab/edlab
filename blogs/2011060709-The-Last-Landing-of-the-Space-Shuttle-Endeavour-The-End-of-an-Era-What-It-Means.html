The Last Landing of the Space Shuttle Endeavour, The End of an Era, What It Means

Alex Park

June 7, 2011

The Space Shuttle Endeavour, the youngest ship in the now-discontinued fleet of NASA's Space Shuttles, landed for the last time yesterday. There aren't many missions left for the Space Shuttle program; Atlantis has an upcoming mission, and maybe a few more (I'm not 100% sure), but the program is coming to an end. Personally, as I was growing up, space shuttles were *the* symbol of humanities' advanced technology. Even in Korea, space shuttles were the de facto non-scifi spaceships that we drew or talked about. Now that the program is reaching its end, I'm afraid it will take away from the children's imaginations in space, and our willingness to delve into the "final frontier" will enter a hiatus. Of course, this does not have to go in such a negative direction. With the economy's speedy recovery and a renewed interest in commercial and noncommercial space travel, we may come up with something even more exciting to stimulate the children's imaginations. If we were to take this route, then the end of the space shuttle program will have its rightful place in history, not as an unnecessary expenditure, but as one of mankind's greatest achievements.
