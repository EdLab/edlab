Thinking and Tinkering into the "Networked Age"

jvazquez

March 18, 2013


This vialogue features an adaptation of John Seely Brown’s keynote speech at 2012’s Digital Media and Learning conference. Using slick, animated text to accompany his audio, the video effectively puts viewers in a trance as they listen to Brown explain what it means to learn in a networked age.

Brown hits a controversial note when he recalls the age of the one-room schoolhouse, recounting the unique social dynamic it fostered. Because it housed students of all learning levels, more advanced students could help the struggling ones while the teacher simply imparted the knowledge. In other words, the teacher gave information while the students taught. Brown argues for the return of this scenario, but on a much larger scale—the global one-room schoolhouse.

"We used to know how to know," Brown says. "We got our knowledge from experts… but now, topics have no boundaries." He encourages learners to tinker, make, play, and fail. When curiosity drives you, education is a fortunate side effect. In finding new resources to learn new things, the entrepreneurial learner distinguishes herself from the traditional student and resets expectations for the future of learning.

Do we need to go back [to the schoolhouse] in order to move forward? While we’re at it, is there a better fitting metaphor? If you can think of one, we want to hear it. Leave it in the ongoing discussion on Vialogues.
