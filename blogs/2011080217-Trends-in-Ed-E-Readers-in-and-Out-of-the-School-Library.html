Trends in Ed: E-Readers in and Out of the School Library

LauraCostello

August 2, 2011

School Library Journal recently reported an Amazon crackdown on school libraries violating its relatively new 1-book:1-device end user agreement. As of February of this year, institutions using Amazon Kindles would have to register each device to separate email addresses and Amazon accounts to prevent content sharing between devices. Before the change in the user agreement, the Amazon support page allowed allowed content to be shared between 6 Kindles, though it did recommend 1:1 for institutions. This change precedes a rumored back-end management system, but until this system is in place librarians who wish to use Kindles in school libraries will have to individually register, purchase, and manage content on each unit. Amazon support has been directing users towards their developing agreement with Overdrive, but subscriptions to Overdrive are out of price range for many school libraries and the pace of the Overdrive/Amazon agreement has been glacial. Barnes & Noble has declared its support for library institutional subscriptions, promising greater back-end control, pre-loaded devices, and the ability to manage multiple devices with lending. They too are recommending a 1:1 agreement, but offering lending and central management will make this model much more feasible for school libraries. Amazon may be missing out on a big marketing opportunity in school libraries, kids and teens could represent the next boom in e-reader sales. Whatever the future of these devices, it's sad to see the wild west days of digital rights management slip away. It's especially sad when it quiets the lovely sound of small fingers flipping digital pages.
