What I Learned at the American Copy Editors Society Conference: Day Three

Brian.Sweeting

March 30, 2015

*Private*

Saturday was my last day at the American Copy Editors Society conference and I have a few more takeaways worth sharing. For full-on conference geekery, feel free to dive into all of the notes.

Giving Copy a Facelift, With No Pain

Merrill Perlman (@meperl), former managing editor of the New York Times News Service, walked us through her three rules for copy editing:

  1. Do no harm.
  2. If you can't explain why you want to change something, you can't change it.
  3. No surprisesâ€“they can damage relationships with authors.

With these rules in mind, there are several ways to make your writing smoother and tighter: look for things like back-to-back quotations from different people that might cause indirection or confusion about the speaker; use people's titles as an aside to the reader; don't use words that will send readers to the dictionary; don't refer to something you haven't mentioned yet; and avoid crash blossoms, words that can be either a noun or a verb (e.g., this confusing headline, "Citizen Science Charts Horse Chestnut Tree Pest Spread").

Although these seem like perfectly common sense, it's amazing how many bad examples you can easily pull from respectable publications.

Headlines, Not Cheapline or Borelines

I loved this session by ACES President Teresa Schmedding (@tschmedding) on criteria for writing good healines. To be frank, I think we struggle with this on NLT (myself included), and to be fair, a lot of publications do. It's really not an easy task because you need to do a lot in just a few words.

I'm a sucker for puns, but Teresa made a compelling case against them that's making me rethink my dad humor. A punny headline might be a good laugh, but it might miss the point entirely, or may convey something you didn't mean to. Example: a local paper's article about a new Chic fil A restaurant with the headline "Frying the Coop" is catchy, but it misses the interesting pieces of the story: the business added 70 jobs to the local economy, it's part of an expanding food mecca, and they gave out free chicken to 100 people.

Some guidelines for creating interesting headlines that aren't cheap or boring:

  1. Pique curiosity while respecting intelligence and understanding interests.
  2. Write for your audience and their friends. 16% of all viral headlines contain the words you and your.
  3. Include unusual or unexpected information. In Teresa's newsletters, negative superlatives have a 63% higher click-through rate than positive.
  4. Sound human and trigger some emotion. Example: "We get it, desserts are bad for us. But this chart finds sugar in the unlikeliest of foods"
  5. Tell readers what they'll learn with active key words (e.g., how to, tips, why, best, great, meet, watch, see).
  6. Tell readers how fast they'll learn it. The headline "Cuisine, No Controversy" was changed to "5 Reasons You Should Try These Student-run Restaurants" and saw a big increase in click-through rates.

IMHO I think a question you should always ask yourself about a headline you've written is, "If I saw this, would I want to read it?"

Building Your Brand With Social Media

We've been active on social media for a while, but there are areas where we can improve. Although most of this session was a primer for people who still have an egg as their Twitter profile pic, there were some tips that would be good to revisit.

Creating and maintaining a voice on social media is tricky enough as an individual, and as an organization it's even trickier. I think we have to be careful not to push out messages without sounding human. It's not easy to reduce articles down to a sentence, but we ought to do it in a simple, precise, relatable way. After you've written a post, think "Would I want to click on this?" before hitting send.

Thanks for reading if you've made it this far! Feel free to ask questions about these sessions. I'll build some of these ideas into a D&R so we can work together to improve our writing, editing, and social media skills.
