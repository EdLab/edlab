Trends in Ed: The Controversy Continues

Fred Rossoff

November 18, 2011

Continuing an internal argument that came to light at last year's annual American Anthropological Association conference, yesterday found many anthropologists at this year's meeting still hashing over the issues. As far as I can tell, the argument is between what I'll call the Bolsheviks (majority), who believe in anthropology as a cultural discipline defined by narrative, qualitative field-work, and humanistic values. The Menshevisk (minority), on the other hand, are worried that the idea of anthropology as a scientific discipline (overlapping, for example, with biology) is under attack. Some argue, as the Chronicle of Higher Education puts it, that these are the downstream effects of austerity measures within the university. “Departments have been closed or merged and resources have dwindled, and some anthropologists turned on each other, as happened last year.” It's certainly possible that a downturn in funding has exposed fault lines that are more easily ignored in more prosperous times. But are other vulnerable disciplines experiencing this kind of internal tension? Or is this about something specific going on with Anthropology (disagreements about whether to take a social or biological approach to the study of race, for instance)?
