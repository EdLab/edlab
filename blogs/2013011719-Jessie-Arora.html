Jessie Arora

Kate Meersschaert

January 17, 2013


Jessie Arora is an educational technology entrepreneur and thought leader. Both the writer of The EdTech Handbook and the founder of TeacherSquare.com, Arora combines thought leadership with practice in her mission to create a "square" for teachers and stakeholders to unite around solving educational challenges. We had the chance to talk with her about her past and current work.

EXCLUSIVE INTERVIEW WITH NLT

Q: How did your educational trajectory affect your current work?

A: During my senior year at UCLA I took a seminar course as part of my minor in Spanish Language where I taught at an adult literacy center in South Central LA. That was my first real experience teaching and I was so inspired by the transformation we were making in people's lives by empowering them with the ability to read. At that point I directly saw the impact that education had on people and the quality of their lives and knew I wanted to focus my energy on this work.

Q: What experiences do you apply to your work with TeacherSquare?

A: Teach-A-Class is an early stage education nonprofit with a massive vision to actually deliver a free, world class education to anyone, anywhere. Having partnered deeply with Khan Academy and other OER content providers, Teach-A-Class is focusing on the last mile work of training local teachers and providing them with offline access to all the amazing free online OER (Open Educational Resources) content that is more easily available to students in the developed world. Teach-A-Classâ€™ work is quite similar to TeacherSquare because the focus is on supporting teachers and empowering them with the skills to make the technology work to improve instructional practice and not just the software. The technology is merely a tool and the real innovation is making it accessible to children in schools and communities all over the world.

Q: How were you impacted by your involvement with Stanford's Ed 333B: "Envisioning the Future of Learning"?

A: Stanford is really a magical place. The quality of individuals, energy and ideas is unmatched and I was honored to bring my work with Teach-A-Class to Ed 333B: "Envisioning the Future of Learning" through the Khan Academy design challenge. As an alum of the Stanford School of Education (POLS '08) it was really inspiring to come back to campus and build relationships with some of the current students. The education technology space has developed substantially in the past few years, so it was interesting to see how newer technologies were being incorporated into designing models for learning opportunities on a global level.

Q: What lessons did you learn during your time at Google that you apply to your work as a founder?

A: (Wow! This is really a tough question as Google was my first 'real' job in the tech world after spending a year teaching English in Japan.) I joined Google in late 2003 as one of the first members of the AdWords team and it was phenomenal to help grow that team (in India and beyond) and support a product that is one of the best business models on the web. Of all the lessons I learned during my 4 years at Google what has been most influential is the importance of building a strong company culture. You can read a ton of books and articles about how to do this but nothing is as powerful as seeing an organization grow and evolve from the inside and how much that culture drives growth and success. People naturally want to be a part of a community, a movement; something larger than themselves and one of Google's enduring qualities has been cultivating an amazing culture that celebrates people, innovation, quality and utility. I see lots of organizations talk and think about this, but making it a reality is a different story.

Q: How do you envision TeacherSquare helping to change the current/future learning landscape?

A: TeacherSquare is building a community of educators who are passionate about learning and sharing their expertise. We know how much teacher quality impacts student outcomes and that providing meaningful professional development helps retain high quality educators, yet when we think about 'edtech' we often focus on the software and less on how it will be implemented and integrated into pedagogical practice. While technology has tremendous potential to make an impact on K-12 education, the real challenge and rewards are around preparing teachers to best utilize the 'new' innovative tools and resources. TeacherSquare is technology agnostic, focusing on the human capital piece of the K12 system, built on the idea that once teachers have the access and support to use various education technology tools they will find their own creative ways to transform teaching and learning beyond the current fixation with online content delivery.

Q: How do you imagine the EdTech Handbook helping to push forward better/more relevant educational technologies?

A: The Edtech Handbook is a community curated guide to help aspiring education entrepreneurs gain a better understanding about some of the nuances and complexities of working in the education startup space. I am excited to see that so many talented individuals are interested in solving problems around teaching and learning and found myself answering the same questions over and over again around where to start and what are the key challenges to keep in mind, especially when taking on issues in the K12 world. The Edtech Handbook is a living resource with new posts added each month, so I invite anyone with expertise in the education startup space to contact me if they are interested in contributing their thoughts and experience.

Q: Why is educational technology emerging as a way to "save education?"

A: The most promising elements of the current wave of education technology tools is the ability to personalize learning and truly make it accessible to anyone, anywhere. Previously it was considered cost prohibitive to think about delivering education to everyone in this manner, and the recent developments in education technology, especially around online content delivery, has shifted this mindset. I wouldn't call it a revolution quite yet, but the early implications are very promising.

Q: Final thoughts?

A: The more we can create opportunities for students to take control of their own learning goals and foster a culture of achievement and motivation for all students, the more we will see improved learning outcomes for all students. Education software, and the data that it surfaces, has the potential to make learning more engaging and relevant, which are important factors in motivation. Overall, I think we must be mindful of our expectations of what software tools can offer and remember that learning is a social practice as well and community plays an important role in the learning process, for students and teachers.

Jessie Arora, founder of TeacherSquare, is passionate about cultivating the education startup ecosystem to help create tools and services that improve teaching and learning for all students. She is an active angel investor mainly focused on the education space, applying her experiences from Google, Citizen Schools & Stanford School of Education. She blogs at edcrunch.org and you can follow her @Jessie_Arora.
