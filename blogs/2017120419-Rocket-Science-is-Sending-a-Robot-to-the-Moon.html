Rocket Science is Sending a Robot to the Moon

DebraLee

December 4, 2017


Team Indus is one of five of startups from around the world competing for the honor of being the first commercial company to land a rover on the Moon. The team’s spacecraft is six feet tall, weighs 1323 lbs., and is scheduled to blast off with its robotic payload, a rover, in late December 2017. The 24-day mission includes 10-days in orbit and 14-days on the surface of Mare Imbrium, one of the Moon’s vast lava plains.

Mission control will be watching the landing, the most planned phase of the mission, with baited breath as onboard software controls the entire process. The pre-programmed software will ensure the thrusters are fired at the right moment to decrease orbit and that laser sensors collect data that detect and analyze the moon’s surface and orient the spacecraft for a soft touchdown on Mare Imbrium at lunar dawn.

In the final phase, the rover, with its three fish-eye cameras, will roll over the surface on wheels designed to minimize Moondust damage. Then it will capture and beam back high definition video and stills to one of five stations waiting back on Earth: Goldstone, USA; Madrid, Spain; Byalalu, India; Baik, Indonesia; or Canberra, Australia.
