Left as Jell-O in My Seat

Roy Kim

July 23, 2013

**PRIVATE** I like to imagine that the stories I read are input-output devices that wring me through and through like dirty laundry. But these are not washing-machines because some of them leave me dirtier than when I had first been thrown in! There's one type of story that I cannot ever ever have enough of: The tearjerker. (The Little Prince, The Stranger, The Dandelion Girl, the myth of Orpheus, Chopin's ballades...); these compositions are Dangerous. I feel masochistic running after works like these over and over. Just why does it hurt so good!? I just read The Education of Little Tree by Asa Earl Carter. Its ending sequence, featuring a "back" peddler of whom I mistakenly read as a "black" peddler, destroyed me. I loved him so much... and I didn't even know what the precise difference between a back peddler and a normal peddler was--I guess some scenes don't even have to make sense to produce strong feelings. I'll remember that black peddler, Mr. Wine. For teachers, I feel that sad emotions are really effective in causing your students to remember a book long after they step out of your class. But every student has such different standards, and "taste" is absolutely determined culturally. Is it better to let students decide what they will read in their English classes? Edlabbers, I wonder what your most powerful stories are, of any medium.
