Trends in Ed, 3.2.10: In a World of Multimodality

Ting Yuan

March 2, 2010

Henry Jenkins, a professor of Communication, Journalism, and Cinematic Arts at the University of Southern California and the principal investigator for Project New Media Literacies (NML), recently blogged about his interview with Spanish educational researcher Pilar Lacasa about the relationship between old and new media. Related to an E-book research study that Stephen and I are working on, Dr. Jenkins asserts that burgeoning new media will not replace old ones -- "we will live in a world where books and printed matter still matters even as students get more information from computers than ever before." This reminds me of some previous discussions on Knobel and Lankshear's book A new literacies sampler (2007). Do students and teachers today view E-books and other technologies/social media/learning platforms as a "technical stuff" or "ethos stuff" (p. 7)? The hybridity of media today brings increasing multimodal literacy experiences to learners. The new-literacy experience challenges our conventional understandings of time and space, of static texts and the notion of reading and writing, offer new alternatives to think about practices, participation, collaboration, and distribution. The fluid nature of new literacies in a world of multimodality may further push us to rethink teaching and learning while trying to catch up with new technologies.
