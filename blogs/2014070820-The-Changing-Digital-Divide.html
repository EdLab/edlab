The Changing Digital Divide

Ting Yuan

July 8, 2014


Title: The Digital Divide Shifts to Differences in Usage (2014)

Authors: Alexander van Deursen & Jan van Dijk

Source: New Media & Society

Research Question: How do socio-demographic variables relate to the amount and types of Internet use?

Study Design: To investigate the amount and the types of Internet use by today’s netizens, the authors distributed a survey to 1,481 participants randomly recruited from a larger population in the Netherlands. The survey examined age, gender, Internet experience, income, employment status, and residency to find out how each factor contributes to the amount or type of Internet use. The authors further conducted factor analyses to validate new categories of Internet usage.

Findings: The authors found seven clusters of Internet usage: information, news, personal development, commercial transaction, leisure, social interaction and gaming. Education level stands out as one of the most salient indicators for differences in Internet usage. Individuals with a lower level of education reported using the internet for more hours per day than individuals with medium or higher education levels.

Participants with lower education levels reported that they used the Internet more for gaming and social interaction while more highly educated respondents reported that they used the Internet for personal development. These findings suggest that the digital divide should not be viewed simply as binary but rather as a set of complex practices that reproduce and potentially accelerate social inequality.

Moving Forward: The new digital divide might mean that sociocultural factors influence the kind and quality of Internet use, even if access is universal. For the software developer, the challenge lies in how to create better online platforms that maximize each digital learning experience; for online educators, the findings might help them avoid assumptions about students’ Internet behavior and generate better ideas for instructional design, including making learning mobile, informal and adaptive, especially for those who might come from marginalized or less educated groups.
