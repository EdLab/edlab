Commercially reasonable. The perils of multiple educational data stakeholders

dinosossi2

July 20, 2017


First off, thanks to Alvaro and Xiaoting for an entertaining and informative seminar yesterday!


As the discussion alluded to, there are a number of issues that come into sharp relief when we view privacy over vulnerable populations like K–12 students. The contrasts are especially stark when we compare public versus private institutions and overtly educational versus non-educational stakeholders. This may be best demonstrated by the privacy policy put in place by ETS, which deals with "commercially reasonable" uses of data. As touched upon yesterday, it appears that the ambiguity inherent in the term allows it some wiggle room later if it feels that it needs to change its policy toward student test data in response to its peers. As such, today's privacy protections may not be in effect tomorrow. Uncertainty seems to be the name of the game.


As Purdue's acquisition of Kaplan (the test prep behemoth) has shown, there is increasing interplay between formal, traditional institutions (e.g., Purdue) versus corporations (e.g., Kaplan). Although Purdue seems honestly interested in education and supporting this venture, we do not know how long this relationship will endure. And, if it doesn't, what will be the prevailing privacy policy with student data? Intuitively, you would think that institutions like Purdue, subject to strict institutional review board (IRB) review processes, would have a much stronger compulsion to protect student data.


We'll see how these privacy policies with respect to student data evolve over time.
