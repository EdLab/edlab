Robotics and 3D Tech Reveal How the Dinosaurs Really Moved

DebraLee

October 15, 2017


Edelman Fossil Park, a 64-acre former marl pit in New Jersey, contains thousands of fossils from the Cretaceous Period, the heyday of the dinosaurs. Since it opened in 2012, paleontologists have unearthed sea turtles, sharks, bony fish, crocodiles and mosasaurs, which they are using to transform STEM education through hands-on discovery and world-class research.

Recently, researchers started using 3D modeling to scan preserved Dreadnoughtus bones (discovered in Argentina by the Park’s director) and robotics to reveal how the 65-ton dinosaur moved. The bones show remnants of ligaments in detail, allowing paleontologists to accurately map virtual models then use these to make 3D prints of the bones. Biomechanical modelling also enables computer simulations to examine the efficiency of the artificial limbs by using robotics to mimic their motion. All of this technology means critical information sharing can occur in real time with paleontologists and museums around the world.

Fossil Park, now part of Rowan University, is thought to have been associated with the extinction that wiped out the 165-million-year reign of the dinosaurs 65 million years ago. In addition to research, the Park runs an educational dig site, where students can take guided tours and unearth ancient skeletons alongside the experts. Plans to build a museum, a visitor center, a nature trail and a paleontology-themed playground are underway, and a fossil preparation lab will reveal how scientists study, collaborate and use technology to further our understanding of life before the common era.
