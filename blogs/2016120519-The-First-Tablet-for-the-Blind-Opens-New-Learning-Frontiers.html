The First Tablet for the Blind Opens New Learning Frontiers

Jenny Abamu

December 5, 2016


New technologies to support learning sometimes have an unintended effect of excluding some students. Visually rich materials can aid learning, but for blind students such materials can present additional barriers. The makers of Blitab address this issue with their new tablet for the blind.

Their software comes from open source solutions that convert any text into braille code. The tablet uses smart liquids that create bubble-like tactile pixels on the screen, mimicking braille. Users can also download books on their devices. Now educators can seamlessly differentiate their classrooms to accommodate blind students using the device. This technology becomes increasingly relevant as the use of open education resources continues to rise. Increased technology accessibility for blind students, and others of different abilities, is certain to bring new perspectives on a host of issues.
