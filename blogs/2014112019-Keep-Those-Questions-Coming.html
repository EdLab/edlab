Keep Those Questions Coming

Carmen James

November 20, 2014


Warren Berger is famous for making a big deal about a small thing: questions. In Berger’s view, asking good questions has led to the success of many of the biggest companies in Silicon Valley. Questions lay bare assumptions and things we have thought to be commonsensical but aren’t necessarily. Noticing and asking the right question—"why hasn’t someone tried X?"—is the seed of innovation.

Critically, Berger believes we can learn to ask better questions. To be fair, Socrates came to the same conclusion thousands of years ago, but Berger takes this point and places it into our current context of innovation. He is concerned that in our high-stakes testing era that rests heavily on rigid accountability schemes, time for asking good questions is being pushed out of the curriculum. Teachers do not feel they have time to entertain questions and so demand answers they can easily assess.

Berger holds that focusing on questions, not answers, in school can have a tremendously positive impact on education. Formulating questions, as Berger points out, is a skill requiring students to think for themselves, a key to success in modern society.
