What Will The Future of Inclusive Technology Look Like?

Rebecca Beeson

May 15, 2016


One child out of 110 is diagnosed with autism. With such a high incidence rate, professionals in medical, educational, and technological fields are thinking critically about tech products that can best serve the needs of those with autism spectrum disorder. Commonplace technologies are now making it easier for them to research and experiment with how best to address needs ranging from speech communication to tactile perception.

Software designed for touch screens enables students to have more control over their motor skills and digitally manipulate information with more ease than using a mouse and monitor. Individuals with autism spectrum disorder may have poor motor control; using a traditional mouse when on a computer can be challenging, as it requires connecting the disjointed mouse and screen. However, touch screens allow them to interact directly with the visuals, providing them the opportunity to showcase their abilities and increase their technological fluency.
