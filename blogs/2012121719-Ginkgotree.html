Ginkgotree

LauraCostello

December 17, 2012


The digital textbook is great in theory, but modern incarnations often get bogged down with design, scope, or reach. Ginkgotree is a conglomeration of old ideas with the possibility to transcend that trifecta; half Tumblr, half college printshop style coursepack, Ginkgotree allows instructors to seamlessly merge free stuff on the internet with copyrighted content. The powerhouse couple behind the site, Lida and Scott Hasbrouck, have a philosophy that the internet should be beautifully useful, not just pretty. They lived this philosophy in their first venture together, the popular iPad app PaperDesk, and their sophomore effort is just as functional, simple, and lovely.

Pros:

The site opens with a surprising layered front page, but the user interface is extremely straightforward. This combination seems well-placed to attract the digitally-savvy but remain unintimidating to any technology stragglers, a good mix for academia. Copyright issues surrounding the digitization of the coursepack have been problematic, but the benefits of steps taken by Ginkotree are clear. The ability to track student usage and a nicely automated copyright clearance process with a single monthly fee for students add layers of functionality to a traditionally clunky and expensive process.

Cons:

Ginkgotree hasn’t ironed out all the copyright kinks yet. The service has promised mail-in scanning and streamlined copyright clearance for book chapters, but this feature is not up and running yet. The site uses a mix of user-purchased copyrighted content, open source content, and subscribed digital content through the user’s institution. If the site attracts a user community, it may be possible to circumvent the copyright clearance process and negotiate directly with copyright holders similar to unglue.it. That said, the current $10 monthly charge for unlimited classes is an attractive contrast to traditional textbooks and coursepacks.

Our Takeaway:

Coursepacks are definitely on the road to web town. The physical version is too slow and unwieldy for our digitally-adjusted patience. Sites like Ginkgotree, XanEdu, Symtext, and Study.net are rushing to fill the void, but Ginkgotree has a real bid to be the most appealing in simplicity and design. If the site can iron out their copyright and scanning issues, they may even evolve to transcend the traditional course pack.

The Bottom Line:

Ginkgotree is a necessary update with style.
