Fifth Conference on the Statistical Methods in Psychometrics

SeungYeon.Lee

November 30, 2017

I participated in the “Fifth Conference on the Statistical Methods in Psychometrics” on November 17th. Here is the information about the conference: http://www.scientifichpc.com/Focus2017_2.html


The brief information about the conference can be found in Xiang’s post (e.g., conference format, agenda, participants).

Until last year, the topics of the conference were mostly about cognitive diagnosis models, but this year's topics were much more diverse. 

Most of research revolved around the application and the development of statistical models and related issues in analyzing item response data.


Among the talks, the talk of Shiyu Wang, a professor at University of Georgia, was of the most interest for me. The title of the presentation was "Using response time to assess learning progress: A hidden Markov Model for Response and Response Time”.

The topic was very similar to my dissertation study in that the presented model was for the analysis of responses from diagnostic assessment over time and to understand learning progress. 

Her approach was to use Hidden Markov Models but she considered higher-order Hidden Markov Models. The higher-order Hidden Markov Model defines the higher-order learning ability which is a continuous latent variable and assumes that the transition probability from a skill mastery pattern at T to a different pattern at T+1 depends on the learning ability. Particularly, in this talk, the model was extended to incorporate response time. 


Recently, I have found an increasing number of literatures about psychometric modeling approaches to assess learning process.

With continued technological improvements, we are able to observe how people interact with e-learning tools. 

Back in the days, we couldn’t observe learners’ process until they reach their final answers. So the traditional methods in psychometrics have historically been used to rank task takers based on their final responses but what has been conspicuously missing from such analyses were more in-depth understanding of the steps in between.


Although we, at EdLab, do not focus on test responses or cognitive learning, those approaches are similar to our research interest in that we want to understand their learning steps and behavior patterns occurred while they are learning.
