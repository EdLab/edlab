Ethical Steel Cage Match: Good People v. The Corporation

dinosossi2

July 5, 2017


Joel Bakan’s book The Corporation is a multifaceted examination of the rise of one of the most dominant organizational structures of the modern era.


It discusses the emergence of the legal personhood of corporations, corporate social responsibility, the negative externalities that these types of institutions cause, and many other important issues.


In some ways an antithesis to Good People, The Corporation notably explores the legal person status of corporations against the DSM-IV's classification for psychopaths. For those unfamiliar with the term psychopath, just think of a not so good person...


The characteristics of a psychopath (and, by extension, a corporation) include being incapable of feeling guilt, disregard for the safety of others, the inability to maintain human relationships, disregard for other people’s feelings, deceitfulness for profit, and the failure to conform to corporate and social norms.


In short, not the best guest for a dinner party.


If you believe in the inherent dignity of people, the impulses of a traditional legally defined corporation seem to be in direct contravention with what we connote as the essential traits of a just and humane person.


I sincerely believe that Good People was written with the best of intentions and it can prompt a substantive conversation, despite some of the legitimate concerns mentioned during today's seminar.


However, perhaps the morality of natural law and its humane impulses toward ethical behavior are too far apart from the exploitative mandates of a corporation.


In short, perhaps we have confronted a gulf too wide and a chasm too deep when we try to reconcile humans and corporations.


Something to consider over a delicious can of Coke...
