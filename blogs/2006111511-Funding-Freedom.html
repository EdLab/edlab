Funding Freedom

Karen Bryner

November 15, 2006

Harvard Graduate School of Education (HUGSE) just announced that it will provide three years of funding for all doctoral students in addition to the current $7,000 stipend for first year students. This largesse is estimated to save each student up to $88,000. Such funding certainly frees up time for students to focus on their studies and move more quickly through their programs. There are significanlty fewer doctoral students as well as doctoral programs at HUGSE than at Teachers College. However, where there is overlap of programs offered by both institutions, this new funding is likely to be a huge factor in new students' decisions on attending Harvard or TC. Will TC be able to compete? At Harvard, with potenially fewer doctoral students applying for work study and research assistant positions at the school, it would seem that master's students may have more interesting job opportunities. I wonder how such a trend could affect the kind of research produced by the institution. Perhaps the additional funding will allow doctoral students and professors to be more collaborative in research projects given the students may have more time to devote to research.
