You’ll Have to Travel Below Earth to Find Its Biggest Musical Instrument

saraehardman

June 29, 2017


Deep underground lays the world’s largest musical instrument. Housed in Virginia’s Luray Caverns, this Great Stalacpipe Organ combines technology with earth’s natural elements to create a subterranean organ. Instead of pipes, however, this instrument uses the cavern’s stalactites to sound its musical notes. Each key sends an electric impulse to a rubber mallet that strikes a single stalactite, which causes a vibration that creates a musical tone.

The 37 musical stalactites span 3.5 acres underground, but the music can be heard all throughout the 64 acre Luray Caverns. The Great Stalacpipe Organ was created by Leland Sprinkle, a mathematician and electronic scientist; he began constructing the organ in 1954, but the behemoth instrument was not completed for another three years. It is the only instrument of its kind in existence, and in its uniqueness it causes us to consider what it means to manipulate the earth with technology to create art.

What are the aesthetic or environmental effects of using natural elements of the earth such as stalactites to create a musical instrument? What would be the difference in experience between listening to a regular organ or listening to this organ in the caverns? Answer these questions and more on Vialogues.
