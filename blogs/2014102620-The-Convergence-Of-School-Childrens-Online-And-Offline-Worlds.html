The Convergence Of School Children’s Online And Offline Worlds

Ting Yuan

October 26, 2014


Title: Children's Construction of Identity in Virtual Play Worlds—A Classroom Perspective (2013)

Author: Anne Burke

Source: Language and Literacy

Research Question: What types of identities do young gamers construct?

Study Design: This case study reports on a group of elementary school children in a virtual gaming world—Disney’s Club Penguin. The data set includes field notes, interviews, and video recordings as well as written and oral text during game play. The data analysis drew on ethnographic data from three fourth-grade boys as part of a larger study following the online and offline play spaces of twenty children over a period of three years.

Findings: Major finding suggests that children navigate fluidly among different roles online as well as between real and virtual world experiences. They naturally apply their real-world knowledge to make meaning of their virtual explorations, while utilizing virtual literacies learned to solve real-world problems. Students’ virtual experiences stimulated their real world social interactions; they formed affinity groups and social connections with other players in both real and virtual worlds by creating overlapped or connected identities in various meaningful ways.

Moving Forward: The study responds to the lack of research on elementary-school-based virtual learning experiences. For educators, it suggests the importance of acknowledging students’ fluid learning patterns and social identities as well as their ability to bridge online and offline worlds by designing immersive, remixed learning activities for the classroom. The virtual world can further serve as an alternative learning space for students to express themselves, to practice citizenship skills and to collaborate with peers in many playful ways.
