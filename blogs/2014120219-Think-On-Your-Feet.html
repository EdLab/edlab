Think On Your Feet

Meredith Powers

December 2, 2014


Walking has long been associated with thinking, and research emphasizes the link between walking and innovative insight, as well as the power of walking to help us organize our thoughts. Recent studies had students complete thinking tasks, and the group using treadmills came up with more creative ideas than the seated group (though they fared worse on some focused-thinking tasks). Perhaps the recent trend of bringing treadmill desks into the classroom will help to harness some of the positive effects, like promoting new neural connections, that walking has on thinking and learning. But as far as reducing attentional fatigue—another factor important for learning—treadmills by themselves are probably not going to recreate anything like Virginia Woolf’s "solitary trampling."
