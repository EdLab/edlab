Smartphone Separation Anxiety

DebraLee

November 14, 2017


Nomophobia is the discomfort caused by non-availability of a mobile device. It is characterized by increases in heart rate, anxiety, blood pressure, and unpleasant feelings. A study has found that when users perceive smartphones as their extended selves, they are more likely to get attached to the devices, which, in turn, leads to nomophobia.

In the study university students in South Korea (138 men and 163 women) completed an online survey that assessed their levels of positive memories, self-extension, attachment, phone proximity-seeking behavior, and nomophobia. Based on their scores respondents were divided into either Group A. high nomophobia, or Group B. low nomophobia, and asked to free write about what smartphones meant to them.

In a surprising revelation the word "hurt" appeared in the writing samples of only those participants in Group A, who also more frequently (compared with Group B) reported wrist and neck pain due to smartphone use. This group was also more likely to get distracted from their studies and work.

The data support the researcher’s hypotheses that the ability of smartphones to retrieve and relate to users’ personal memories results in the technology becoming part of the extended self. Further, attachment to smartphones has a direct effect on the severity of nomophobia, and this relationship is mediated by users’ tendency to continuously check their smartphone and keep the device close at hand.

Han Seunghee, Kim Ki Joon, and Kim Jang Hyun (2017). Cyberpsychology, Behavior, and Social Networking. 20(7): 419-427.
