Trends in Ed: The Pace of e-Book Statistics

LauraCostello

April 12, 2012

This week heralded the launch of two huge data reports on the use of e-books in libraries, one from the American Library Association and the other from major e-book distributor Overdrive. The Overdrive report will be launched at the London Book Fair, but all pre-reports seem to suggest a thriving e-book market with 337,000 users in 219 countries clocking in to “virtual branches.” In contrast, the ALA report indicates a decline in libraries offering digital book services, 67% from last year's 72%. The discrepancies may highlight the need for changes in the way library organizations gather statistics. The numbers for the ALA report come from survey data administered through Library Journal, their trade publication. Investigation by Nate Hoffelder of The Digital Reader uncovered hesitation on the part of the ALA to interpret or verify the report released in their name. This is not to paint Overdrive's tactics in a favorable light, though from their press releases it does seem that they made a good faith effort to respect library privacy policies this time. If indeed there was a drop in e-book adherence this year, it wouldn't be difficult to point the finger at Overdrive's pricing increases and general shadiness. In comparing the impressive numbers from Overdrive's "billions of eBook impressions" and the ALA's unverifiable but potentially alarming drop, the impression is that modern data gathering has left library research behind. Library organizations have continued to rely on survey data, when we have the ability to process actual data and the responsibility to be authorities on use within our institutions. It's clear from these reports that if library organizations don't step up to the research plate, someone else will.
