Finland’s High Achieving Students Have Low Tech Classrooms—What About The United States?

Meredith Powers

July 8, 2014


Schools in Finland use almost no technology in classrooms, yet Finland’s scores on the Programme for International Student Assessment (PISA) outranks the United States and many other OECD countries. Some may question why the United States spends billions of dollars on technology for the classroom when Finnish students have been outperforming Americans every year since 2001, but education in the two countries differ on more issues than the number of classroom gadgets: unlike the United States, the government of Finland provides free education, works cooperatively with unions, has no private schools and very little high-stakes testing, and is not involved in teacher evaluations. The US can try to match Finland’s achievements, but adjusting only one element of a complex system is unlikely to dramatically alter the outcomes.
