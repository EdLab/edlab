Understanding the users of tcrecords.org - post 1

Megha Agarwala

November 19, 2010

Starting today, I am going to be posting updates on the data analysis of TC Record (TCR). It just seems like there is alot of data on how people have been using TCR from the database and other analytics. However, in order to make sense of the data, we need to look inside the raw data to see what is going on and perhaps why is it going on. The focus of this series of analysis is going to be on the user. If you have any suggestions to aid in my analysis or any questions, please let me know. In this post, I looked at how TCR is been used by people at different institutions. I cleaned up some of the data (for years 2006 and some part of 2007), to analyze it for useful patterns. I broke down the session length of the users into a default value of 20 minutes (20 min is the default time out session in an asp.net session). I found that within a single session, around two different articles were clicked by the users. Most of the time, each article was clicked only once. On average, users spent around eight minutes on the site within a single session. The different articles which were clicked around this time belonged to the following categories: ‘Feature Article', ‘For the Record', ‘Book Reviews', ‘Social Frontiers' and ‘Frontiers of Democracy'. It is interesting to see that the most clicked articles were the ‘Social Frontiers' category articles (which in fact is not even a part of the journal). The first graph shows the breakdown. Most clicked articles belonged to Social frontiers category followed by feature articles . Further, the users clicked most articles which were published in the years of 1935-1936. (Most of these articles were Social frontier articles). This was followed by clicking the articles published in 2006 (Feature articles and book reviews). The second graph represents these numbers. It is interesting to see that for the number of sessions I have analyzed so far, users have not clicked on articles pre 1934.

. It is also interesting to see that wherever given an option to view the article as a PDF as opposed to HTML, people clicked to view an article as a PDF. The third graph shows the percentage distribution.
