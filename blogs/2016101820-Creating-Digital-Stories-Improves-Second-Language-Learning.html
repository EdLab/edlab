Creating Digital Stories Improves Second Language Learning

dinosossi2

October 18, 2016


This study explored digital storytelling as a way to improve English language skills for non-native speakers. It focused on an enterprising group of four collegians who created a digital story for their instructor to teach EFL students. The students were enrolled in an applied course on teaching English.

Instead of focusing on the digital book product, the researcher instead examined some of the underlying processes that went into making the story. For example, sociocognitive interactions deal with the relationship between the mind, oral interaction, and society. Methodological processes include management and organizational activities like deciding work plans, divvying up tasks, and distributing roles. Reflective processes help learners develop their reflective consciousness and emphasize the need for collaborative reflection. Finally, techno-semiopragmatic interactions are the sum of interactions taking place in and around machines, media, and communication technology.

By dividing story creation into distinct processes, the study allowed educators to understand learning opportunities occurring below the surface when students worked together. The research also highlights the roles communities play in deciding activities for lower level language groups. By better understanding group dynamics, educators can promote collaboration and language learning.

Studies like this one will become increasingly important as the population of EFL students continues to rise across schools. Kids quickly adapt to new tech, but often resist required reading. Why not use the digital tools they love and already use to express themselves online?

Kalyaniwala-Thapliyal, C. (2016). Collective digital storytelling: An activity-theoretical analysis of second language learning and teaching. Canadian Journal of Learning and Technology, 42(4), 1–27.
