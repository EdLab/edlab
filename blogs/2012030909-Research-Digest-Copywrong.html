Research Digest: Copywrong

LauraCostello

March 9, 2012

McDermott, A. J. (2012). Copyright: Regulation Out of Line with Our Digital Reality? Information Technology and Libraries, 31(1). Description: The article explores several facets of the current copyright landscape for libraries beginning with a discussion of the restrictions of the Digital Millennium Copyright act and the copyright term extension act of 1998. The author goes on to detail the issues arising from digitization, especially in the case of orphan works and the challenges to service that stem from an increasingly digital and increasingly restricted materials base. The very concept of ownership has shifted to a licensing model in the case of digital materials and this shift has significant implications for access. The second part of the paper is a guide for librarians meant to increase the visibility of these laws and create action around open source initiatives. The author advocates education and activism around the gamut of library-centric issues. Tellingly, the article was featured in the first open access issue of Information Technology and Libraries. EdLab Relevance: Painting the Authors Guild as selfish lurkers waiting for us to let our copyright guard down makes for a dynamic paper, but it ignores the notion that whatever we may offer in added value, libraries are content. What libraries can do with developing license models is ensure that we cultivate a diversity of providers. Copyright holders and libraries can agree on this point and really, promoting intellectual content should be one of our central missions. Sometimes this means setting information free but mostly it means bringing it to a community in a way that sustains and encourages the future of thought. Instead of open, we should aim for flexible. Creative Commons and the delights of adaptation in a culture that increasingly relies on peer-to-peer sharing as a marketing tool will ensure that there will be a lot for us to agree upon in future iterations of these laws...though doubtless we'll still find things to sue each other over.
