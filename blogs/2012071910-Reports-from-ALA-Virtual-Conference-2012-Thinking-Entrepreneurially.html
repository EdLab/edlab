Reports from ALA Virtual Conference 2012: Thinking Entrepreneurially

Rebekah Wallin

July 19, 2012

Yesterday afternoon at the ALA Virtual Conference: Mapping Transformations, Brian Matthews presented on "Thinking Entrepreneurially: What libraries can learn from startups and other innovative organizations." His talk was based on his recent white paper, which I blogged about last month after learning about the conference. Full circle. Everything Matthews presented to librarians about cultivating a "transitional mindset" is relevant for EdLab. He provided a great summary of the "lean startup" model:

1) Don't waste time on things that don't work (fail faster) 2) Good enough is good enough to start (minimum viable product) 3) Feed the feedback loop (iterations and rapid development cycles) 4) Pivot towards success (didn't just follow Plan A into failure)
Matthews synthesizes several different approaches to startup methodology into a coherent vision. First, he presented the alternating feedback loop of "build->measure->learn" and "learn->build->measure." This is how Groupon evolved from an unsuccessful platform for collective social activism to a multi-million dollar "deals" business. Next, he gave examples of three essential qualities of of a successful new product or service, which must be: 1) usable, 2) feasible, and 3) valuable. One point Matthews emphasized was the importance of letting the user community be involved with improving the tools. Finally, he discussed the concept of "functional fixedness" and the need to break away from preconceived ideas of what a library can do. Harvard Library Lab is an exciting example of the entrepreneurial library spirit, which offers infrastructure and financial support for innovation. Do any of those ideas resonate with your entrepreneurial experience? How do you see this transitional mindset playing out here at EdLab?
