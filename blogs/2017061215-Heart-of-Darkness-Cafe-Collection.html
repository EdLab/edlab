Heart of Darkness Cafe Collection

Kaitlin.Kehnemuyi

June 12, 2017

We are curating the next Cafe Collection to complement the work that "Literacy Unbound" is doing in the Learning Theater this summer. They are bringing together teachers and high school students to remix Joseph Conrad's Heart of Darkness into an original theater production.


Thinking about remixing Heart of Darkness got us thinking about other books that have been remixed, modernized or written as homages to other books. One of my favorites is Imperfect Bliss which is a retelling of Pride and Prejudice in modern times with the mom marrying off daughters via reality tv. This was the adaptation that finally got me to read Pride and Prejudice to see how the story lines compared. One of the great things is how these remixes can lead you to classics you might have missed or assumed you knew the story already.

 

Hadley recommended Rosencrantz and Guildenstern Are Dead, which blew my mind because it has become such a classic itself that I forget it builds off of Hamlet.

 

When I was researching Heart of Darkness, I came across Darkness by Yedda Morrison, where she only reveals the words in Heart of Darkness that refer to the natural world.

 

In searching for other remixes it seems as though the new versions breakdown into three categories: Modern Retellings, Spin-Offs, and Homages. Modern retellings take a storyline that we all know fairly well and update it to fit with current times; spin-offs take a character (sometimes minor) and build a new adjacent story to the original; whereas homages often slyly wink and nod to the original for the in crowd.

 

Modern Retellings:

There are a ton of retellings of Pride and Prejudice - Bridget Jones’ Diary by Helen Fielding, Eligible by Curtis Sittenfeld, Imperfect Bliss by Susan Fales-Hill. Reality TV, Paleo, and meddling mothers galore.

Cinder by Marissa Meyer is Cinderella reimagined as a cyborg. What more do you need to know?

The Innocents by Francesca Segal retells Edith Wharton’s The Age of Innocence, moving the story to present day London.

  

Spin-offs:

Wicked by Gregory Maguire tells the backstory of Elphaba, the Wicked Witch of the West from L. Frank Baum’s The Wizard of Oz. It beautifully humanizes Elphaba, one of the most well known bad guys in all of pop culture.

March by Geraldine Brooks, tells the story of the mostly absent father from Little Women by Louisa May Alcott, while he is off serving as a chaplain in the Union Army.

Insect Dreams by Marc Estrin picks up from Kafka’s Metamorphosis but assumes that Gregor Samsa lived and follows the path of a talking cockroach.

 

Homage to:

His Dark Materials by Philip Pullman references Paradise Lost by John Milton, starting with the title. When I read this series I thought it was just referencing parts of the Bible. I am off to read Paradise Lost now.

James Joyce’s Ulysses is based on Homer’s Odyssey, from the structure of the story to the epic tale of one man.

On Beauty by Zadie Smith starts off with an homage to Howards End by EM Forster in the first sentence, and models the story of two opposite families perpetually crashing into each other to match.
