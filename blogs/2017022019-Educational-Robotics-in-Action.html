Educational Robotics in Action

csd2126

February 20, 2017


Robots aren’t taking over the classroom just yet, but their use in educational settings is being explored in many ways. Educational robotics is a branch of knowledge where students program, build, and design robotic actions. New research in this field quantitatively examines the effects of educational robotics on children’s executive functions, or the set of adaptive, goal-oriented, top-down mental processes that are used to focus attention.

Research using educational robots has not previously focused on their use in young children, but the kindergarten participants of this study ranged in age from 5–6 years old. According to the theoretical model of executive functions, working memory and inhibition represent two of the main components emerging at an early age.

This study used a robot toy called Bee-Bot to assess young learners’ ability to plan and control for progressively more complex navigation tasks. Children can program Bee-Bot by giving it simple steps such as forward or backward, left or right, stop and go. The Bee-Bot is able to store up to 40 instructions in one programmed sequence.

While this study is identified as a pilot due to its small sample size, children who participated in the study increasingly demonstrated the ability to wait and check the Bee-Bot moves rather than relying on their own behavioral control. These indicate that it is possible to quickly improve executive functions in young children through the use of educational robotics.

Di Lieto, M. C., Inguaggiato, E., Castro, E., Cecchi, F., Cioni, G., Dell’Omo, M., . . . & Dario, P. (2017). Educational Robotics Intervention on Executive Functions in preschool children: A pilot study. Computers in Human Behavior, 71, 16–23.
