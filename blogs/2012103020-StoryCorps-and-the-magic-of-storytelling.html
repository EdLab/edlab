StoryCorps and the magic of storytelling

Laura Scheiber

October 30, 2012


While oral storytelling has been an important method of teaching for thousands of years, the evolution of technology has transformed how stories are told. Storycorps is on the leading edge, bringing oral storytelling to life in new ways.

The StoryCorps Project captures everyday people’s stories, which are archived at the American Folklife Center at the Library of Congress. Friends and family conduct interviews, creating an authenticity in how the stories are told compared to other forms of media, like news and entertainment. Each week, a StoryCorps piece airs on National Public Radio. Thanks to the use of new media, these universally themed stories that implicitly teach about history, culture, and social values, are accessible to a broader audience.

Bringing oral storytelling to life in new ways, StoryCorps has taken to animating stories, bringing oral tradition to living color. This vialogue features a humorous StoryCorps tale told by two siblings. The piece inspired vialogue participants to ask important questions about the art of storytelling:

The StoryCorps team has logged over 40,000 interviews with 80,000 participants and they’re hoping to touch all American families in this ambitious project. They are currently working on an app/website feature to make stories more easily accessible, because, as they say, "listening is an act of love."
