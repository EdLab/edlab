Higher Ed meets the inevitable inflection point?

Fred Rossoff

June 8, 2012

Jeremiads decrying our exorbitant and ballooning higher education system are a dime a dozen, but what is often missed in the frenzy is a simple recognition of Herbert Stein's Law “If something cannot go on forever, it must stop.” In the Hechinger report last week was an article by Jon Marcus indicating that we may have entered the final phase of this process. Marcus points to evidence indicating that, even with increased financial incentives, private and public institutions are having more and more trouble meeting their enrollment goals. For instance, he writes that “community-college enrollment dropped one percent this year after climbing almost 22 percent since 2007.” And an increasingly large percentage of private schools are reporting vacancies even after the end of the enrollment season This may be the result of an increasingly cost-conscious student cohort confronting an era of reduced public funding for higher education (see, for instance, the tuition hikes in the California, or, close to home, here at the CUNY system). Whatever the reason, a decline in public and private money entering the higher education system might be just the spur it needs to fully embrace the necessary (and cost-saving) technological reforms that we've been nurturing.
