The Future of Scholarly Publication in Education

Mar 20, 2011 10:11 AM

Gary Natriello will participate in a panel on The Future of Scholarly Publication in Education as part of the meeting of the Special Interest Group on the Communication of Research at the annual meeting of the American Educational Research Association. The session will take place at the Hotel Monteleone in the Bienville Room Session Description Join AERA Publications Director Todd Reitzel, and Teachers College Record Editor Gary Natriello to discuss recent developments in publication in the field of education. The Communication of Research SIG has invited these speakers to discuss scholarly publishing in general and in particular the new AERA Paper Repository. The Repository allows meeting registrants to access all meeting papers and should lead to higher readership and greater impact for many papers. Todd can discuss the vision from the AERA side and Gary, whose journal recently published a special issue on education informatics, will provide a perfect complement to that very specific issue as he discusses trends he sees in the publishing arena. This is an informal session, bring questions, comments and alternative views for a lively informative discussion. During the Repository's development phases, AERA explored repositories in a number of academic fields, questioned the utility and status of the materials that would be made available, and in general worked to assure that the needs of both researchers and readers would be met by the new product. While other disciplines, especially in the sciences, have led the way to increase access to research papers at more preliminary stages of development, this is new in education. Indeed new technologies are changing the landscape in many areas of the field and Gary Natriello has taken the lead in experimenting with trends. The October 2010 issue of TCR (Volume 112, Number 10) discusses access to information as technology has confounded efforts at accountability, access, security and individual rights. Researchers want both readers and ownership of their own ideas. Publishers want to be able to provide the branded access that has made education publications reliable for decades while remaining relevant as new technologies change how everyone generates, communicates and interacts with the research product.
