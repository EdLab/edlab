Debra Lee

EdLab Alumni

In Bruegel's painting "Landscape with the Fall ofIcarus", the Greek mythological character flies too close to the sun and falls to his death, unseen by the inhabitants of the bustling seaside community into whose harbor he plunges. The painting inspired the poet W.H. Auden to write about humanity's indifference to suffering:

            In Breughel's Icarus, for instance: how everything turns away

            Quite leisurely from the disaster; the ploughman may

            Have heard the splash, the forsaken cry,

            But for him it was not an important failure; the sun shone

            As it had to on the white legs disappearing into the green

            Water, and the expensive delicate ship that must have seen

            Something amazing, a boy falling out of the sky,

            Had somewhere to get to and sailed calmly on.

    I keep a tatty color photocopy of the painting in my kitchen, stuck to the wall with Blu Tack. It's there for amusement more than creative inspiration: those white legs disappearing into the crest of a wave have a comical effect on me––humor is such antidote to the human condition.

    But one afternoon leaning against my refrigerator, munching chocolate-coated raisins and reading about FaceBook's new mission statement (which came hot on the heels of Mark Zukerberg's announcement that the platform had achieved two billion monthly active users), I found myself considering another interpretation of the painting. It wasn't that nobody was looking when Icarus fell from the sky, but that nobody noticed the tragedy: the ploughman has his back turned, the fisherman, the shepherd (and his dog) are looking the other way, and the sailors are too busy steering their ship through the channel to hear the body splash into the water.

    This interpretation could be a maxim for the inward focus that characterizes society today. Except, I reminded myself, we shouldn't be fooled into believing nobody's looking. FaceBook is looking. In amending its mission from: 'making the world more open and connected' to: 'give [ing] people the power to build community and bring the world closer together', FaceBook signaled a shift in its gaze from the developed world to the developing world––where the next two billion users will be educated to receive and share content in service of FaceBook's core business: data acquisition through surveillance, not community building.

    Any NGO in the developing world will tell you that education is at the heart of efforts to eradicate poverty and build sustainable communities. Shared experiences in the form of storytelling are an important part of transforming lives. As a fiction writer, journalist and communications practitioner I have long understood the power of storytelling and the social and familial cooperation enabled through narrative. But FaceBook, with its fake news and post truths has no use for education that promotes survival and wellbeing. Its algorithms aim to homogenize; knowledge will be unnecessary to the next two billion content creators. And that puts storytelling at risk because sophisticated algorithms have no need for consciousness or heart as they serve a single fiction––money––and calculate ways to keep us connected.

    If AIs had been in charge when I was 16 and struggling to come to terms with my sexuality, I would not have been directed to a caring gay community where I was introduced to literature and characters who thought and felt as I did. I would not have been encouraged to open my mind to my human possibilities, or learned to overcome intolerance. I would not have found the courage to hope, dream, feel. Instead, I would have relied solely on my device to fulfill my innate need for emotional connection and belonging.

    In today's technology dependent, economy-driven world EdLab can be forgiven for appearing as a beacon shining at a new frontier: improving, evolving, reconfiguring and advancing education paradigms. Such a mandate is fraught with possibilities, excitement and danger as the thinkers collaborate, consolidate and conquer existing and emerging problems. I am honored to join EdLab's committed and dedicated team at a time in history when it has never been more important to be awake to our surroundings and on the lookout for an important failure, one that may hold the key to a new landscape.
